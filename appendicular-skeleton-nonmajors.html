The Appendicular Skeleton

The appendicular skeleton is the part of you that moves through the world: your arms, your legs, and the shoulder and hip girdles that connect them to the trunk. These 126 bones are built for action, reaching, gripping, walking, and running, while still carrying your weight. We will look at what each part is, and then at what it does for the living body. Part 1

Meet the Appendicular Skeleton

The limbs and the girdles that attach them, the moving parts of the skeleton.

Part 1 · Big picture

Limbs and girdles

Skeletal

The appendicular skeleton is everything except the central axis, 126 bones in four groups.

  • Pectoral (shoulder) girdle: attaches the arms to the trunk.
  • Upper limbs: arms, forearms, wrists, and hands.
  • Pelvic (hip) girdle: attaches the legs to the trunk.
  • Lower limbs: thighs, legs, ankles, and feet.
  • A girdle is the bridge that anchors a limb to the axial skeleton.

Part 2

Shoulder and Upper Limb

A lightweight, loosely attached design built for a huge range of motion.

Part 2 · Shoulder

The pectoral girdle

Skeletal
  • Clavicle (collarbone): a slender strut across the front that holds the shoulder out to the side.
  • Scapula (shoulder blade): a flat, triangular bone on the back that the arm hangs from.
  • The pectoral girdle attaches to the trunk in only one small spot, so the shoulder trades stability for an enormous range of motion.

Part 2 · Arm to hand

Bones of the upper limb

Skeletal
  • Humerus: the single bone of the upper arm.
  • Radius and ulna: the two forearm bones. The radius is on the thumb side and rolls to let you turn your palm.
  • Carpals (8): the small wrist bones.
  • Metacarpals (5): the bones of the palm.
  • Phalanges (14): the finger bones, three per finger and two in the thumb.

Part 3

Hip and Lower Limb

A heavy, tightly attached design built to carry your whole body weight.

Part 3 · Hip

The pelvic girdle

Skeletal
  • Each hip bone forms from three fused bones: the ilium (the broad upper flare), the ischium (the part you sit on), and the pubis (the front).
  • The two hip bones plus the sacrum form the pelvis, a strong bowl that supports the organs and transfers body weight to the legs.
  • Unlike the shoulder, the hip is firmly anchored, so it trades some motion for stability and strength.

Part 3 · Thigh to foot

Bones of the lower limb

Skeletal
  • Femur: the thigh bone, the longest and strongest bone in the body.
  • Patella: the kneecap, which protects the knee joint.
  • Tibia and fibula: the two lower-leg bones. The thick tibia (shinbone) bears the weight; the thin fibula adds support.
  • Tarsals (7): the ankle and heel bones.
  • Metatarsals (5) and phalanges (14): the bones of the foot and toes.

Part 4

Built for Different Jobs

Upper and lower limbs share a layout but are shaped by what they do.

Part 4 · Compare

Mobility vs. weight-bearing

Function
  • Same basic plan: one bone, then two bones, then many small bones, in both the arm and the leg.
  • Upper limb: lighter bones and a loose shoulder for reaching, gripping, and fine movement.
  • Lower limb: thicker, stronger bones and a tight hip for standing, walking, and carrying weight.
  • Form follows function: the same template is tuned for two very different jobs.

Figure 3. One body plan, tuned for two jobs. Diagram: Dr. Sharilyn Rennie.

Part 4 · Pelvis shape

A pelvis built for birth

Function
  • The female pelvis is generally wider and rounder, with a broader opening, an adaptation for childbirth.
  • The male pelvis is generally narrower and taller, with a heavier build.
  • These differences make the pelvis one of the most useful bones for estimating sex from a skeleton.

Part 5

Physiology: Why It Matters

The appendicular skeleton turns muscle power into movement, carries your weight, and keeps your blood supply going.

Part 5 · Movement

Bones are levers for muscles

Physiology
  • Muscles cannot move you on their own; they need something stiff to pull on.
  • Each long bone acts as a lever, and a joint acts as the fulcrum (pivot point).
  • When a muscle shortens, it swings the bone around the joint, bending an elbow or straightening a knee.
  • This is how chemical energy in muscle becomes real-world motion.

Figure 2. Muscle pulls, bone swings, the limb moves. Diagram: Dr. Sharilyn Rennie.

Part 5 · Support

Carrying and moving your weight

Physiology
  • With every step, the lower limbs and pelvis absorb forces several times your body weight.
  • The arches of the foot act like springs, spreading that load and giving a push at the end of each stride.
  • Bone is living tissue: it gets stronger when it is used, which is why weight-bearing exercise builds bone density.

Part 5 · Inside the bone

A factory and a storehouse

Physiology
  • Large limb bones such as the femur hold marrow that makes blood cells and stores fat as an energy reserve.
  • Like all bones, they act as a calcium bank, releasing minerals into the blood when the body needs them.
  • So even the bones built for movement are busy on the inside, supporting blood and mineral balance.

Part 5 · In the clinic

When things go wrong

Clinical
  • Clavicle fracture: one of the most commonly broken bones, often from a fall onto the shoulder or outstretched hand.
  • Hip fracture: a serious injury in older adults, where weakened bone breaks near the top of the femur.
  • Wrist (distal radius) fracture: very common from catching yourself in a fall.

Wrap-up

Key takeaways

Summary
  • The appendicular skeleton (126 bones) is the limbs plus the two girdles that attach them.
  • The pectoral girdle and upper limb are light and loosely attached, built for reaching and gripping.
  • The pelvic girdle and lower limb are strong and tightly attached, built for weight-bearing and walking.
  • Both limbs follow the same one-bone, two-bones, many-bones plan, tuned for different jobs.
  • Physiology: these bones act as levers for movement, carry and absorb weight, and make blood cells and store minerals.
