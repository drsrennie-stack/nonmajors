The Axial Skeleton

The axial skeleton is the central support column of the body: the skull, the spine, and the rib cage. These 80 bones hold you upright, protect the brain, spinal cord, heart, and lungs, and give your muscles something to pull against. We will look at what each part is, and then at what it does for the living body. Dr. Sharilyn Rennie

Part 1

Meet the Axial Skeleton

The body's bones split into two teams. The axial skeleton is the core; the appendicular skeleton is the limbs.

Part 1 · Big picture

Axial vs. appendicular

Skeletal

The 206 bones of the adult skeleton are divided into two groups.

  • Axial skeleton (80 bones): the bones along the body's center line, the skull, vertebral column (spine), and thoracic cage (ribs and sternum).
  • Appendicular skeleton (126 bones): the arms, legs, and the girdles that attach them, covered in a separate lecture.
  • Think of the axial skeleton as the trunk of a tree and the appendicular skeleton as the branches.

Figure 1. The axial bones (highlighted) form the central axis; the limbs make up the appendicular skeleton. Credit: OpenStax / CNX Anatomy & Physiology, CC BY. Loads online.

Part 1 · Why it matters

What the axial skeleton does

Function
  • Support: it holds the head and trunk upright and carries the body's weight down to the hips.
  • Protection: it surrounds the most delicate organs, the brain, spinal cord, heart, and lungs.
  • Anchor: many muscles for the head, neck, breathing, and posture attach here.
  • Blood and minerals: bones such as the sternum and vertebrae make blood cells and store calcium.

Part 2

The Skull

Twenty-two bones form a helmet for the brain and a frame for the face.

Part 2 · The skull

Two sets of skull bones

Skeletal
  • Cranial bones (8): the rounded braincase. Main ones are the frontal (forehead), two parietals (top and sides), two temporals (by the ears), and the occipital (back and base).
  • Facial bones (14): the framework of the face, including the maxillae (upper jaw), mandible (lower jaw, the only movable skull bone), zygomatics (cheekbones), and nasal bones.

Figure 2. Cranial bones (braincase) and facial bones (face). Credit: OpenStax / CNX Anatomy & Physiology, CC BY. Loads online.

Part 2 · Skull details

Joints, air spaces, and the baby skull

Function
  • Sutures: the squiggly seams where cranial bones lock together. In adults they barely move, which keeps the braincase rigid.
  • Sinuses: hollow, air-filled spaces in some skull bones. They lighten the skull and add resonance to the voice; when they get congested, you feel "sinus" pressure.
  • Fontanelles: the soft spots in a newborn's skull. These gaps let the skull flex during birth and leave room for the brain to grow, then close in the first couple of years.

Part 3

The Vertebral Column

Twenty-six bones stack into a strong, flexible, S-shaped support for the body.

Part 3 · The spine

Five regions, top to bottom

Skeletal
  • Cervical (7): the neck. The top two let you nod and shake your head.
  • Thoracic (12): the upper back. Each one connects to a pair of ribs.
  • Lumbar (5): the lower back. These are the largest and carry the most weight.
  • Sacrum: five fused bones that join the spine to the hips.
  • Coccyx: the tailbone, a few small fused bones at the very bottom.

Memory cue: breakfast at 7, lunch at 12, dinner at 5, the cervical, thoracic, and lumbar counts.

Figure 3. The spine's five regions and its natural curves. Credit: OpenStax / CNX Anatomy & Physiology, CC BY. Loads online.

Part 3 · How it works

Curves and cushions

Function
  • Natural curves: the spine is not straight. Its gentle S-shape acts like a spring, absorbing shock and keeping you balanced when you stand and walk.
  • Intervertebral discs: soft, rubbery pads of cartilage between the vertebrae. They cushion impacts and let the spine bend and twist.
  • Protection: a tunnel running through the stacked vertebrae surrounds and shields the spinal cord.

Part 4

The Thoracic Cage

The ribs and sternum form a protective, movable basket around the heart and lungs.

Part 4 · The rib cage

Sternum and ribs

Skeletal
  • Sternum: the flat breastbone down the front of the chest.
  • Ribs (12 pairs): curved bones that wrap from the spine around to the front.
  • True ribs (1–7) attach directly to the sternum by cartilage.
  • False ribs (8–10) attach indirectly, sharing cartilage above them.
  • Floating ribs (11–12) do not reach the front at all.

Part 5

Physiology: Why It Matters

The axial skeleton is not just scaffolding. It protects, it helps you breathe, and it keeps your blood and bones healthy.

Part 5 · Protection

A shield for the control center

Physiology
  • The skull surrounds the brain like a helmet, absorbing bumps that would otherwise injure delicate nerve tissue.
  • The vertebral column forms a bony tunnel around the spinal cord, the cable that carries messages between the brain and the rest of the body.
  • Together they protect the central nervous system, which cannot repair itself the way skin or muscle can.

Part 5 · Breathing

The rib cage helps you breathe

Physiology
  • The rib cage is not a fixed box; the ribs swing up and out, then back down, with every breath.
  • When the ribs lift, the chest gets bigger, pressure inside drops, and air rushes into the lungs.
  • When the ribs drop, the chest shrinks and air flows out.
  • So the axial skeleton is part of the respiratory system, not just protection.

Figure 4. Each breath is a sequence: rib movement changes the size of the chest. Diagram: Dr. Sharilyn Rennie.

Part 5 · Inside the bone

Making blood, storing minerals

Physiology
  • Blood cell factory: red marrow inside flat axial bones such as the sternum, ribs, and vertebrae produces red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets throughout life.
  • Mineral bank: bone stores most of the body's calcium and releases it into the blood when needed for muscles and nerves to work.
  • This is why a bone marrow sample is often taken from the sternum or hip.

Part 5 · In the clinic

When things go wrong

Clinical
  • Scoliosis: a sideways curve of the spine that can affect posture and, if severe, breathing.
  • Herniated disc: a cushioning disc bulges and presses on a nearby nerve, causing back or leg pain.
  • Osteoporosis: weakened, porous bone. In the spine it can lead to compression fractures and a stooped posture in older adults.

Wrap-up

Key takeaways

Summary
  • The axial skeleton (80 bones) is the body's central axis: skull, vertebral column, and thoracic cage.
  • The skull protects the brain and frames the face; the mandible is its only movable bone.
  • The spine has five regions and natural curves, with discs that cushion and a tunnel that shields the spinal cord.
  • The thoracic cage guards the heart and lungs and moves with every breath.
  • Physiology: the axial skeleton protects the central nervous system, powers breathing, makes blood cells, and stores calcium.
