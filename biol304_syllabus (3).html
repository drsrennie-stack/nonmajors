01

About This Course

A one-semester survey of human anatomy and physiology, built for students heading into allied health programs.

Course description

From the Course Outline of Record

This course examines body systems from an anatomical and physiological point of view. The basic anatomy and physiology of all the body systems are covered with an emphasis on developing vocabulary in each area. BIOL 304 is an introductory course for health career programs, such as Healthcare Interpreting, Gerontology Health Care, and Funeral Service that require one semester of combined anatomy and physiology.

Translated into plain language: we work through the body’s eight organ systems, one per week, paired with a clinical tool (a risk score, an assessment, an exam) that you will actually use in your field. The textbook is free. The pace is firm. The rhythm is built so your weekends can be yours.

Student Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course, you will be able to:

  1. Identify major organs, cavities, bones and bone markings, muscles, and blood vessels on models or drawings.
  2. Explain how different organ systems contribute to the maintenance of the living cells of our bodies.
  3. Explain how different organ systems work together to maintain homeostasis.
  4. Correlate course knowledge of organ systems to disease, aging, and healing processes.
  5. Integrate the body’s structural design with its functioning to explain how life is maintained.
  6. Differentiate between credible sources of scientific information and anecdotes or stories.

Who this course serves

BIOL 304 meets the science requirement for several ARC programs, including Emergency and Episodic Care, Funeral Service Education, Gerontology: Geriatric Health Care, and Healthcare Interpreting. It is also accepted for Cal-GETC Area 5B (Biological Science) and 5C (Laboratory), and transfers to both CSU and UC systems. Prerequisites: none.

Recommended

The trainers and anatomy labs are easier (and more memorable) with a few colors. Three to five colors is plenty.

03

How This Class Works

One body system per week, for eight weeks. Each week pulls from the same four tracks: Lecture, Discussion, Trainer, Lab.

The four weekly tracks

Track 01

Lecture

About 90 minutes of concept videos per week, in 4 to 6 short chunks. A weekly quiz checks the concepts (two attempts, best score kept, due Sunday).

Track 02

Discussion

Weekly async forum. Prompt opens Wednesday. First post due Friday so classmates have the weekend to read. Replies due Sunday.

Track 03

Trainer

One trainer per week, printed and hand-completed as you watch the concept videos. Photograph and upload by Sunday.

Track 04

Lab

Two parts, one submission. Anatomy labeling on OpenStax figures plus a physiology activity using a clinical risk calculator. Due Sunday.

The full day-by-day rhythm lives in the Rhythm Card, linked on the course home page. Your weekly chapter assignments live in the Reading Map, also linked there.

05

Late Work

Two firm deadlines each week. One short grace window. No exceptions after that.

The policy

Everything is due Sunday end of day, with one exception: your first discussion post is due Friday so classmates have time to reply. Submissions within 24 hours of either deadline earn 50% of the points they would have earned on time. After 24 hours, the grade is zero. No exceptions.

The grace window exists because life happens: you get sick, a shift runs long, the internet dies. It exists once per week, and it caps at 50%. After that, the gradebook closes so I can grade on a predictable schedule without the weekly cycle bleeding into the next.

If something is genuinely catastrophic (hospitalization, a death in the family, something documented and serious), email me as early as possible so we can figure out next steps together.

08

Regular & Substantive Interaction

How this asynchronous course stays a real course, with a real instructor, who shows up for your learning every week.

Online classes are required by federal and California regulation to include regular and substantive interaction (RSI) between the instructor and students, and among students themselves. RSI is what distinguishes a real course from a correspondence packet. Here is how I plan to meet that standard every week in BIOL 304.

Instructor to student, every week

Monday

Weekly announcement

An orientation announcement each Monday setting up the week’s system, pointing to key videos and readings, and flagging anything unusual.

Wednesday

Discussion engagement

When the prompt opens, I enter the forum and participate with substantive follow-up questions and clarifications as posts come in.

Friday

Synthesis Loom

A short recorded video each Friday pulling the week’s concepts together, addressing common questions, and flagging what matters for the next week.

Ongoing

Virtual Office Hours

Active presence in the Virtual Office Hours discussion throughout the week. Substantive responses within 48 hours of the next business day.

Feedback on your work

Trainers, labs, discussion posts, and portfolio entries receive feedback from me within one week of the Sunday deadline. Quizzes are auto-graded with immediate feedback on correct and incorrect answers. Feedback is not just a score. You will hear what worked, what to revisit, and (where relevant) how the concept shows up in your future program.

Student to student

The weekly discussion is where you connect with classmates. Your first post is due Friday so classmates have the weekend to read and engage. Replies by Sunday are required and graded for substance, not just quantity. You will learn from how other people think about the same material you are working through.

If you are quiet, I notice

If I see a student disappear from discussion, from assignment submissions, or from Canvas activity for more than a few days, I will reach out. Not to pressure, but to check in. Online summer sessions move fast and it is easy to fall behind without realizing it. Early contact is almost always recoverable. Silent withdrawal usually is not.

Academic Integrity & AI

Your work represents your own thinking. AI tools are allowed as tutors, not as writers.

Academic integrity

Every submission in this course must be your own work. This includes trainers, quizzes, discussion posts, lab write-ups, and portfolio entries. Submitting someone else’s work as your own, whether from a classmate, a website, or an AI tool, is academic dishonesty. Consequences include a zero on the assignment, a failing grade in the course, and referral to ARC for further action.

AI policy

AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and others) are part of how people learn now. Here’s the line in this class:

You can use AI to

  • Ask for explanations of concepts you don’t understand
  • Check your reasoning on a calculation or interpretation
  • Get feedback on the clarity of your own writing
  • Practice quiz-style questions for study

You cannot use AI to

  • Write your discussion posts or portfolio entries
  • Generate your lab interpretations
  • Answer quiz questions during an attempt
  • Replace your own thinking in any graded work

If you use AI to help you understand something, that’s fine. Just write the submission in your own words. When in doubt, note the AI assistance in your submission. Honesty about your process is always better than pretending.

Your trainers (hand-written on paper) and your lab calculations (your own work with the tools) are the most durable evidence of your learning. The course is designed so the real engagement shows up there.
