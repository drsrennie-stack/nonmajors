01

About this course

Course overview

This course is an introductory survey of human anatomy and physiology. We move through the body system by system across 8 weeks, building up the structural vocabulary and functional reasoning you need to succeed in allied health programs. Each week pairs anatomical study (drawing, labeling, identification) with applied physiology (lab interpretation, calculations, clinical scenarios) so you build both halves of the discipline at once.

BIO 304 is ideal for students preparing for healthcare interpreting, funeral services, gerontology, EMT or paramedic certification, mortuary science, or general allied health pathways. This course does not satisfy the prerequisite for RN nursing programs. Students preparing for nursing should take the BIOL 430 / 431 sequence at ARC.

That said, BIO 304 is excellent preparation for any future A&P coursework. The clinical reasoning skills, terminology, and study patterns you build here transfer directly to BIOL 430, BIOL 431, and any nursing-track A&P sequence at any institution.

A note on previous course numbering

This course was previously listed as BIO 102. The catalog identifier changed to BIOL 304. The course content and rigor are unchanged. If you have transcripts referencing BIO 102, the courses are equivalent.

02

What you will be able to do

Learning outcomes

By the end of this course, you will be able to:

  • Identify major anatomical structures on diagrams, models, and reference images across all 11 body systems.
  • Apply correct anatomical terminology to describe the position and relationship of structures.
  • Explain how each organ system maintains homeostasis through structural and functional coordination.
  • Interpret common clinical lab values and bedside assessments (ABG, ECG, urinalysis, neuro exam, LFTs, lipid panel, others) using evidence-based decision logic.
  • Connect cellular and tissue-level structure to organ-level and system-level function.
  • Apply A&P knowledge to clinical scenarios including burn resuscitation, fall risk, diabetes recognition, stroke screening, respiratory failure, and end-of-life decision-making.
  • Build and use clinical reference documents that synthesize structural anatomy with physiological function.
  • Evaluate sources of biomedical information critically and apply that evaluation to your future practice.

This is a 4-credit course compressed into 8 weeks. The same total amount of student work that would typically take 15-16 weeks is condensed into half that time. Federal Carnegie Unit standards expect approximately 22.5 hours per week of total student engagement for a 4-credit, 8-week intensive course.

Communicate with me early through the Virtual Office Hours forum or email. Catching trouble in Week 1 or 2 is very different from catching it in Week 6. I can point you to study strategies, time management tools, and ARC support resources, but only if I know you need help.

Course components

Each week of BIO 304 includes the following components, organized through the course Dashboard. These six tools work together as a learning system: the videos and trainer build foundational knowledge, the workbooks apply it, the portfolio synthesizes it, and the discussion deepens it.

Video lectures

Concept lectures recorded by Dr. Rennie plus curated YouTube content for each body system. Watch first, then run the cognitive trainer.

Component 02

Cognitive trainer

Interactive HTML tool for retrieval practice. Run 3 to 4 sessions per week, each 30 to 60 minutes. Builds vocabulary and recognition for the workbook.

Component 03

Anatomy workbook

Draw with reference, label every required structure using the numbered structure list, complete synthesis questions. Submitted Sunday as PDF.

Component 04

Physiology workbook

Clinical dry lab. Lab interpretation, calculations, patient cases, evidence-based reasoning. Submitted Sunday as PDF.

Component 05

Clinical Reference Portfolio

One single-page reference per week, building toward an 8-page personal clinical reference document by end of course. See the Portfolio Hub for details.

Component 06

Discussion forum

Initial post by Wednesday 11:59 PM. Two substantive replies by Sunday 11:59 PM. Graded discussion forum, separate from the Virtual Office Hours forum.

Component 07

Quizzes

Honorlock-proctored quiz on each week's content. 30 minutes, 1 attempt, available Friday through Sunday.

Component 08

Lab Exams (2)

Honorlock-proctored exams in Week 4 and Week 8. Practical structure identification on diagrams, models, histology.

08

How you turn work in

Submission policy

All weekly assignments (workbooks, portfolio pages) are submitted to Canvas as a single PDF. The full Submission Directions document explains how to convert work to PDF on every device and walks through three options for completing the work.

Three options for completing your work

  • Option 1: Print the workbook, handwrite, scan to PDF. Recommended for most students. Use any phone scanning app (Adobe Scan, Microsoft Lens, Apple Notes, Google Drive) or a desktop scanner.
  • Option 2: Build it digitally with shapes and text labels. Use Google Slides, Google Docs, PowerPoint, or Word. Required: revision history must be visible (showing you built the work over time, not in a single session). Submit the PDF AND share a link to the editable file with revision history accessible.
  • Option 3: Handwrite on blank paper, scan to PDF. No printer required. Strict format rules apply: every page must include the workbook title, current section number, and page number (e.g., "Page 3 of 14"). All pages must be in numerical order.

Drawing policy

For each anatomy figure, find a clear reference figure in OpenStax or any anatomy atlas, then reproduce it in the drawing space. The numbered structure list beside each drawing tells you which structures to include. Place each number from the structure list on the matching location of your drawing.

You may build drawings by hand OR digitally using shapes, lines, and text labels. Either way, you must build the drawing yourself, structure by structure. Copying a finished image (from Google Images, an AI tool, a peer, or any other source) earns zero on the assignment and is reported as academic dishonesty. Submitting work to Canvas certifies that the work is your own.

Universal submission rules

All submissions: PDF format, pages in order, page numbers on every page, workbook title and section number on every page, name and section and date on the first page, complete (no skipped sections), submitted by Sunday 11:59 PM.

How workbooks are graded · 50 points each

Each workbook (anatomy and physiology alike) is evaluated on three dimensions:

Workbook rubric

Completion. Each incomplete or trivially-answered question deducts 5 points. Substantive effort is required (a single word or "idk" counts as incomplete). Skip 8 or more items and completion goes to zero.40 pts
Authenticity. Your own work, built by you. If digital, revision history must show genuine building over time. If handwritten, handwriting must be consistent throughout. No copying from peers, the internet, or AI tools. Violations earn zero for the entire workbook (not just this 5 points) and are reported as academic dishonesty.5 pts
Accuracy spot-check. One or two undisclosed questions per workbook are graded for correctness. Build your understanding for every section, not just the ones you think will be checked.5 pts
Total50 pts

What this means in practice

A genuinely complete workbook with all your own work and correct spot-check answers earns 50/50 (100%). A complete workbook with one skipped item earns 45/50 (90%). A complete workbook with five skipped items earns 25/50 (50%). Skipping is the fastest way to tank a workbook grade. Plan to engage substantively with every section, even if you find a topic difficult. A genuine attempt that arrives at the wrong answer earns more credit than a blank space.

Doing your own work

Academic integrity

Academic integrity is required in all coursework at American River College and in this class. All submitted work must be your own. Cheating, plagiarism, and unauthorized use of AI or other tools are not allowed.

Examples of cheating

  • Copying answers from another student or website
  • Using AI tools (e.g., ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) to complete graded work without permission
  • Sharing quiz or exam questions or answers with others, including in study groups
  • Submitting someone else's work as your own (including AI-generated work)
  • Using an unauthorized device, notes, or reference during a proctored exam

Examples of plagiarism

  • Using another person's words or ideas without citation
  • Copying lab reports, assignments, or discussion posts from any source (including AI)
  • Paraphrasing closely without crediting the original source
  • Submitting the same work for multiple courses without permission

See ARC's official policy at arc.losrios.edu/student-resources/student-conduct.

Consequences

Any violation of academic integrity will result in at minimum a zero on the assignment in question. Severe or repeated violations may result in an F for the course and referral to the V.P. of Student Services for disciplinary action. Plagiarism or AI detection tool ratings above 25% on a graded assignment will earn an automatic zero unless AI use was specifically permitted on that assignment.

Using AI responsibly

AI use policy

Generative AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and similar) are powerful study aids and increasingly part of professional life in healthcare. This course does not ban AI use, but it requires transparency. You will sign an AI Use Honor Contract during the first week of class.

I may use AI detection tools to screen student work in this class. If you have questions or concerns about these tools, raise them in the Virtual Office Hours forum or by email.

How AI may be used in this course

You can use AI to

  • Clarify difficult concepts or summarize complex information for your own learning
  • Create a personal tutor for retrieval practice
  • Generate practice questions or outlines for review
  • Generate review podcasts to help you retain difficult information
  • Organize your thinking before drafting your own writing
  • Verify information and evaluate bias in published sources
  • Reflect on and improve your own study processes
  • Check accuracy of clinical decision logic on portfolio pages (after you build the page yourself)

You cannot use AI to

  • Generate any content for graded assignments unless the assignment is specifically designated AI-allowed
  • Generate workbook drawings, labels, or synthesis answers
  • Generate portfolio page content, scenarios, or reflections
  • Complete quizzes or exams (Honorlock-proctored, AI use is automatically a violation)
  • Write discussion posts in your name
  • Bypass the requirement to build digital drawings yourself, structure by structure

AI Honor Contract

All students must read, sign, and upload the AI Use Honor Contract for this course in Week 1. The contract outlines responsible AI use in your A&P coursework and is required for course participation. Submission deadline: by end of day Tuesday, June 9, 2026.

About my use of AI

I may use AI tools to support the design and delivery of instructional materials. AI can help with organizing content, formatting rubrics, drafting clinical case prompts, and enhancing clarity in explanations. All final content is reviewed and edited by me to meet rigorous academic and professional standards. I may also use AI tools to assist in organizing grading workflow or generating feedback summaries when identifying class-wide trends. AI-assisted grading uses anonymous, de-identified submissions only. No personally identifiable information is shared with AI systems. All grading decisions reflect my professional judgment and are grounded in your submitted work and the course rubric.

AI is a vital and rapidly evolving professional tool in healthcare, education, and beyond. In this course, we do not shy away from it. We learn to use it responsibly, transparently, and in ways that enhance rather than replace your own thinking.

Deadlines and exceptions

Late work & make-ups

Success in an 8-week intensive course requires consistent on-time submission. Because of how compressed the schedule is, this course does not accept late work and does not offer make-up exams.

Submission deadlines

  • Weekly workbooks, portfolio pages, and quizzes are due Sunday 11:59 PM of the week they are assigned.
  • Discussion initial posts are due Wednesday 11:59 PM of the week they are assigned.
  • Discussion replies are due Sunday 11:59 PM of the week they are assigned.
  • Lab exams are due during their announced testing window.
  • The final compiled Clinical Reference Portfolio is due Friday of Week 8 (July 31, 2026) by 11:59 PM.

Late submissions

Work submitted after the deadline is not accepted and receives a grade of zero. There are no exceptions for technical issues, illness, travel, or personal circumstances. Plan ahead and submit early if you anticipate any obstacles.

Why this policy exists

This is not punitive. The 8-week format compresses 16 weeks of work into half the time. Each week's content builds on the previous week. Allowing late submissions in one week throws off the next week, and the cumulative effect across 8 weeks would be unmanageable for everyone, including the students keeping up. Holding everyone to the same on-time standard is the fairest possible policy in an intensive course.

If you anticipate trouble

If you know in advance that you will not be able to meet a deadline (planned medical procedure, family emergency, etc.), contact me before the deadline. I cannot extend deadlines, but I may be able to suggest study strategies, point you to ARC support resources, or help you decide whether continuing in this intensive format is the right choice for your situation.
