This week we covered the principle that holds the rest of the course together: homeostasis. The body is constantly maintaining set points (temperature, glucose, blood pressure, fluid balance, oxygen) and constantly responding when something pushes it off.

For someone going into healthcare, recognizing when homeostasis is breaking down is often the entire job. For everyone else, it shows up everywhere from athletic performance to working in heat to caring for a sick family member. This week we connect the concept to real life.

Step 01

Pick one homeostatic system

Choose one from the list, or pick your own. Whatever interests you.

  • Body temperature regulation
  • Blood glucose regulation
  • Blood pressure regulation
  • Fluid & electrolyte balance
  • Oxygen & carbon dioxide balance
  • Or another homeostatic system you find interesting
Step 02

Write your post (150 to 300 words)

01

Walk through the feedback loop

What is the receptor, the control center, and the effector for this system? Keep it general. No need for deep physiology yet.

02

Connect it to a profession

When might someone in your future field encounter a patient or client where this homeostatic system is breaking down? Healthcare paths (EMT, funeral service, healthcare interpreting, gerontology, allied health) are obvious fits. If you are not heading into healthcare, pick a profession you are familiar with, or one you have always wondered about. Athletic coaching, firefighting, social work, teaching, parenting, military service, restaurant work, animal care. Be specific.

03

Name one question this raised for you

What do you want to know more about? We may not have the answer yet. Naming the question is part of how learning works.

Step 03

Reply to two classmates by Sunday

What makes a reply substantive

Read at least three classmates’ posts before you reply. Then leave two replies that engage with what they actually wrote.

Counts as substantive

  • Connecting their example to one you know about
  • Asking a follow-up question that pushes their thinking
  • Offering a different angle on the same scenario
  • Sharing what you would want to know more about based on what they wrote

Does not count

  • Great post!
  • I agree.
  • Same.
  • Anything you could write without reading their post
Important

No AI for this discussion

This is a discussion. The point is your thinking, in your words, in conversation with classmates’ thinking. AI cannot do that work for you, and it should not.

Do not use ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot, or any other AI tool to draft your post or your replies. Canvas and Turnitin both flag AI-generated content automatically. AI-generated responses (or responses suspected of being AI-generated based on tool flags) will receive a zero on the initial post with no opportunity to revise.

The only exception: an assignment that explicitly tells you to interact with an AI tool. This is not one of those assignments.

If you are stuck on what to write, the LRC Tutoring Center can help you brainstorm and draft (free, online or in person at arc.losrios.edu/lrc-tutoring). You can also revisit your Week 1 trainer: the homeostasis section already walks through a feedback loop and is your starting point.

A note from me

Homeostasis is the easy concept to underestimate. It sounds abstract until the first time you see someone whose temperature regulation has failed, or whose blood glucose has crashed, or whose blood pressure will not stabilize. Then you realize the whole rest of the course is teaching you to recognize and respond to homeostatic breakdown.

This discussion is a chance to start practicing that recognition while the stakes are zero. I will read every post. I respond selectively in the forum so my voice does not crowd out yours, but I am paying attention to all of them.

With warmth, Dr. Rennie
