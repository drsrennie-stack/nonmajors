How to use this trainer

This trainer is your live note-taking tool. It is not a worksheet to power through. The point is to slow down and put concepts in your own handwriting.

  1. Print it on paper at the start of the week, OR download it as a PDF and write on it with a tablet stylus. Either is fine.
  2. Skim the OpenStax chapters first (5 minutes, just headings and figures).
  3. Watch the concept videos for context.
  4. Read each topic only when its trainer section asks for it. Then fill in that section.
  5. Photograph each page, combine into ONE PDF in page order, and upload that single PDF to the Week 1 Trainer assignment in Canvas by Sunday. See the submission instructions at the end of this trainer for how.

Handwriting only. No typed text, no handwriting fonts, no screenshots of typed responses. The act of writing it by hand is the learning. I check for this. Typed or font-generated submissions earn a zero.

Section 03

Body Mapping

Use OpenStax Chapter 1 figures as your reference. You do not need to read more before this section.

Drawing is part of how you learn this. The pictures do not need to be beautiful. Stick figures, blocks, ovals, squares, all fine. The labels are what get you the credit. Use the checklist beside each drawing to be sure you included everything.

01 Draw the three body planes.

Drawing space

Sketch a simple figure (a stick person or a block body) and draw the three planes slicing through it. Label each plane and what it divides.

Abstract is fine: three squares with a plane cutting each one differently is enough.

Self-check

Make sure you drew & labeled:

  • Sagittal plane
  • Frontal (coronal) plane
  • Transverse plane
  • An arrow showing what each plane divides

02 Draw the major body cavities.

Drawing space

Sketch the body in side view. Outline the major cavities and label each one. Add at least one organ inside each cavity to show you know what is in there.

Abstract is fine: a vertical body outline with bubbles for each cavity, labels, and an organ name in each bubble.

Self-check

Make sure you drew & labeled:

  • Cranial cavity
  • Vertebral (spinal) cavity
  • Thoracic cavity
  • Abdominal cavity
  • Pelvic cavity
  • One organ in each

03 Draw the four abdominal quadrants.

Drawing space

Draw the abdomen as a square or oval. Divide it into four quadrants. Label each quadrant and name one organ found in each.

A square with a cross dividing it into four labeled boxes is exactly the right level of detail.

Self-check

Make sure you drew & labeled:

  • Right Upper Quadrant (RUQ)
  • Left Upper Quadrant (LUQ)
  • Right Lower Quadrant (RLQ)
  • Left Lower Quadrant (LLQ)
  • One organ per quadrant

04 Draw and label the major body regions.

Drawing space

Draw a stick figure with simple boxes for the body parts (head, torso, limbs). Label each region listed in the checklist. These are the named regions clinicians use to communicate where on the body something is.

Stick figure with boxes and arrows pointing to each labeled region is the assignment. Anatomical art skill not required.

Self-check

Make sure you labeled:

  • Cephalic (head)
  • Cervical (neck)
  • Thoracic (chest)
  • Abdominal
  • Pelvic
  • Brachial (upper arm)
  • Antebrachial (forearm)
  • Femoral (thigh)
  • Crural (leg)

Section 06

Homeostasis

Read OpenStax Chapter 1, Section 1.5 before completing this section.

01 Define homeostasis in your own words.

02 Name the three components of a homeostatic feedback loop.

03 Draw the general homeostatic feedback loop.

Drawing space

Draw the loop showing how the body senses change and pulls back to a set point. Use boxes and arrows. Label the receptor, the control center, the effector, and the direction of the response.

Boxes connected by arrows is exactly what this should look like. No artistic skill required.

Self-check

Make sure you drew & labeled:

  • Stimulus (the change)
  • Receptor (sensor)
  • Control center (integrator)
  • Effector (responder)
  • Set point or response arrow
  • Arrows showing the direction of the loop

04 Pick one example of homeostasis and walk through the three components for that example.

Body temperature, blood glucose, and blood pressure are good examples. What is the receptor? The control center? The effector?

05 What is the difference between negative feedback and positive feedback? Give one example of each.

06 Why does homeostasis matter for someone in healthcare or a related field?

One or two sentences. This is your hook. Every system in this course is a homeostasis story.

If you do not know how to combine photos into a PDF

This is a skill worth learning. It will save you time in every future class and most workplaces. The simplest way:

  1. Open Microsoft PowerPoint (free for students through Los Rios).
  2. Insert your photos onto blank slides, one photo per slide, in page order.
  3. Use File → Save As → PDF. PowerPoint converts the whole deck into a single PDF.

Other options that work: Adobe Scan (free phone app, photographs and combines in one step), Google Drive (upload photos, then download as PDF), Apple Notes (scan documents on iPhone, share as PDF), or any free image-to-PDF tool online.

Points deduction for separate photos. If you upload individual photos instead of one combined PDF, I will deduct points. This is not a courtesy request. I have to download and open each photo separately to grade you, and with a class full of students that adds hours every week. One PDF in page order keeps the grading manageable and your feedback faster.
