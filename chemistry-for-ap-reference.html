01

Atoms, Ions, and Electrolytes

Everything in the body is built from atoms, and many of the body's signals come from charged atoms called ions.

An atom is the smallest unit of matter. It has a nucleus of protons (positive charge) and neutrons (no charge), surrounded by electrons (negative charge). An element is one type of atom. Just six elements, oxygen, carbon, hydrogen, nitrogen, calcium, and phosphorus, make up about 99% of body weight.

An ion is an atom that has gained or lost electrons, so it now carries a charge. A cation is positive (it lost electrons); an anion is negative (it gained electrons). An electrolyte is an ion that conducts electricity when dissolved in water. These are the workhorses of nerve, muscle, fluid, and pH balance.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

An atom is like a tiny LEGO brick that everything is made of. An ion is just an atom wearing a "+" or "−" sticker because it traded away or grabbed an extra electron. Those little charges are what let your nerves and muscles send electrical signals.

The electrolytes you will see again and again
IonSymbolChargeMain job in A&P
SodiumNa⁺Cation (+)Nerve and muscle signals; main solute outside cells; fluid balance
PotassiumK⁺Cation (+)Resting membrane potential; main solute inside cells
CalciumCa²⁺Cation (+)Muscle contraction; nerve signaling; clotting; bone
HydrogenH⁺Cation (+)Sets pH; more H⁺ means more acidic
ChlorideCl⁻Anion (−)Follows sodium; fluid balance; stomach acid
BicarbonateHCO₃⁻Anion (−)The body's main pH buffer

Where you'll meet this in A&P

Nerve impulses and muscle contraction (Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺), acid-base balance (H⁺, HCO₃⁻), and every "electrolyte panel" lab value. When a patient is "low potassium" or "high calcium," this is the chemistry behind it.

02

Chemical Bonds

Atoms join by sharing or transferring electrons. The type of bond decides how a molecule behaves in water.

Three bonds, from strongest to weakest
BondWhat happensStrengthExample
IonicOne atom gives electrons to another; the resulting + and − ions attractStrong (dry), but breaks apart in waterTable salt, NaCl, splits into Na⁺ and Cl⁻
CovalentAtoms share electrons. Equal share = nonpolar; unequal = polarStrongestWater (polar); fats (nonpolar)
HydrogenA weak attraction between polar molecules (a slightly + part pulls on a slightly − part)Weak, but powerful in large numbersHolds water together; shapes proteins and DNA

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Ionic = one atom gives, the other takes, then they stick together because opposite charges attract. Covalent = they share, like two kids holding the same toy. Hydrogen bonds = light static-cling between neighboring molecules, weak alone but strong when there are millions.

Where you'll meet this in A&P

Why water dissolves salts but not fats, why the membrane's phospholipid bilayer forms, and why protein shape (held by hydrogen bonds) makes enzymes, receptors, and antibodies work. Break the bonds with heat or wrong pH and the protein stops working (denatures).

03

Water, the Body's Solvent

You are roughly 50 to 60% water. Almost every reaction in the body happens dissolved in it.

Water is a polar molecule: one end is slightly positive, the other slightly negative. That single fact explains most of what water does for the body.

Water's properties and why they matter
PropertyWhat it meansWhy the body cares
Universal solventDissolves charged and polar substances (these are "water-loving," hydrophilic)Blood, cytoplasm, and urine are water carrying dissolved cargo
Does not dissolve fatsNonpolar substances are "water-fearing" (hydrophobic)This is why cell membranes form and stay intact
High heat capacityAbsorbs a lot of heat with little temperature changeStabilizes body temperature
CohesionWater molecules stick togetherSurface tension; helps blood and fluids move
ReactantUsed to build (dehydration synthesis) and break (hydrolysis) moleculesDigestion and metabolism run on these reactions

Where you'll meet this in A&P

Blood plasma and body fluids, thermoregulation (sweat), why the membrane bilayer exists, and why a drug being water-soluble vs. fat-soluble changes how it crosses membranes and travels in blood.

04

Solutions and Concentration

A solution is a solute dissolved in a solvent. Concentration is how much solute is packed into a given volume, and the body regulates it tightly.

The vocabulary

Solvent
The dissolver (in the body, almost always water).
Solute
What gets dissolved (salt, sugar, ions).
Solution
Solute fully and evenly dissolved in solvent.
Concentration
Amount of solute per volume of solution.
Osmolarity
Total solute particles per liter (drives osmosis).

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Think of mixing a drink. The water is the solvent, the powder is the solute, and the mixed drink is the solution. Concentration is just how strong you made it: a little powder is weak, a lot is strong.

How concentration is measured in A&P (and typical reference values)
UnitMeansExample value
Percent (%)Grams of solute per 100 mL0.9% normal saline (isotonic IV fluid)
mg/dLMilligrams per deciliterBlood glucose ~70 to 99 mg/dL (fasting)
mEq/LMilliequivalents per liter (for ions)Sodium ~135 to 145; potassium ~3.5 to 5.0
Molarity (mol/L)Moles of solute per literUsed for lab and reaction chemistry
Osmolarity (Osm/L)Solute particles per literBlood plasma ~285 to 295 mOsm/L

Where you'll meet this in A&P

Osmosis and tonicity (isotonic, hypotonic, hypertonic depend on concentration), IV fluids, and every lab value you will read. When osmolarity differs across a membrane, water moves, that is the entire basis of fluid balance.

05

Gradients: Chemical and Electrical

A gradient is a difference across a space. Things tend to move "down" a gradient, and cells use gradients to move substances and to send signals.

Three gradients that drive physiology
GradientThe differenceWhat it drives
Chemical (concentration)More of a substance on one side than the otherDiffusion and osmosis (high to low)
ElectricalMore charge on one side than the otherIons move toward the opposite charge
ElectrochemicalBoth combinedThe true driving force on ions across a membrane

Cells keep a small voltage across the membrane called the membrane potential, with the inside slightly negative compared to the outside (about −70 mV at rest in a neuron). It is set up by uneven ion distribution: sodium (Na⁺) is high outside and potassium (K⁺) is high inside, maintained by the Na⁺/K⁺ pump (active transport, costs ATP). When channels open, ions rush down their electrochemical gradient and the voltage changes, that change is the signal.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

A chemical gradient is like a crowd squished in one corner, they spread out on their own. An electrical gradient is like magnets, opposite charges pull together. Your cells keep a tiny charged "battery" across their skin (membrane). When they flip a switch (open a channel), the battery discharges a little, and that flicker is how nerves and muscles send messages.

Where you'll meet this in A&P

Resting membrane potential, action potentials in neurons and muscle, the heart's electrical conduction (the ECG), and secondary active transport, where the sodium gradient pulls glucose into cells. Almost all "excitable tissue" runs on this.

06

Acids, Bases, pH, and Buffers

pH measures how acidic or basic a fluid is. The body holds blood pH in a tiny window, and buffers are how it does it.

An acid releases hydrogen ions (H⁺), which lowers pH. A base accepts H⁺, which raises pH. The pH scale runs 0 to 14, where 7 is neutral. It is logarithmic: each whole number is a tenfold change, so pH 5 is ten times more acidic than pH 6.

Blood pH is held between 7.35 and 7.45. Below 7.35 is acidosis; above 7.45 is alkalosis. A buffer resists pH change by soaking up extra H⁺ or releasing it as needed. The body's main one is the bicarbonate buffer system (HCO₃⁻).

pH of some body fluids
FluidApprox. pH
Gastric (stomach) acid1.5 to 3.5
Urine4.5 to 8 (avg ~6)
Blood7.35 to 7.45
Pancreatic juice~8

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

pH tells you how sour (acid) or slippery-soapy (base) something is. Lemon juice is a strong acid; baking soda is a base. A buffer is like a sponge that grabs or squeezes out acid to keep your blood at just the right "not too sour, not too soapy" level.

Where you'll meet this in A&P

Acid-base balance handled by the lungs (breathe off CO₂) and kidneys (adjust HCO₃⁻ and H⁺), enzyme function (each enzyme has an ideal pH), stomach digestion, and the acidosis/alkalosis you will study in respiratory and renal physiology.

07

Biomolecules and ATP

Four families of large molecules build and run the body, and ATP is the energy that powers it.

The four biomolecules
ClassBuilding blockMain jobs
CarbohydratesMonosaccharides (e.g., glucose)Quick energy; glucose is the body's main fuel
LipidsFatty acids and glycerolEnergy storage, cell membranes, steroid hormones, insulation
ProteinsAmino acidsEnzymes, structure, transport, receptors, muscle contraction, antibodies
Nucleic acidsNucleotidesDNA and RNA store and carry genetic instructions

The body builds big molecules by dehydration synthesis (joining blocks and removing water) and breaks them by hydrolysis (adding water to split them). ATP (adenosine triphosphate) is the cell's energy currency: splitting off one phosphate (ATP to ADP) releases energy the cell uses for work.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Carbs are quick cash, fats are the savings account, proteins are the workers and machines, and nucleic acids are the instruction manual. ATP is a rechargeable battery: use it, recharge it, use it again.

Where you'll meet this in A&P

Metabolism and cellular respiration, muscle contraction (powered by ATP), enzymes (proteins) in every pathway, the phospholipids that form membranes, and steroid hormones (lipids) in the endocrine system.

08

Reactions and Enzymes

Chemical reactions build and break molecules. Enzymes are what make those reactions fast enough for life.

Types of chemical reactions
ReactionWhat happensBody example
Synthesis (anabolic)Small parts join into a bigger molecule (A + B → AB)Building protein from amino acids
Decomposition (catabolic)A molecule breaks into parts (AB → A + B)Digesting food; breaking glycogen for glucose
ExchangeParts swap placesThe bicarbonate buffer trading H⁺

Most reactions need a push of energy to start, called activation energy. An enzyme is a biological catalyst (usually a protein) that lowers that push, so the reaction runs fast at body temperature. The molecule an enzyme works on is its substrate, which fits the enzyme's active site like a key in a lock. Enzymes are sensitive to temperature and pH, push those too far and the enzyme denatures and stops working.

Where you'll meet this in A&P

Digestion (digestive enzymes), cellular respiration (making ATP), blood clotting, and every metabolic pathway. It also explains why fever or severe pH changes are dangerous: they disable the enzymes the body runs on.
