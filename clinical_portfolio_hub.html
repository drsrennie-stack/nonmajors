Clinical Portfolio Hub

One reference page per week. Eight pages total. Build them as if you'll actually use them.

← Back to syllabus hub

The Clinical Portfolio is a series of eight reference pages, one per week, that translate the week's anatomy and physiology into a tool a clinician would actually reach for. Each page lives on a single printable sheet. Each page is yours: your organizing logic, your phrasing, your call on what's high-yield enough to make the page.

The point

Anatomy and physiology stop feeling abstract the moment you have to decide what to do with them. A clinician doesn't think, "what is the loop of Henle." They think, "this patient is hypokalemic on furosemide; what now." The portfolio is where you practice that translation, eight times, across every system you study this term.

By the end of the course you'll have an eight-page personal clinical reference. Bring it to BIO 304 lab, to your future nursing or paramedic program, to the bedside. It's a working document.

Each week's portfolio template is a separate file you'll open from your weekly dashboard. The template is scaffolded: the structure is laid out, the prompts are written, you fill in the substance.

Every portfolio page, regardless of topic, has the same four-part structure. This is non-negotiable. Consistency is what makes the portfolio usable as a reference set.

1. Quick Reference (top of page)

The values, cutoffs, formulas, and ranges you'd want to grab in five seconds. This is the lookup zone. Bullet lists, small tables, or labeled values work; dense paragraphs do not.

2. Decision Logic (middle of page)

A series of if/then statements that walk through the actual clinical decisions for the topic. Aim for three to five logic blocks per page. Format:

When serum K+ is below 3.5 mEq/L and patient is on furosemide
→ Hold the next dose, replace potassium per protocol, recheck in 4 hours.

3. Patient Scenarios (bottom of page)

Two to three short vignettes, each with a prompt asking what you'd do, recognize, or order. These are application questions, not recall. The point is to force the page through a real situation while you build it.

4. Integration sentence

One sentence at the very end connecting this week's topic to a prior week's system. For Week 1 there is no prior system; in that case, write one sentence connecting this page to something you already knew before BIO 304.

04 Rubric

How each page is scored

Each portfolio page is worth 12.5 points. Eight pages = 100 points = 15% of your final course grade.

ComponentPointsWhat earns full credit
Quick Reference4.0Values, cutoffs, and formulas are accurate, complete, and organized for rapid retrieval.
Decision Logic4.0If/then statements are clinically correct and cover the high-yield decision points for the topic.
Patient Scenarios3.0Two to three vignettes show application, not memorization. Reasoning is shown, not just an answer.
Integration sentence1.5One sentence connects this week to a prior week's system in a non-trivial way.
Total per page: 12.5 points
Two ways to lose points fast
  • Lecture summary instead of decision tool. If a stranger reading your page can't tell what action it points toward, you've written notes, not a reference.
  • Page exceeds one printable side. Compression is part of the skill. Cut, don't expand.

06 Examples

What a strong page looks like

A strong portfolio page has these visible signs:

  • Specific numbers. "HbA1c 6.5% or higher = diabetes" beats "elevated HbA1c suggests diabetes" every time.
  • If/then structure throughout. Even the Quick Reference is organized so you can find the value and know what it triggers.
  • Original phrasing. The page reads as if the student wrote it for themselves, not as if they pasted from a textbook.
  • Visual hierarchy. The reader can locate the Quick Reference, the Decision Logic, and the Scenarios without reading carefully.
  • Compression. Fits one side of one sheet, with white space, not crammed margins.

A weak page reads like a chapter summary, has no decision points, lists facts without consequences, or sprawls onto two pages.

07 AI policy

What you can and cannot use AI for

Build it yourself

The portfolio's value is that you built it. Asking an AI to generate a portfolio page defeats the entire assignment. Submissions that show signs of generated content (uniform phrasing across students, generic structure, content the student can't explain when asked) earn a zero and a referral under the academic integrity policy.

Allowed

  • Asking an AI tutor to explain a concept you find confusing. Example: "explain why furosemide causes hypokalemia."
  • Asking an AI to quiz you on the topic before you build the page.
  • Using AI to check your understanding of a value or cutoff after you've drafted it.

Not allowed

  • Asking AI to write the page, draft sections, or generate the decision logic.
  • Asking AI to produce scenarios for you.
  • Pasting your draft into AI and asking it to "improve" or "rewrite" the content.

Rule of thumb: AI can teach you the topic. Your fingers, on your keyboard or pen, build the page.

08 Submission

How to turn each page in

  1. Open the week's portfolio template (linked from your weekly dashboard).
  2. Fill in your content. Stay within the one-page limit.
  3. Save as PDF. (Print to PDF works on every platform; see the Submission Directions document if you need device-specific guidance.)
  4. File-name your PDF: LastName_BIO304_PortfolioWk[N].pdf. Example: Garcia_BIO304_PortfolioWk03.pdf.
  5. Upload to the Canvas Clinical Portfolio assignment for that week.
One page only

Pages that print to two sides will not be re-graded for length. The single-page constraint is part of the assignment. If you cannot fit it, cut content, do not extend.

09 Final note

Why this matters

From Dr. Rennie

The students who do well in clinical settings, in nursing programs, in EMT and paramedic ride-alongs, in mortuary science labs, are not the ones who memorized the most facts. They are the ones who built reliable tools for thinking, then trusted those tools when the moment got loud. This portfolio is a small version of that practice. Build it for yourself.
