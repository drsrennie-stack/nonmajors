Immune System Case Passport

This week you draw one clinical case, the nail in the foot, by hand in three panels: normal, then abnormal, then treatment. The passport is your coach and walks you through every step. Draw each panel yourself, put all three panels on one page, and submit that one page here. Due Sunday, July 26 at 11:59 PM.

How to complete it

Four steps, by hand.

The passport does the coaching. Your job is to draw it, photograph it, and turn it in.

Open the passport and set up your paper

Open the interactive passport (button below). Take ONE sheet and split it into three panels, plus a pencil or pen and two or three colors. Everything goes on that one page. No printer needed.

Draw the three panels

The passport walks you through each panel one step at a time: panel 1 the normal immune defense, panel 2 the spreading wound infection, and panel 3 one first-line treatment you research. Draw from memory first, then use the reference to check yourself.

Photograph your one page

Take one clear, well-lit photo of your single page with all three panels on it. Number the panels 1, 2, and 3 on the page.

Submit your one page

Upload the one photo of your page on the submission page (button below). One page only, and more than one page loses points. Be ready to tell the case out loud from your own drawing.

The one rule

It has to be hand-drawn.

Draw every panel yourself, on paper, with pencil, pen, or colored pencils. Not on an iPad or any tablet, and not with AI. The whole point is the thinking that happens in your hand while you draw, pause the video, and look things up, and that is the one thing an app cannot do for you. Your drawings will be checked. It all goes on one page: three panels, one page, one upload. More than one page loses points, because following directions is part of the grade.

Why it is drawn: drawing from memory is retrieval practice, turning words into a picture is dual coding, and one running case ties it into a story. That combination is what makes it stick far better than typed notes.

No shortcuts

Googling the answers defeats the point.

This map is built so the only way through it is to actually engage with the material. If you go looking for a finished answer to copy, you skip the learning, and it will show. Build it from your own sources: your notes, your textbook, your online lecture video, and any YouTube videos you choose. Pulling it together yourself is the assignment.

Why it works

A map with less text is how you remember.

Turning your notes into a picture with only a few words forces you to organize the information, and organizing it is exactly what lets you pull it back out on an exam. This is a skill worth keeping. Once you can do it here, use the same technique in your other classes to learn and hold on to a lot of material, faster.

How you are graded

Completeness and accuracy, in your own words.

You will not be graded on your art. You will be graded on how complete and accurate your map is, how well you translated the information into your own drawing, and that the work is your own, in your own words.

Plan your time

BIO 304 expects about 25 to 30 hours a week. Each passport should take about 2 to 4 hours. If you have not been keeping up so far, this will feel like a lot of work, and some of that is the catching up, not the drawing. Coming in prepared, having read and watched the material first, makes it go much faster.

What you submit

One page, your own hand.
