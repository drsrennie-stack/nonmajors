Joints & Movement

A joint is wherever two bones meet. Some joints lock bones together for protection, some give a little for cushioning, and some let you move freely. This lecture sorts joints by how much they move, takes a close look at the freely movable synovial joints, and ends with what joints do for the living body and what happens when they wear out. Dr. Sharilyn Rennie

Part 1

What Is a Joint?

Joints are where the skeleton bends. We sort them by how they are built and by how much they move.

Part 1 · Big picture

Where bones meet

Joints

A joint (or articulation) is any place where two bones come together.

  • Joints make the skeleton more than a stiff statue; they let it bend, twist, and move.
  • There is always a trade-off: the more a joint moves, the less stable it tends to be.
  • We can group joints two ways: by what connects the bones and by how much they move.

Part 1 · Classifying

By structure and by function

Joints
  • By structure (the material in between): fibrous, cartilaginous, or synovial.
  • By function (how much they move): immovable, slightly movable, or freely movable.
  • The two systems line up: most fibrous joints are immovable, most cartilaginous joints are slightly movable, and all synovial joints are freely movable.

Part 2

The Holding Joints

Fibrous and cartilaginous joints trade movement for strength and cushioning.

Part 2 · Fibrous

Fibrous joints: locked tight

Joints
  • Bones are held by tough connective tissue, with little or no space between them, so there is almost no movement.
  • Sutures of the skull are the classic example: they lock the cranial bones into a protective case.
  • The slot that holds a tooth in the jaw is also a fibrous joint.
  • Job: maximum stability and protection.

Part 2 · Cartilaginous

Cartilaginous joints: a little give

Joints
  • Bones are joined by cartilage, which allows a small amount of movement.
  • The discs between vertebrae let the spine bend and absorb shock.
  • The pubic symphysis at the front of the pelvis softens during childbirth to let the pelvis widen.
  • Job: cushioning plus limited movement.

Part 3

Synovial Joints

The freely movable joints, the ones you usually picture, share a clever shared design.

Part 3 · Structure

Inside a synovial joint

Joints
  • Articular cartilage: a smooth, slippery cap on each bone end that reduces friction.
  • Joint capsule: a sleeve that encloses the joint and holds everything together.
  • Synovial fluid: a slick fluid inside the capsule that lubricates and feeds the cartilage.
  • Ligaments: tough bands that connect bone to bone and keep the joint stable.

Figure 2. The shared parts of every synovial joint. Credit: OpenStax / CNX Anatomy & Physiology, CC BY. Loads online.

Part 3 · Six shapes

Six kinds of synovial joints

Joints
  • Hinge: bends one way, like a door. Example: elbow, knee.
  • Ball-and-socket: moves in all directions. Example: shoulder, hip.
  • Pivot: rotates. Example: the neck joint that lets you shake your head "no."
  • Gliding (plane): flat surfaces slide. Example: wrist and ankle bones.
  • Saddle: lets the thumb move widely. Example: base of the thumb.
  • Condyloid: oval surface, moves two ways. Example: knuckles.

Figure 3. Joint shape decides what movement is possible. Credit: OpenStax / CNX Anatomy & Physiology, CC BY. Loads online.

Part 4

Movements at Joints

Synovial joints make a vocabulary of movements your muscles can produce.

Part 4 · Movement words

The everyday movements

Function
  • Flexion and extension: decreasing or increasing the angle at a joint, like bending and straightening the elbow.
  • Abduction and adduction: moving a limb away from, or back toward, the body's midline.
  • Rotation: turning a bone around its own axis, like turning your head.
  • Circumduction: moving a limb in a cone shape, like drawing a big circle with your arm.

Part 5

Physiology: Why It Matters

Joints have to balance movement against stability, and keep their own cartilage alive without a blood supply.

Part 5 · Lubrication

Oil and nutrition in one fluid

Physiology
  • Synovial fluid works like oil in a hinge, cutting friction so cartilage surfaces glide instead of grind.
  • Cartilage has no blood supply, so it depends on this fluid to deliver nutrients and carry away waste.
  • Movement squeezes the fluid in and out of the cartilage, which is one reason gentle activity keeps joints healthy.

Figure 4. Movement keeps cartilage fed: a repeating squeeze-and-soak cycle. Diagram: Dr. Sharilyn Rennie.

Part 5 · Trade-off

Stability vs. mobility

Physiology
  • The shoulder is a ball-and-socket joint with a shallow socket, so it moves freely but dislocates easily.
  • The hip is also ball-and-socket but has a deep socket, so it is far more stable and harder to dislocate.
  • Ligaments, muscle tone, and joint shape together set each joint's range of motion.

Figure 5. Socket depth trades mobility for stability. Diagram: Dr. Sharilyn Rennie.

Part 5 · In the clinic

When joints wear or fail

Clinical
  • Osteoarthritis: articular cartilage wears down over time, so bones rub and the joint becomes stiff and painful.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis: the immune system attacks the joint lining, causing swelling and damage.
  • Sprain: an overstretched or torn ligament, common at the ankle.
  • Dislocation: bones forced out of their normal position at a joint, most often the shoulder.

Wrap-up

Key takeaways

Summary
  • A joint is where two bones meet; more movement usually means less stability.
  • By structure, joints are fibrous (immovable), cartilaginous (slightly movable), or synovial (freely movable).
  • Synovial joints share articular cartilage, a capsule, synovial fluid, and ligaments, and come in six shapes.
  • Joints produce named movements: flexion/extension, abduction/adduction, rotation, and circumduction.
  • Physiology: synovial fluid lubricates and feeds cartilage, joint design balances stability against mobility, and wear or injury leads to arthritis, sprains, and dislocations.
