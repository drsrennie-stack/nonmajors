Levels of Organization

From atoms to organism: how complexity scales, and where brand-new function appears.

Start here: anatomy vs. physiology

Anatomy is the study of structure, what a part is and where it sits. Physiology is the study of function, what a part does and how. The two are inseparable because of one rule you will use all course long: structure determines function. The shape of a part is the reason it can do its job. A red blood cell's flattened disc shape is exactly what lets it carry oxygen efficiently.

Anatomy splits into gross (macroscopic) anatomy, seen with the naked eye, and microscopic anatomy (histology), seen only under a microscope.

The body is organized as a hierarchy. Each level is built from the level below it, and at each step up, a new function appears that the smaller parts did not have on their own. A single sodium atom does nothing interesting. Billions of them, arranged as a gradient across a membrane, produce a nerve impulse. Biology lives in the relationships between parts, not in the parts by themselves.

Key idea: emergent property

An emergent property is a function or characteristic that appears only when parts combine at a higher level. It does not exist in the individual parts.

Worked example: a single heart muscle cell can twitch, but it cannot pump blood. Link millions of them with intercalated discs so they contract as one coordinated sheet (tissue level), build them into the four-chambered heart (organ level), and a brand-new function emerges, pumping blood through the whole body. "Pumping" exists at the organ level, not in any one cell. That is emergence.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

One LEGO brick can't do much. But snap thousands together the right way and you get a spaceship that can "fly" across the room. The flying isn't hiding inside any single brick, it shows up only when the bricks are put together. Your body works the same way: tiny parts team up, and brand-new powers appear that none of the parts had alone.

The six levels (bottom to top)

  • Chemical: atoms join into molecules (water, proteins, DNA).
  • Cellular: molecules build the cell, the smallest living unit.
  • Tissue: similar cells working together (e.g., muscle tissue).
  • Organ: two or more tissue types forming a structure with a job (the heart).
  • Organ system: organs cooperating toward a shared goal (cardiovascular system).
  • Organism: all systems together, a living person.

Don't mix these up

Organ
One structure, e.g., the kidney.
Organ system
The kidney plus ureters, bladder, urethra (the urinary system).

Why this matters clinically

When a patient has a problem, you have to locate it at the right level. A drug acts at the chemical or cellular level, but the patient feels it at the organism level. Treatment-resistant high blood pressure might be chemical (a receptor mutation), cellular (kidney cells malfunctioning), an organ problem (kidney damage), or a system problem (hormone dysregulation). Each level needs a different fix.

Clinicians who move fluently between levels catch what others miss, like the patient whose "fatigue" turns out to be a cellular side effect of a medication rather than something psychological.

  2. Pick three organ systems and write one sentence on each one's job.
  3. Explain "emergent property" using an example of your own.

Anatomical Terminology and Body Regions

Anatomical position, directional terms, body planes, cavities, and abdominal regions.

Anatomical position is the agreed-on reference frame: standing upright, facing forward, feet together, arms at the sides, palms facing forward. Every directional term, plane, and region name assumes this position, even if the patient is lying face-down on a table. That is the whole point: the vocabulary stays consistent no matter how the patient is actually lying.

Serous membranes (the slippery linings)

A serous membrane is a thin, double-layered sheet that lines a ventral cavity and folds back to cover the organs inside it. The parietal layer lines the cavity wall; the visceral layer hugs the organ. Between them is a film of serous fluid that lets organs glide without friction as you breathe, beat, and digest.

Mapping the abdomen

Four quadrants (quick clinical shorthand), split at the navel:

  • RUQ right upper: liver, gallbladder
  • LUQ left upper: stomach, spleen
  • RLQ right lower: appendix, right ovary
  • LLQ left lower: descending/sigmoid colon, left ovary

Nine regions (more precise), like a tic-tac-toe grid:

  • Top row: right hypochondriac, epigastric, left hypochondriac
  • Middle row: right lumbar, umbilical, left lumbar
  • Bottom row: right iliac (inguinal), hypogastric (pubic), left iliac

Why this matters clinically

This is the language every clinical report uses. A radiology read says "mass in the right upper quadrant, anterior to the kidney." A nursing note documents pain "lateral to the umbilicus, radiating distally down the right leg." You cannot follow, or write, the report without the vocabulary.

It is not academic decoration; it is the minimum standard for safe handoffs. A patient with right lower quadrant pain (think appendix) gets a very different workup than one with left lower quadrant pain.

Homeostasis and Feedback Loops

Set points, the four-part negative feedback loop, and why positive feedback is the rare exception.

Homeostasis is the body keeping its internal conditions steady within a narrow range. It is the deepest idea in physiology: almost every system you study exists to regulate something (temperature, blood glucose, blood pressure, pH, fluid balance, calcium). Master the template once and the rest of the course becomes variations on a theme.

The four parts of a negative feedback loop

  1. Stimulus: a change pushes a value away from its target.
  2. Receptor: a sensor detects the change.
  3. Control center: compares the value to the set point and decides the response.
  4. Effector: carries out the response that opposes the change.

Set point vs. current value

Set point
The target the body aims for (e.g., 37°C).
Current value
What the receptor is actually measuring right now.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Negative feedback is just a thermostat. You pick a temperature you want (the set point). If the room gets too cold, the heater kicks on. Once the room warms back up, the heater shuts off. The system is always nudging things back to "just right." Your body has thermostats like this for temperature, blood sugar, and lots more, all keeping you steady.

Negative vs. positive feedback

Negative feedbackPositive feedback
Effect on changeOpposes it (stabilizes)Amplifies it (drives to an endpoint)
How commonThe norm, runs everywhereRare, because it is risky
ExamplesBody temperature, blood glucose, blood pressure, pHChildbirth (labor), blood clotting
Shut-offStops as value returns to set pointStops only when the endpoint is reached

Reframe the names: "negative" does not mean bad, it means stabilizing. Positive feedback is rare because it is dangerous, not because it is unimportant. The body uses it only to reach an endpoint fast (deliver a baby, seal a wound) and then shuts it off. Whenever a system runs to extremes, suspect a broken negative loop or a positive loop that won't turn off.

When the set point itself moves: fever

Disease often resets the set point rather than breaking the machinery. In fever, chemicals called pyrogens tell the hypothalamus to defend 39°C instead of 37°C. The feedback system dutifully drives toward the new target: the patient shivers and vasoconstricts (effector responses) to raise heat, which is why a rising fever feels cold. Once body temperature reaches the new set point, the patient feels hot, because the body actually is hot.

Why this matters clinically

Almost every disease is a homeostatic failure. Diabetes is broken glucose homeostasis; hypertension is broken blood-pressure homeostasis; acidosis and alkalosis are broken pH homeostasis. Heart failure breaks pressure, then volume, then breathing, in a cascade.

Every set of vital signs is a homeostatic readout. A patient who is tachycardic and hypotensive is showing you their baroreflex fighting to save their blood pressure. Reading vitals as feedback loops, not just numbers, changes the level of care you can give.

  3. Explain in your own words why a fever feels cold at the start and hot at the end.

Cell Anatomy and Physiology

The cell is the smallest living unit. Its membrane decides what gets in and what gets out, and that single job drives most of what cells do.

Every cell is wrapped in a plasma membrane built from a phospholipid bilayer: two sheets of fat molecules with water-loving heads facing out and water-fearing tails tucked inside. Proteins float in this layer (the "fluid mosaic" model). The membrane is selectively permeable, meaning it lets some things cross freely and blocks others. Three sub-topics below all come back to this one idea.

The one rule to anchor everything

Gradient
A difference in concentration between two sides of the membrane.
Down the gradient
High to low. No energy needed (passive).
Up the gradient
Low to high. Costs ATP (active).
Selectively permeable
Some molecules pass freely; others need help or are blocked.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Imagine a crowd squished into one corner of a gym. Open the doors and people naturally spread out until they're evenly scattered. They didn't need a push, crowding does it for free. That's diffusion going "downhill," and it costs the cell no energy. But if you wanted to herd everyone back into that one corner, you'd have to push them, that takes work. That's active transport going "uphill," and the cell pays with ATP energy.

4B · Tonicity and red blood cells (RBCs)

Osmosis is the diffusion of water across a membrane toward the side with more dissolved solute. Tonicity describes how a solution outside the cell affects the cell's water, and therefore its size. Red blood cells are the classic demonstration because they swell or shrink visibly.

Compare the solution OUTSIDE the cell to the inside
Solution outsideSolute vs. cellWater movesRBC result
IsotonicEqualNo net movementNormal shape (stable)
HypotonicLess solute outsideInto the cellSwells, can burst (hemolysis)
HypertonicMore solute outsideOut of the cellShrinks and shrivels (crenation)
  • Water chases solute. It moves toward the saltier side.
  • Hypo = low outside, so water rushes in: think "hippo swells up."
  • Hyper = high outside, so water leaves: the cell shrivels.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Water likes to even things out, so it always sneaks toward the saltier side. Put a grape in very salty water and water leaves the grape, it turns into a wrinkly raisin (that's a cell shrinking). Put a raisin in plain water and water rushes in until it puffs back up into a grape, and if too much water piles in, it could pop. Your red blood cells do the exact same thing depending on what they're floating in.

Why this matters clinically

IV fluids are chosen by tonicity. Isotonic fluids (like 0.9% normal saline) stay put and expand blood volume without shifting water into or out of cells. A hypotonic fluid drives water into cells and can make them swell, which is dangerous in the brain. A hypertonic fluid pulls water out of cells. Giving pure water IV would make RBCs burst, which is why IV fluids are never plain water.

4C · Exocytosis and endocytosis (bulk transport)

Some cargo is too big to cross the membrane by diffusion or pumps, so the cell wraps it in a membrane bubble called a vesicle. This bulk (vesicular) transport always costs ATP. The direction is in the name: endo = into the cell, exo = exit the cell.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Think of a factory shipping a toy. The instructions come from the office (nucleus), the toy gets built on the assembly line (rough ER), it rides a cart to the wrapping station (Golgi) where it's boxed up, then a delivery cart (vesicle) carries it to the front door (plasma membrane) and tosses it outside (exocytosis). Each station passes the package to the next, in order.

ProcessDirectionWhat it movesTypes / examples
EndocytosisInto the cellLarge particles, fluid, targeted moleculesPhagocytosis (cell eating: immune cells engulf bacteria); pinocytosis (cell drinking: sips fluid); receptor-mediated (grabs specific molecules, e.g., cholesterol/LDL)
ExocytosisOut of the cellSecretions made inside the cellReleasing neurotransmitters, hormones, digestive enzymes, mucus
  • Phagocytosis = solid particles (immune defense).
  • Pinocytosis = fluids and dissolved bits.
  • Exocytosis = the cell's way of secreting and signaling.

Why this matters clinically

These processes run the body's communication and defense. Nerve cells use exocytosis to release neurotransmitters across a synapse, which is where many drugs act. White blood cells use phagocytosis to clear infection. Faulty receptor-mediated endocytosis of LDL is the cause of familial high cholesterol, because cells cannot pull cholesterol out of the blood.
