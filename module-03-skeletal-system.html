Bone Functions, Cartilage, and Bone Shapes

Bones are living organs, not dry sticks. They do far more than hold you up.

Six jobs of the skeletal system
FunctionWhat it means
SupportThe body's framework; holds organs and soft tissue in place
ProtectionSurrounds soft organs (skull guards the brain, ribs guard the heart and lungs)
MovementBones act as levers that muscles pull on
Mineral storageStores calcium and phosphate and releases them when blood levels drop
Blood cell formationRed marrow makes red and white blood cells (hematopoiesis)
Energy storageYellow marrow stores fat for energy

Cartilage in the skeleton

  • Hyaline: most common; covers bone ends (articular), forms the growth plate and the nose, ribs, and trachea.
  • Fibrocartilage: tough shock absorber; intervertebral discs and knee menisci.
  • Elastic: bendy but keeps shape; the external ear.

Bones by shape

  • Long: longer than wide (femur, humerus, finger bones).
  • Short: cube-like (wrist and ankle bones).
  • Flat: thin and often curved (skull, sternum, ribs).
  • Irregular: odd shapes (vertebrae, hip bones).

Why this matters clinically

Because bone stores most of the body's calcium, the skeleton is tied to nerve and muscle function, the heart, and clotting. And because red marrow makes blood cells, bone problems can become blood problems, which is why a marrow sample (and conditions like leukemia) involve the skeletal system.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. List four of the six functions of the skeletal system.
  2. Which cartilage cushions the spine, and which covers the ends of bones?
  3. Classify the femur, a wrist bone, and the sternum by shape.

Long-Bone Anatomy and Bone Tissue

A long bone is a tube of dense bone with spongy ends, wrapped in living membranes.

Parts of a long bone
PartWhat it is
DiaphysisThe shaft; a tube of compact bone
EpiphysisThe ends; spongy bone, often holding red marrow
Epiphyseal plate / lineThe growth plate (cartilage in kids); becomes a line when growth ends
Medullary cavityThe hollow center; holds yellow (fatty) marrow in adults
PeriosteumTough outer membrane; carries vessels and nerves; anchors tendons
EndosteumThin membrane lining the inner cavity
Articular cartilageHyaline cartilage on the ends, where bones meet at a joint

Compact vs. spongy bone

  • Compact (dense) bone: the hard outer shell; built from repeating units called osteons (rings of bone around a central canal carrying vessels). Strong along lines of stress.
  • Spongy (cancellous) bone: an open lattice of struts called trabeculae; lighter, holds red marrow, and braces against stress from many directions.

Inside an osteon

Central canal
Carries blood vessels and nerves.
Lamellae
Concentric rings of hard matrix.
Lacunae
Spaces that house osteocytes.
Canaliculi
Tiny channels that pass nutrients between cells.

What bone is made of (the matrix)

  • Collagen (organic): ropy protein that gives bone flexibility and resists being pulled apart.
  • Calcium salts (inorganic, hydroxyapatite): mineral crystals that give bone its hardness and resist being crushed.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Bone is like reinforced concrete. The collagen is the bendy steel rebar that keeps it from snapping, and the calcium is the hard concrete that keeps it from being squished. You need both: too little mineral and bones bend (soft), too little collagen and they shatter (brittle).

Why this matters clinically

This is why bone needs calcium and vitamin D (for the mineral) plus protein (for the collagen). In children with vitamin D deficiency (rickets), bones lack mineral and bow under weight. The vessel-filled periosteum is also why a broken bone bleeds and hurts, and why it can heal at all.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. Name the shaft and the ends of a long bone, and what type of bone tissue is in each.
  2. What is an osteon, and what runs through its central canal?
  3. Which part of the matrix gives bone hardness, and which gives flexibility?

Bone Cells

Four cell types build, maintain, and recycle bone throughout your life.

CellJobMemory hook
Osteogenic cellStem cell that divides to make new bone cellsThe "starter"
OsteoblastBuilds bone by laying down new matrix"B" for Build
OsteocyteA mature osteoblast trapped in bone; maintains it and senses stressThe "caretaker"
OsteoclastBreaks down bone, releasing calcium to the blood"C" for Carve

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Two crews are always at work in your bones. Osteoblasts Build new bone, and osteoClasts Carve old bone away. When the building crew keeps up with the carving crew, your bones stay strong. When carving outpaces building, bones get weak.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. Which cell builds bone, and which breaks it down?
  2. What is an osteocyte, and what does it do?
  3. When an osteoclast breaks down bone, what gets released into the blood?

Bone Formation, Growth, and Remodeling

Bones form before birth, grow through childhood, and are rebuilt for the rest of your life.

Two ways bone first forms (ossification)

  • Intramembranous: bone forms directly within a soft membrane. Builds the flat bones of the skull and the clavicle.
  • Endochondral: bone replaces a hyaline cartilage "model." Builds most bones, including all long bones.

Two ways bone grows

  • Longer: at the epiphyseal (growth) plate; cartilage grows and is replaced by bone until the plate closes at puberty.
  • Wider: osteoblasts add bone to the outside under the periosteum (appositional growth).

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

For long bones, the body first makes a cartilage "rough draft," then slowly swaps it out for real bone, like scaffolding being replaced by the finished building. You grow taller at the growth plates near the ends of your bones, and when those plates "close" in your teens, you stop getting taller.

Remodeling: bone is never finished

Throughout life, osteoclasts remove old bone and osteoblasts build new bone. About 10% of your skeleton is replaced each year. Bone follows Wolff's law: it adds material where it is stressed, which is why weight-bearing exercise builds stronger bones and why bones thin when unused (bed rest, spaceflight).

Tie-in: your bones are a calcium bank

Blood calcium is held in a narrow range by a negative feedback loop (Module 1). When blood calcium drops, parathyroid hormone (PTH) tells osteoclasts to release calcium from bone (and the kidneys to keep it). When blood calcium is high, calcitonin slows that release. Bone is the savings account the body draws on to keep blood calcium just right.

Why this matters clinically

In osteoporosis, carving outpaces building, so bones lose density and break easily (common after menopause). Prevention leans on calcium, vitamin D, and weight-bearing exercise, exactly the inputs remodeling needs. A child's growth-plate injury is serious because damage there can stunt or distort future growth.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. Which type of ossification builds long bones, and from what model?
  2. Where does a bone grow in length, and when does that stop?
  3. Name the hormone that raises blood calcium by activating osteoclasts.

Fractures and Bone Repair

A fracture is a broken bone. Bone heals itself in a predictable set of steps.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

When a bone breaks, first it bleeds and forms a clot (like a scab inside). Then the body builds a soft "bridge" across the gap, hardens that bridge into bone, and finally sands it down smooth. Clot, soft bridge, hard bridge, smooth finish.

Why this matters clinically

Setting a bone (reduction) lines up the pieces so the callus forms in the right place. Healing is slower when blood supply is poor, in older adults, or in smokers (nicotine narrows vessels). Open fractures are treated urgently because the broken skin invites infection straight into the bone.

The Axial and Appendicular Skeleton

The 206 bones split into two groups: the central axis, and the limbs that hang off it.

  • Skeleton (206 bones)
    • Axial (80): the central core, protects and supports
      • Skull: cranial bones (protect the brain) + facial bones
      • Vertebral column: cervical 7, thoracic 12, lumbar 5, sacrum, coccyx
      • Thoracic cage: 12 pairs of ribs + sternum (protect heart and lungs)
    • Appendicular (126): the limbs and their girdles, built for movement
      • Pectoral girdle: clavicle + scapula
      • Upper limb: humerus, radius, ulna, carpals, metacarpals, phalanges
      • Pelvic girdle: the two hip bones
      • Lower limb: femur, patella, tibia, fibula, tarsals, metatarsals, phalanges

Spine, top to bottom

Cervical (7)
Neck
Thoracic (12)
Chest (ribs attach)
Lumbar (5)
Lower back
Sacrum
Fused base, joins the pelvis
Coccyx
Tailbone

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Picture a coat rack. The axial skeleton is the central pole (skull, spine, ribs) that holds everything up and shields your vital organs. The appendicular skeleton is the arms and legs (the "appendages") that hang off it and let you move. Spine count is easy as breakfast and lunch: 7, 12, 5.

Why this matters clinically

Spine levels are how clinicians map the body: a "C5 injury" or "L4 to L5 disc" points to an exact location. The radius is the forearm bone on the thumb side, a useful anchor when reading an x-ray. And the clavicle is one of the most commonly broken bones because it takes the force of a fall on an outstretched arm.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. Which division includes the skull, spine, and ribs?
  2. Give the number of vertebrae in the cervical, thoracic, and lumbar regions.
  3. Name the two bones of the forearm and the two of the lower leg.

Joints and Body Movements

A joint is where two bones meet. How they are joined decides how much they move.

Classifying joints by movement (and structure)

  • Joints
    • Fibrous – held by fibrous tissue; immovable (skull sutures)
    • Cartilaginous – joined by cartilage; slightly movable (pubic symphysis, discs between vertebrae)
    • Synovial – have a fluid-filled cavity; freely movable (most limb joints)

Inside a synovial joint

Articular cartilage
Smooth hyaline caps on the bone ends.
Joint cavity
Space holding synovial fluid.
Synovial fluid
Slippery lubricant that cuts friction.
Capsule + ligaments
Hold the joint together.

Types of synovial joints

  • Hinge: bends one way (elbow, knee).
  • Ball-and-socket: moves all ways (shoulder, hip).
  • Pivot: rotates (neck, between the two forearm bones).
  • Gliding: flat bones slide (wrist, ankle).
  • Saddle: the base of the thumb.

Body movements (the action words)

MovementMeans
Flexion / ExtensionDecreasing / increasing the angle of a joint (bending / straightening)
Abduction / AdductionMoving away from / toward the body's midline
RotationTurning a bone around its own axis (shaking your head "no")
CircumductionMoving a limb in a cone/circle (windmill arm)
Pronation / SupinationTurning the palm down / up
Dorsiflexion / Plantar flexionLifting the foot up / pointing the toes down

Why this matters clinically

Joints are high-wear parts. Osteoarthritis is the wearing away of articular cartilage, so bone grinds on bone. Rheumatoid arthritis is different: the immune system attacks the synovial membrane. Sprains stretch or tear ligaments. And the freely moving but shallow shoulder (ball-and-socket) trades stability for range of motion, which is why it dislocates more than any other joint.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. Match each to its movement: fibrous, cartilaginous, synovial.
  2. Name the joint type at the shoulder and at the elbow.
  3. Define flexion and abduction.
