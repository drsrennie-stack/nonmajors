Skeletal Muscle Microanatomy

A muscle is a bundle of bundles. Zoom in and you reach the tiny machine that actually shortens: the sarcomere.

Skeletal muscle has four jobs: movement, posture, joint stability, and heat. It is wrapped at three levels by connective tissue that all merges into the tendon.

  • Whole muscle (wrapped by epimysium)
    • Fascicle – a bundle of fibers (wrapped by perimysium)
      • Muscle fiber – one muscle cell (wrapped by endomysium)
        • Myofibril – the contractile rod inside the cell
          • Sarcomere – the contracting unit, made of actin and myosin

Inside a muscle fiber

Sarcolemma
The muscle cell's membrane.
T-tubules
Tunnels that carry the signal deep inside.
Sarcoplasmic reticulum
Stores and releases calcium.
Myofilaments
Thin (actin) and thick (myosin) strands.

Why muscle looks striped

The sarcomere is the unit from one Z disc to the next. Inside, thin actin and thick myosin filaments overlap in an orderly pattern. That overlap is what gives skeletal and cardiac muscle their striped (striated) look under a microscope.

Why this matters clinically

The connective wrappings merge into the tendon that anchors muscle to bone, which is why a torn tendon disables a whole muscle. And because the sarcoplasmic reticulum stores the calcium that triggers contraction, anything that disturbs muscle calcium handling (some genetic disorders, certain anesthesia reactions) can cause dangerous muscle rigidity.

  1. Put these in order from largest to smallest: sarcomere, fascicle, muscle fiber, myofibril, whole muscle.
  2. What does the sarcoplasmic reticulum store?
  3. Which two filaments overlap to make a sarcomere, and why is muscle striped?

02

The Neuromuscular Junction

Muscle does nothing until a nerve tells it to. The meeting point of nerve and muscle is the neuromuscular junction.

A motor neuron reaches out to a muscle fiber but does not touch it. At the tip, the axon releases a chemical messenger, acetylcholine (ACh), into a tiny gap (the synaptic cleft). ACh drifts across and binds receptors on the muscle's membrane (the motor end plate), which opens channels, lets sodium in, and starts an electrical signal that spreads across the muscle fiber and down the T-tubules.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

The nerve and muscle do not actually touch. The nerve "throws" a chemical (acetylcholine) across a tiny gap, like tossing a key. The key fits a lock on the muscle, the lock opens, and the muscle gets the message to fire.

Why this matters clinically

This junction is a favorite target. In myasthenia gravis, the immune system destroys ACh receptors, so muscles tire quickly. Botulinum toxin (Botox) blocks ACh release, paralyzing the muscle. Many nerve gases and some pesticides stop ACh from being cleared, causing nonstop, dangerous muscle firing.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. What chemical does the motor neuron release at the muscle?
  2. What happens when that chemical binds the muscle's receptors?
  3. How does Botox cause muscle paralysis?

Sliding Filament and the Cross-Bridge Cycle

Muscle shortens not because the filaments shrink, but because they slide past each other.

In the sliding filament theory, myosin (thick) filaments grab actin (thin) filaments and pull them inward, so the sarcomere gets shorter while the filaments themselves stay the same length. The trigger is calcium, and the fuel is ATP.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Think of a tug-of-war. The myosin "hands" grab the actin "rope," pull, let go, grab higher up, and pull again, dragging the rope in. Calcium is the whistle that says "go," and ATP is the energy snack the hands need to keep grabbing. No ATP, the hands stay clamped (that is why bodies stiffen after death, rigor mortis).

Why this matters clinically

Rigor mortis happens because dead muscle runs out of ATP, so myosin cannot release actin and the body stiffens. Muscle cramps involve calcium and energy handling gone wrong. And low blood calcium can cause muscle twitching and spasms, a direct link back to the calcium balance you studied with bone.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. In sliding filament theory, do the filaments shorten? What actually happens?
  2. What ion starts contraction, and where is it stored?
  3. Why does the body stiffen after death?

Motor Units and Muscle Mechanics

How the body controls how hard and how smoothly a muscle pulls.

Key terms

Motor unit
One motor neuron plus all the fibers it controls.
Recruitment
Turning on more motor units for more force.
Twitch
One quick contraction from a single stimulus.
Tetanus
Rapid stimuli blur into one smooth, strong contraction.
Muscle tone
Constant slight tension that keeps muscles ready.

Two kinds of contraction

  • Isotonic: the muscle changes length and moves a load (lifting a cup).
  • Isometric: the muscle tenses but does not change length (holding a plank).

More force comes from recruiting more motor units and from firing them faster. Fine control (eye muscles) uses small motor units; powerful movements (thigh) use large ones.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

A muscle is like a team of rowers (motor units). To pull harder, you wake up more rowers (recruitment) and have them row faster. One quick pull is a twitch; lots of fast pulls blur into one strong, smooth pull (tetanus).

Why this matters clinically

Loss of muscle tone or weakness shows up when motor units fail, from nerve injury, disuse, or disease. Physical therapy works partly by improving recruitment. And the difference between isometric and isotonic work matters in rehab: isometric holds can strengthen a muscle without moving a healing joint.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. What is a motor unit?
  2. Name two ways the body increases a muscle's force.
  3. Which contraction changes muscle length: isotonic or isometric?

The Neuron and the Resting Membrane Potential

Neurons are built to carry electrical messages. At rest, they sit like a charged battery, ready to fire.

Parts of a neuron
PartJob
DendritesReceive incoming signals
Cell body (soma)Holds the nucleus; combines the signals
AxonCarries the signal away to other cells
Myelin sheathFatty insulation that speeds the signal
Nodes (gaps in myelin)Let the signal jump and travel faster

Neurons are supported by neuroglia (glial cells), which insulate, feed, and protect them. The cells that make myelin are glia.

The resting membrane potential

At rest the inside of a neuron is about −70 mV, slightly negative compared to the outside. This is set up by uneven ions: sodium (Na⁺) is high outside and potassium (K⁺) is high inside, maintained by the sodium-potassium pump (active transport, costs ATP). This is the charged "battery" from the chemistry reference, ready to discharge into a signal.

Why this matters clinically

Diseases that strip myelin, like multiple sclerosis, slow or block signals, causing weakness, numbness, and vision problems. And because the resting potential depends on potassium and sodium, abnormal blood electrolyte levels can make nerves and the heart misfire, which is why these labs are watched so closely.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. Name the three main parts of a neuron in signal order.
  2. What does myelin do, and what happens when it is damaged?
  3. Which ions are high inside vs. outside a resting neuron?

Action Potentials and Synaptic Transmission

An action potential is the nerve signal itself: a quick electrical spike that travels down the axon.

It is all-or-none: either the stimulus is strong enough to fire a full spike, or nothing happens. In myelinated axons the spike jumps node to node, which is why myelin makes signals fast.

Crossing the synapse

When the spike reaches the axon's end, it cannot jump to the next cell electrically. Instead, calcium enters and triggers exocytosis of neurotransmitter (the same vesicle release you met in Module 1). The neurotransmitter crosses the gap and binds receptors on the next cell, starting a new signal. Then it is quickly cleared so the synapse can reset.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

An action potential is like a row of dominoes falling down the axon, fast and all-or-nothing. When it reaches the end, it cannot leap the gap, so it sends a chemical "text message" (neurotransmitter) across to the next cell, which then starts its own row of dominoes.

Why this matters clinically

Most drugs that act on the brain work at the synapse: antidepressants, anesthetics, painkillers, and stimulants all change how neurotransmitters are released, received, or cleared. Local anesthetics block the sodium channels that make the action potential, so the nerve cannot fire and you feel no pain.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. Which ion rushes in during depolarization, and which flows out during repolarization?
  2. What does "all-or-none" mean for an action potential?
  3. How does a signal cross from one neuron to the next?
