Nervous System Organization

The nervous system is the body's fast control network. It splits into a control center and the wiring that reaches the rest of the body.

  • Nervous system
    • Central (CNS): brain + spinal cord; integrates and decides
    • Peripheral (PNS): all the nerves outside the CNS
      • Sensory (afferent): carries info toward the CNS
      • Motor (efferent): carries commands away from the CNS
        • Somatic: voluntary; controls skeletal muscle
        • Autonomic: involuntary; controls organs and glands

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

The CNS is the boss (brain and spinal cord) that makes decisions. The PNS is all the phone lines running out to the body. Some lines carry news in (sensory), some carry orders out (motor). The orders are either ones you choose (somatic, like waving) or automatic ones you do not think about (autonomic, like your heartbeat).

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. What two organs make up the CNS?
  2. Which division carries information toward the CNS: sensory or motor?
  3. Is controlling your heartbeat somatic or autonomic?

The Brain and Spinal Cord (CNS)

The brain is the control center; the spinal cord is the express highway connecting it to the body.

The main brain regions
RegionWhat it does
CerebrumThinking, memory, senses, and voluntary movement (the wrinkled outer brain)
CerebellumBalance, coordination, and smooth movement
DiencephalonThalamus (relay station) and hypothalamus (homeostasis and hormone control)
BrainstemControls the vital automatic functions: breathing, heart rate, blood pressure
Spinal cordTwo-way highway for signals; runs reflexes on its own

How the CNS is protected

Bone
Skull and vertebrae.
Meninges
Three protective membranes.
CSF
Cerebrospinal fluid cushions and floats the brain.
Blood-brain barrier
Filters what gets from blood into brain tissue.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Your brain is so precious it gets four layers of protection: a bony helmet (skull), a few wrapping blankets (meninges), a water cushion it floats in (CSF), and a security gate that screens the blood before it gets in (blood-brain barrier).

Why this matters clinically

Knowing the regions lets you predict the damage. A stroke in the cerebrum may take speech or movement; damage to the cerebellum wrecks balance; injury to the brainstem is life-threatening because it runs breathing and heartbeat. A spinal cord injury cuts communication below the level of the damage.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. Which brain region controls breathing and heart rate?
  2. Which handles balance and coordination?
  3. Name the four ways the CNS is protected.

The Autonomic Nervous System and Reflexes

The autonomic system runs the body on autopilot, and reflexes let you react before you even think.

A reflex arc

A reflex is an automatic, protective response. It travels a fixed path so it can happen fast, often before the brain is even aware.

Sympathetic vs. parasympathetic

Body partSympathetic ("fight or flight")Parasympathetic ("rest and digest")
Heart rateSpeeds upSlows down
PupilsWiden (dilate)Narrow (constrict)
AirwaysOpen upNarrow
DigestionSlows downSpeeds up

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

The sympathetic system is the gas pedal: it revs you up for action (racing heart, wide eyes). The parasympathetic is the brake: it calms you down to rest and digest. A reflex is your body acting before your brain catches up, like yanking your hand off a hot stove.

Why this matters clinically

The fight-or-flight response is why a frightened or bleeding patient has a racing heart and wide pupils, the sympathetic system trying to protect them. Reflex tests (like the knee jerk) check whether the nerve pathway and spinal cord are working at a given level.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. List the five steps of a reflex arc in order.
  2. Which division speeds up the heart: sympathetic or parasympathetic?
  3. What does the parasympathetic system do to digestion?

The Special Senses

Special senses turn light, sound, and movement into nerve signals the brain can read.

Vision, hearing, and balance
SenseKey partsHow it works
VisionCornea, lens, retina (rods and cones), optic nerveLight is focused on the retina; rods see dim light and black-and-white, cones see color; the optic nerve carries it to the brain
HearingOuter ear, middle-ear bones, cochleaSound vibrates the eardrum and tiny bones, which shake fluid in the cochlea; hair cells turn it into nerve signals
Balance (equilibrium)Semicircular canals of the inner earFluid moving in the canals tells the brain about head position and motion

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Your eyes are like a camera: a lens focuses light onto a screen (the retina) that sends the picture to the brain. Your ears turn sound into shaking fluid that tiny hairs feel and report. And the same inner ear has built-in "levels" that sense when your head tips or spins, which is how you keep your balance.

Why this matters clinically

Damage to cones causes color blindness; clouding of the lens is a cataract. Loss of the cochlea's hair cells (from loud noise or aging) causes permanent hearing loss. And problems in the inner ear's balance organs cause vertigo, the spinning dizziness.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. Which retinal cells see color, and which see dim light?
  2. What structure in the inner ear turns sound into nerve signals?
  3. Which part of the ear senses balance?

Endocrine Principles

The endocrine system is the body's slow chemical control network, using hormones carried in the blood.

A hormone is a chemical messenger released into the blood by a gland. It travels everywhere but only affects target cells that have the matching receptor, like a key that only opens certain locks. Most hormones are controlled by negative feedback (Module 1): when a hormone's effect is achieved, its release is turned down.

Two control systems compared
Nervous systemEndocrine system
MessengerElectrical signals + neurotransmitterHormones in the blood
SpeedFast (milliseconds)Slow (seconds to days)
DurationBriefLong-lasting
TargetSpecific muscle or glandAny cell with the right receptor

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

The nervous system is a phone call: fast, direct, and over quickly. The endocrine system is mailing letters: slower, but the message goes out widely and the effect lasts longer. Only the houses with the right mailbox (receptor) actually open the letter.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. What is a hormone, and how does it travel?
  2. Why does a hormone only affect certain cells?
  3. Give one way the endocrine system differs from the nervous system.

The Major Endocrine Glands

A quick map of the main glands, their key hormones, and what those hormones do.

Glands and their headline hormones
GlandKey hormone(s)Main effect
HypothalamusReleasing hormonesControls the pituitary; links nervous and endocrine systems
Pituitary ("master gland")Growth hormone, TSH, ACTH, FSH/LH; ADH, oxytocinDirects other glands; controls growth, water balance, labor
ThyroidThyroid hormone; calcitoninSets metabolic rate; lowers blood calcium
ParathyroidParathyroid hormone (PTH)Raises blood calcium
Adrenal glandsCortisol, aldosterone, epinephrineStress response, salt and water balance, fight-or-flight
PancreasInsulin; glucagonInsulin lowers blood sugar; glucagon raises it
Ovaries / testesEstrogen and progesterone / testosteroneReproduction and sex characteristics

Notice the feedback pairs

Many hormones work in opposing pairs to hold a value steady, the same negative-feedback idea from Module 1. Insulin lowers blood sugar while glucagon raises it; calcitonin lowers blood calcium while PTH raises it. The body keeps a value in range by pushing from both sides.

Why this matters clinically

Endocrine disease is usually "too much" or "too little." Diabetes is too little working insulin, so blood sugar stays high. Hypothyroidism slows metabolism (fatigue, weight gain); hyperthyroidism speeds it up. Too much cortisol or growth hormone causes recognizable syndromes. Reading hormones as feedback systems tells you which way the loop has failed.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. Which gland is called the "master gland," and what does it control?
  2. Name the two pancreas hormones and what each does to blood sugar.
  3. Which hormone raises blood calcium, and which lowers it?
