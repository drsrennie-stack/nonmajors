Blood Composition and Hematopoiesis

Blood is a liquid connective tissue: cells floating in a watery matrix called plasma.

Blood does three big jobs: transport (oxygen, nutrients, waste, hormones), regulation (temperature and pH), and protection (clotting and immunity). If you spin a blood sample, it separates into two parts.

What blood is made of
PartShareWhat it is and does
Plasma~55%Watery fluid with proteins, nutrients, and wastes dissolved in it
Red blood cells~45%Carry oxygen using hemoglobin; no nucleus; biconcave disc
White blood cells<1%Defend the body against infection
Platelets<1%Cell fragments that start blood clots

All of these blood cells are made in red bone marrow, a process called hematopoiesis, linking this system right back to the skeleton.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Blood is like a juice with stuff floating in it. The plasma is the watery juice. The red cells are the delivery trucks carrying oxygen, the white cells are the soldiers, and the platelets are the patch kit for leaks. All of them are made in the squishy red marrow inside your bones.

Why this matters clinically

Anemia is too few red cells or too little hemoglobin, so tissues get less oxygen (fatigue, pallor). Leukemia is a cancer of white blood cells. Because blood cells come from bone marrow, marrow disease shows up as blood-count problems, and a marrow transplant can rebuild the whole supply.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. Name the two main parts you get when blood is spun down.
  2. What do red cells carry, and using what protein?
  3. Where are blood cells made?

Hemostasis and Blood Typing

Hemostasis is how the body stops bleeding. Blood typing is how we match blood safely.

Stopping a bleed (hemostasis)

Blood types (ABO and Rh)

Red cells carry markers called antigens. The ABO system (A, B, AB, O) and the Rh factor (positive or negative) decide your type. Your plasma carries antibodies against the antigens you do not have, so the wrong blood triggers a dangerous reaction.

  • Type O negative is the universal donor (no ABO or Rh antigens to attack).
  • Type AB positive is the universal recipient (no antibodies to attack donor blood).

Why this matters clinically

A transfusion reaction happens when mismatched blood is given: antibodies clump and destroy the donor red cells, which can be fatal. Rh matters in pregnancy too, an Rh-negative mother can make antibodies against an Rh-positive baby, which is why Rh is checked and treated. On the clotting side, too little clotting causes bleeding (hemophilia); too much causes dangerous clots (stroke, DVT).

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. List the three steps of hemostasis in order.
  2. Which blood type is the universal donor?
  3. Why does giving the wrong blood type cause a reaction?

Heart Anatomy

The heart is really two pumps side by side: a right pump for the lungs and a left pump for the body.

The four chambers

Right atrium
Receives oxygen-poor blood from the body.
Right ventricle
Pumps it to the lungs.
Left atrium
Receives oxygen-rich blood from the lungs.
Left ventricle
Pumps it to the whole body (thickest wall).

Valves keep blood moving one way

  • AV valves (tricuspid on the right, mitral on the left) sit between each atrium and ventricle.
  • Semilunar valves (pulmonary and aortic) guard the exits to the lungs and body.
  • Valves snapping shut make the "lub-dub" heart sounds.

Two circuits

  • Pulmonary circuit: right heart sends blood to the lungs to pick up oxygen.
  • Systemic circuit: left heart sends oxygen-rich blood to the rest of the body.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Your heart is two pumps in one. The right side sends "used" blood to the lungs for a fresh load of oxygen. The left side sends that fresh blood out to your whole body. The valves are one-way doors so blood never flows backward, and you hear them close as your heartbeat.

Why this matters clinically

The left ventricle works hardest, so it has the thickest wall and is most affected when blood pressure is high for years. Leaky or stiff valves make abnormal sounds (murmurs) and force the heart to work harder. A heart attack happens when the heart's own supply vessels (coronary arteries) get blocked.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. Which chamber pumps blood to the lungs, and which to the body?
  2. What is the job of the heart valves?
  3. Which circuit carries blood to the lungs?

The Cardiac Cycle and Conduction

The heart beats on its own thanks to a built-in electrical system that fires in a set order.

One heartbeat is the cardiac cycle: systole (the chambers contract and push blood out) and diastole (they relax and fill). The signal to contract starts in the heart itself, no nerve required.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Your heart has its own spark plug, the SA node, that fires about 60 to 100 times a minute. The spark spreads in a set path so the top chambers squeeze first, then the bottom chambers, in perfect timing. That is why the heart keeps a steady beat without you telling it to.

Why this matters clinically

An ECG records this electrical activity; abnormal patterns reveal arrhythmias (irregular beats). If the SA node fails or the path is blocked, an artificial pacemaker can take over. A heart attack can damage the conduction path and throw the rhythm off.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. What is the difference between systole and diastole?
  2. Which structure is the heart's natural pacemaker?
  3. Put these in order: AV node, SA node, Purkinje fibers.

Blood Vessels

Three kinds of vessels carry blood out, exchange it, and bring it back.

VesselDirectionBuilt for
ArteriesCarry blood away from the heartThick, muscular walls to handle high pressure
CapillariesConnect arteries to veinsWalls one cell thick, for exchange of gases and nutrients
VeinsCarry blood back to the heartThinner walls and valves to stop backflow at low pressure

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Arteries are the high-pressure pipes leaving the heart (thick, so they do not burst). Capillaries are the tiny, paper-thin pipes where oxygen and food actually pass into your cells. Veins are the return pipes; since the pressure is low, they have little one-way doors (valves) to keep blood from sliding backward.

Why this matters clinically

All exchange happens in capillaries, so that is where oxygen and nutrients reach tissue. When vein valves fail, blood pools and you get varicose veins. Hardened, narrowed arteries (atherosclerosis) are behind heart attacks and strokes.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. Which vessels carry blood away from the heart?
  2. Where does the exchange of gases and nutrients happen?
  3. Why do veins need valves?

Hemodynamics and Blood Pressure

Blood pressure is the force blood pushes on vessel walls. The body guards it closely.

Blood pressure is written as systolic over diastolic (about 120/80 mmHg): the top number is the push when the heart contracts, the bottom is the resting pressure between beats. It depends on three things:

  • Cardiac output: how much blood the heart pumps.
  • Blood volume: how much blood is in the system.
  • Resistance: how narrow or wide the vessels are.

Tie-in: the baroreflex

Blood pressure is held steady by a negative feedback loop (Module 1). Baroreceptors in big arteries sense pressure and report to the brainstem, which adjusts heart rate and vessel width to correct it. A patient who stands up and briefly feels dizzy is feeling this loop catch up.

Why this matters clinically

Hypertension (chronically high blood pressure) quietly damages the heart, vessels, kidneys, and eyes, which is why it is called a silent killer. It is treated by lowering the same three factors: pumping (heart-rate drugs), volume (diuretics), and resistance (vessel-relaxing drugs). A tachycardic, low-pressure patient is showing the baroreflex fighting to keep pressure up.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. What do the top and bottom numbers of a blood pressure mean?
  2. Name the three factors that set blood pressure.
  3. What do baroreceptors do?
