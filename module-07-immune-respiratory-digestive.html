Lymphatic System and Innate Immunity

The lymphatic system drains and cleans body fluid, and the innate immune system is your fast, general defense.

The lymphatic system collects fluid that leaks out of capillaries (now called lymph), filters it through lymph nodes, and returns it to the blood. Its organs (nodes, spleen, thymus, tonsils) are also bases for immune cells.

  • Immune defense
    • Innate (fast, general):
      • Barriers: skin and mucous membranes
      • Internal: phagocytes, inflammation, fever, NK cells
    • Adaptive (slow, specific, has memory): B cells and T cells (next topic)

Inflammation is innate immunity in action; its four signs are redness, heat, swelling, and pain, as vessels widen and immune cells rush in. Fever (the reset set point from Module 1) helps fight infection.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Innate immunity is the castle's basic defenses: high walls (skin), a moat (mucus), and guards who attack anything that gets in (phagocytes that eat germs). It is fast and not picky. The lymph system is the castle's drainage and checkpoint network that catches invaders in its filters (lymph nodes), which is why your nodes swell when you are sick.

Why this matters clinically

Swollen, tender lymph nodes are a sign the immune system is fighting nearby infection. Because the lymph system also carries cells around the body, it is a route cancer can spread (metastasize), which is why nodes are checked in cancer staging.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. What does the lymphatic system return to the blood?
  2. Name two parts of innate immunity.
  3. List the four signs of inflammation.

02

Adaptive Immunity

Adaptive immunity is slower to start but specific and long-remembering. It is why vaccines work.

Adaptive immunity targets a specific invader (recognized by its antigen) and keeps memory of it. It has two arms:

ArmMain cellHow it fights
Humoral immunityB cellsMake antibodies that tag and neutralize invaders in body fluids
Cell-mediated immunityT cellsDirectly kill the body's own infected or abnormal cells

After an infection, memory cells remain. Meet that same germ again and the response is faster and stronger, so you may not even get sick. A vaccine trains this memory safely, without the real disease.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Adaptive immunity is the special forces. B cells make "wanted posters" (antibodies) that stick to a specific germ so it gets caught. T cells hunt down your own cells that have been hijacked by a virus. And the team keeps a memory file, so next time that germ shows up, they crush it fast. A vaccine is just a practice drill using a harmless version.

Why this matters clinically

HIV attacks helper T cells, slowly disabling the whole adaptive system (AIDS). Allergies are the immune system overreacting to a harmless thing. Autoimmune diseases are it attacking the body's own tissue. And vaccines have wiped out or controlled diseases by building memory before exposure.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. Which cells make antibodies, and which kill infected cells?
  2. What do memory cells do?
  3. How does a vaccine protect you?

Respiratory Anatomy and Mechanics

The respiratory system brings air to the lungs and moves it in and out by changing pressure.

Breathing works by changing the size of the chest. When the diaphragm contracts and drops, the chest gets bigger, pressure inside falls, and air flows in. When it relaxes, the chest shrinks, pressure rises, and air flows out. Air always moves from higher to lower pressure.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Your lungs work like a bellows or a stretchy balloon in a box. When the diaphragm (the muscle under your lungs) pulls down, the box gets bigger and air rushes in to fill the space. When it relaxes, the box gets smaller and pushes air back out. You are not sucking air so much as making room for it.

Why this matters clinically

Asthma narrows the bronchioles, making it hard to move air. Pneumonia fills alveoli with fluid, blocking gas exchange. COPD (often from smoking) damages alveoli permanently. All three show up as shortness of breath and low oxygen.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. Put in order: bronchi, trachea, alveoli, larynx.
  2. What does the diaphragm do during inhalation?
  3. Why does air flow into the lungs when the chest expands?

Gas Exchange and Transport

Oxygen and carbon dioxide trade places by simple diffusion, in the lungs and again at the tissues.

WhereWhat happens
In the lungsOxygen moves from alveoli into blood; carbon dioxide moves out to be exhaled
At the tissuesOxygen moves from blood into cells; carbon dioxide moves from cells into blood

Gas exchange is fast because the alveolar wall is one cell thick, the emergence example from Module 1. Each gas simply diffuses from where it is high to where it is low.

How the gases travel in blood

  • Oxygen rides on hemoglobin inside red blood cells.
  • Carbon dioxide travels mostly as bicarbonate dissolved in plasma, linking back to the blood's pH buffer from the chemistry reference.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

At the lungs, your blood is like a bus stop: oxygen gets on and carbon dioxide gets off. At your cells, the opposite happens: oxygen gets off to be used, and the waste carbon dioxide hops on for the ride back. The swap works because gases naturally drift from crowded areas to empty ones.

Why this matters clinically

A pulse oximeter (the finger clip) reads how full your hemoglobin is with oxygen. Carbon monoxide is deadly because it grabs hemoglobin's oxygen seats and will not let go. And because carbon dioxide rides as bicarbonate, breathing problems quickly become acid-base problems, tying breathing to blood pH.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. At the lungs, which gas enters the blood and which leaves?
  2. What molecule carries oxygen in the blood?
  3. Why is the alveolar wall being one cell thick important?

Digestive Anatomy and Motility

The digestive tract is one long tube from mouth to anus, with helper organs feeding into it.

Accessory organs (salivary glands, liver, gallbladder, pancreas) sit off to the side and add fluids and enzymes through ducts. Food is pushed along by peristalsis, waves of smooth-muscle squeezing (the muscle tissue from Module 2 at work).

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Your digestive system is one long water slide from mouth to exit. Muscles in the walls squeeze behind the food to push it along (peristalsis), like squeezing a tube of toothpaste. The liver, gallbladder, and pancreas are side stations that squirt in juices to help break the food down.

Why this matters clinically

GERD (acid reflux) is stomach acid backing up into the esophagus. Ulcers are sores in the stomach or intestinal lining. When peristalsis slows or stops (an obstruction or ileus), food cannot move and it becomes a surgical emergency.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. List the organs food passes through, in order.
  2. Name two accessory organs that add juices to digestion.
  3. What is peristalsis?

Digestion and Absorption

Digestion breaks food into pieces small enough to absorb; absorption moves those pieces into the blood.

Two kinds of digestion

  • Mechanical: physically breaking food up (chewing, the stomach churning).
  • Chemical: enzymes splitting molecules apart (the enzymes from the chemistry reference at work).

Enzymes by nutrient

  • Carbohydrates → broken by amylase.
  • Proteins → broken by proteases (like pepsin).
  • Fats → broken by lipase (bile first breaks fat into droplets).

Absorption happens mainly in the small intestine, whose lining is covered in villi and microvilli (the surface structures from Module 2) that hugely increase surface area. The large intestine mostly absorbs water and forms stool.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Digestion is a disassembly line: first you smash the food (chewing), then chemical "scissors" (enzymes) snip it into tiny pieces. Those pieces get soaked up through the fuzzy, towel-like lining of the small intestine (villi), which has tons of surface to grab nutrients. The leftovers head to the large intestine, which wrings out the water.

Why this matters clinically

When absorption fails, nutrients are lost: in celiac disease the villi are damaged, so the small intestine cannot soak up food well. Lactose intolerance is missing one enzyme (lactase). And because the large intestine reclaims water, severe diarrhea causes dangerous dehydration, tying right back to fluid balance.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. What is the difference between mechanical and chemical digestion?
  2. Which enzyme breaks down fats, and what does bile do first?
  3. Where are most nutrients absorbed, and what increases the surface area there?
