Kidney Anatomy and the Nephron

The kidneys are the body's filters. They clean the blood and fine-tune water, salts, and pH.

What the kidneys do
JobWhy it matters
Filter bloodRemove wastes (like urea) and make urine
Balance water and electrolytesKeep sodium, potassium, and fluid in range
Control pHAdjust acid and bicarbonate to hold blood pH steady
Help blood pressureAdjust fluid volume and release renin
Support red cells and boneRelease EPO (signals red-cell production) and activate vitamin D

Urine drains from the kidney into the ureter, is stored in the bladder, and leaves through the urethra. The working unit inside each kidney is the nephron (about a million per kidney): a tiny filter (the glomerulus) attached to a long winding tubule.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Your kidneys are like coffee filters for your blood. They let water and small wastes drip through, then carefully take back the good stuff your body still needs. Each kidney has about a million tiny filters (nephrons) doing this all day, every day.

Why this matters clinically

When kidneys fail, wastes and fluid build up and electrolytes go haywire, so a machine (dialysis) has to filter the blood instead. Because the kidneys also make EPO, kidney failure often causes anemia, and because they activate vitamin D, it weakens bone too.

  2. Trace urine from kidney to outside the body.
  3. What is the working unit of the kidney called?

02

Urine Formation

The nephron makes urine in three steps: filter everything, take back what you need, and add a little extra waste.

What stays vs. what goes
Kept (reabsorbed)Removed (in urine)
Most water, glucose, amino acids, useful ionsUrea and other wastes, excess water, excess ions, drugs

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Think of it like cleaning out a junk drawer by dumping everything on the table. First you dump it all out (filtration), then you put back the things you want to keep (reabsorption), and toss a bit of extra trash on the pile (secretion). Whatever is left on the table gets thrown away as urine.

Why this matters clinically

Urine tests are a window into the body. Glucose in urine can signal diabetes (the filter is overwhelmed). Protein or blood in urine suggests the filter is damaged. And because the kidney secretes drugs, doctors adjust medication doses when kidneys are weak so drugs do not build up.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. List the three steps that make urine, plus excretion.
  2. Name two things normally reabsorbed back into the blood.
  3. Why might glucose in the urine be a warning sign?

Fluid, Electrolyte, and Acid-Base Balance

The kidneys, lungs, and hormones work together to keep your water, salts, and pH in a narrow safe range.

Water and electrolytes

  • ADH tells the kidney to keep water (concentrating urine) when you are dry.
  • Aldosterone tells it to keep sodium (and water follows).
  • Thirst drives you to drink. These all defend blood volume and pressure.

Acid-base (pH 7.35 to 7.45)

  • Lungs adjust fast by breathing off CO₂.
  • Kidneys adjust slowly with bicarbonate and H⁺.
  • Together they hold blood pH steady (the buffer idea from the chemistry reference).

Tie-in: this is homeostasis in action

Every part of this topic is a negative feedback loop from Module 1. Too little water, ADH brings it back. Too much acid, lungs and kidneys remove it. The body is constantly nudging these values back toward their set points.

Why this matters clinically

Dehydration drops blood volume and pressure; severe cases need IV fluids (the tonicity from Module 2 again). Abnormal potassium can stop the heart, which is why it is watched closely. And acidosis or alkalosis (often from lung or kidney problems) can become emergencies because enzymes only work in a narrow pH window.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. Which hormone makes the kidney keep water?
  2. Name the two organs that control blood pH, and which is faster.
  3. What is the normal range for blood pH?

The Male Reproductive System

The male system makes sperm and the hormone testosterone, and delivers sperm.

Main parts and jobs
PartJob
TestesMake sperm and testosterone
EpididymisStores sperm and lets them mature
Vas deferensCarries sperm toward the urethra
Accessory glandsSeminal vesicles and prostate add fluid to make semen
Penis / urethraDelivers semen out of the body

Testosterone drives sperm production and male characteristics (muscle, deeper voice, body hair), and its release is controlled by feedback from the brain's pituitary gland (Module 5).

Why this matters clinically

The prostate often enlarges with age and can squeeze the urethra, making urination difficult; it is also a common site of cancer. Because sperm and urine share the urethra, infections can affect both, and the testes' need for a cooler temperature is why they sit outside the body.

The Female Reproductive System

The female system makes eggs and hormones, and provides the place where a baby can develop.

Main parts and jobs
PartJob
OvariesMake eggs and the hormones estrogen and progesterone
Uterine (fallopian) tubesCarry the egg; usually where fertilization happens
UterusWhere a fertilized egg implants and a baby grows
VaginaBirth canal and passage to the uterus

The menstrual cycle (about 28 days) prepares the body for possible pregnancy. FSH and LH from the pituitary ripen an egg and trigger ovulation (release of the egg), while estrogen and progesterone build up the uterine lining. If no pregnancy occurs, the lining sheds as a period and the cycle restarts, a feedback system from Module 5.

Explain it simply (like you're 10)

Each month the body gets ready in case a baby is coming: it ripens an egg and thickens a cozy lining in the uterus. If the egg is not fertilized, the body clears the lining out (a period) and starts fresh next month. Hormones are the timers that run the whole schedule.

Why this matters clinically

Hormone shifts explain a lot of clinical care: menopause is when the cycle ends and estrogen falls (affecting bone, as you saw with osteoporosis). Cycle problems can signal hormone or thyroid issues. And the uterine tubes being the usual site of fertilization is why a blockage there causes a dangerous ectopic pregnancy.

  1. What two things do the ovaries make?
  2. Where does fertilization usually happen, and where does the baby grow?
  3. What is ovulation?

The Basics of Pregnancy

Pregnancy begins when a sperm and egg join, and ends with birth, a rare example of positive feedback.

The placenta is the exchange organ: the baby gets oxygen and nutrients and gives back waste, all without the two bloodstreams fully mixing. Development goes zygote → embryo (organs form) → fetus (grows and matures).

Tie-in: birth is positive feedback

Labor is one of the body's few positive feedback loops (Module 1). The baby's head stretches the cervix, which triggers oxytocin, which causes stronger contractions, which stretch the cervix more, building until the baby is delivered and the loop shuts off.

Why this matters clinically

The placenta passes many things to the baby, including alcohol, nicotine, and some medications, which is why these are avoided in pregnancy. Conditions like preeclampsia (dangerously high blood pressure) and gestational diabetes connect pregnancy back to the cardiovascular and endocrine systems you have studied all term.

Quick self-check (no peeking first)
  1. What is formed when a sperm and egg join?
  2. What does the placenta do?
  3. Why is birth an example of positive feedback?
