One case. Three panels. Drawn by hand.

This week you build a single three-panel case drawing on plain paper. No printer needed. Panel one is the normal anatomy and physiology, panel two is what goes abnormal in the case, and panel three is the treatment, drawn onto the same pathway so you can see exactly where each treatment acts and how. This screen is your coach: it walks you through each panel one step at a time. Given, not Googled. The learning happens in your hand.

Your case this week

The wheeze that will not quit

Alex, 17, runs track and has asthma. On a smoky wildfire afternoon Alex is halfway through practice when the chest goes tight, a wheeze starts on every breath out, and the air just will not come. By the time the coach calls for help, Alex is speaking in three-word bursts, leaning forward with hands on knees, shoulders lifting with each breath. You will draw this case in three panels, and by the end you will be able to explain it start to finish, including where every treatment plugs in.

How to work it

Draw the three panels in order. Each one is a chapter of Alex's case: normal, then abnormal, then treatment. When you finish you own one hand-made case poster that is worth far more before an exam than any typed notes.

Alex's case · the baseline

Before anything goes wrong, draw how Alex's airways and lungs are supposed to work on a normal training day. This is the normal that every abnormal finding in panel two is measured against.

Field note

Knowing the normal airway cold is why a medic can hear when it is obstructed, and why a rad tech reads a normal chest film before ever reading an abnormal one. This panel is your normal.

Set up your paper: Divide a large sheet into three panels, vertical columns or horizontal rows (or use three sheets). Head the first panel NORMAL. You will build the breathing pathway from the mouth down to the tissues.

Step 1 of 6

  1. Draw the airway tree top to bottom: nose and mouth → pharynx → larynx → trachea (with C-shaped cartilage rings) → bronchi → bronchioles (smooth muscle, no cartilage) → alveoli. Bracket the top part as the conducting zone (moves air) and the alveoli as the respiratory zone (gas exchange).
  2. Draw one inhale: the diaphragm contracts and flattens, the ribs lift, the chest volume increases, and the pressure inside drops below atmospheric, so air flows in. Add an arrow "air in." Note that a normal exhale is passive elastic recoil.
  3. Zoom in on one alveolus with a capillary beside it, separated by the thin respiratory membrane. Draw O2 diffusing from the alveolus into the blood and CO2 diffusing from the blood into the alveolus. Label it "gas exchange by diffusion, high to low partial pressure."
  4. Draw a red blood cell in the capillary picking up oxygen: about 98 percent binds to hemoglobin, a little dissolves in plasma. Add a small oxyhemoglobin curve (an S shape) and label "hemoglobin carries oxygen to the tissues."
  5. Draw a cross-section of a normal bronchiole: open, wide lumen, relaxed smooth muscle, thin wall, no mucus. Label it "open airway equals low resistance, easy airflow." This is the spot panel two will attack.
  6. Write the one-line normal summary: air moves through open airways to the alveoli, oxygen diffuses into the blood and rides hemoglobin to the tissues, and exhaling is passive.

Alex's case · mid-attack

Now it is Alex, mid-attack on the track. Redraw the pathway and mark every place it breaks, with the bronchiole as the center of the story.

Field note

Leaning forward on the knees, using neck and shoulder muscles, and speaking in short bursts are the field signs that an asthma attack is severe. And a wheeze that suddenly goes silent is worse, not better: it can mean too little air is moving to make the sound.

Set up your paper: Head the second panel ABNORMAL. Redraw the pathway spine, then mark the failures in a second color.

Step 1 of 6

  1. Zoom in on a bronchiole and draw the three things happening at once in an asthma attack: the smooth muscle squeezing tight (bronchospasm), the airway wall swelling (inflammation), and mucus plugging the lumen. Label it "airway narrows, resistance climbs."
  2. Show what narrow airways do to airflow: air gets in but is hard to push out, so it gets trapped. Draw the lungs and ribs over-inflated. Label it "obstructive, worst on exhale, FEV1 over FVC drops."
  3. Draw Alex working to breathe: leaning forward, neck and shoulder (accessory) muscles pulling, a wheeze on exhale. Label the wheeze "air squeezing through narrowed airways."
  4. Follow it to the alveolus: less fresh air reaches the gas-exchange surface, so less O2 crosses into the blood and the oxygen saturation starts to fall. Mark the oxyhemoglobin point dropping and label it "hypoxemia if this continues."
  5. Add the trigger: wildfire smoke and allergens set off mast cells, releasing histamine and inflammation that started the whole thing. Label it "the trigger lit the fuse." (This is the same mast cell from the immune passport.)
  6. Write the one-line abnormal summary: the airways constrict, swell, and clog, resistance climbs, air is trapped on exhale, and oxygen delivery falls.

What you are making: you research one first-line drug for this case, show what it does in plain terms, and prove its impact on the pathway with a before-and-after drawing, pictures, not paragraphs.

Alex's case · the fix

Now open Alex's airways. Every treatment works at a specific spot on the pathway you just drew. Show where, and show how.

Field note

Albuterol is the single most-used breathing drug on an ambulance, and wildfire smoke season fills California ERs with exactly this attack. Knowing where each drug acts is knowing why it works.

Set up your paper: Head the third panel TREATMENT. Lightly redraw the bronchiole and the pathway, then attach each treatment where it acts.

Step 1 of 6

  1. Your first-line drug for an asthma attack is the rescue inhaler, a short-acting beta-agonist (albuterol). Write only its name on your panel, then look up in plain terms what it does. Skip the nerdy receptor details.
  2. Capture the mechanism as a plain-language idea, not a paragraph. In three to five words or a small symbol: "relaxes the airway muscle." That is all the text this drug gets.
  3. Draw the drug acting at its exact spot: an arrow from the inhaler to the bronchiole smooth muscle you drew clenched in panel two.
  4. Demonstrate the impact with a picture, not words. Draw the bronchiole twice, side by side: BEFORE (narrow, muscle clenched, a thin trickle of air) and AFTER (wide open, muscle relaxed, a full stream of air), with an arrow between them for the drug. Let the two drawings show the change.
  5. Follow the impact down the pathway with drawing only: with the airway open, show more air reaching the alveoli and the oxygen climbing back up (move the oxyhemoglobin point up). Arrows and marks, no sentences.
  6. The real test. Tell Alex's whole case out loud, and when you reach treatment, say in plain language how albuterol works and point to your before-and-after drawing. Write the single hardest sentence to say from memory, and re-draw that spot.

Using AI on this passport

You may use AI as a study partner: ask it to quiz you after you draw, or to explain a term you are stuck on. You may not ask it to make your panels. There is nothing to type in here on purpose. The whole assignment is the drawing in your own hand and the case you can tell out loud, and those are the two things an app cannot hand back to you photographed on your paper.

Instructor notes

Why this promotes learning. The assignment is one integrated case rendered as three panels: normal, abnormal, and treatment on the pathway. The asthma case forces the student to build normal airway anatomy and breathing physiology first, then locate the obstructive pathophysiology on that same pathway, then map each intervention to its site of action. The final step has them narrate the whole case from their own drawing, so recall is spoken, not typed.

Evidence-based principles built in. Retrieval practice (draw from memory; targets unlock only after the due date), dual coding, sequencing and organization (one continuous pathway from airway to alveolus to blood), comparison (normal versus obstructed airway, rescue versus controller), prediction and application (where does each treatment act), and case-based synthesis (tell Alex start to finish). Anatomy and physiology are balanced across the normal and abnormal panels.

Why AI cannot shortcut it. The submission is a photo of the student's own three panels plus the spoken case. There is no field to paste into, and the answer targets stay hidden and scrambled in the page source until the unlock date, so there is nothing to copy during the assignment window.
