01

A1C

What A1C Actually Measures

Draw

Glucose stuck to hemoglobin

  • A1C = glycated hemoglobin: percent of your hemoglobin molecules with glucose attached
  • Reflects average blood glucose over 3 months (the lifespan of a red blood cell)
  • Cannot be faked by skipping breakfast
  • < 5.7%: normal
  • 5.7-6.4%: prediabetes
  • ≥ 6.5%: diabetes (on 2 separate tests)
  • Anemia, recent transfusion, or sickle cell can throw off the reading

Conversion

A1C → Estimated Average Glucose

Draw

Translate the percent to mg/dL

  • A1C 5.0% ≈ 97 mg/dL average
  • A1C 6.0% ≈ 126 mg/dL average
  • A1C 7.0% ≈ 154 mg/dL average
  • A1C 8.0% ≈ 183 mg/dL average
  • A1C 9.0% ≈ 212 mg/dL average
  • Formula: eAG = (28.7 × A1C) − 46.7
  • Goal for most diabetics: A1C < 7% (some < 6.5%)
  • Each 1% drop in A1C reduces complications ∼ 20-40%

Fasting

Fasting Glucose

Draw

Snapshot after 8 hours without food

  • < 100 mg/dL: normal
  • 100-125: prediabetes (impaired fasting glucose)
  • ≥ 126: diabetes (on 2 separate tests)
  • Measured first thing in the morning before eating
  • Less reliable than A1C: can swing day to day
  • Stress, illness, poor sleep can elevate it
  • A normal fasting glucose does not rule out diabetes: get A1C too

04

High vs low

Symptoms: Hyper- vs Hypoglycemia

Draw

What you feel and why

  • Hyperglycemia (high): excessive thirst, frequent urination, blurred vision, fatigue, slow wound healing
  • Hypoglycemia (low): shakiness, sweating, irritability, confusion, racing heart
  • Severe low (< 54 mg/dL): seizure, loss of consciousness, can be fatal
  • Rule of 15: if low, eat 15g fast carbs (juice, glucose tab), wait 15 min, recheck
  • High = osmotic shift (water follows glucose into urine)
  • Low = brain doesn't get fuel, sympathetic nervous system fires

05

Risk factors

Who Should Get Tested

Concept

Screening recommendations

  • All adults ≥ 35 regardless of risk (USPSTF recommendation)
  • Earlier if: BMI ≥ 25 + risk factor (family history, sedentary, hypertension)
  • Family history of type 2 diabetes (parent or sibling)
  • Gestational diabetes in pregnancy → get tested every 3 years
  • PCOS (polycystic ovary syndrome): higher diabetes risk
  • High-risk ethnicity: Black, Hispanic, Native American, Asian American, Pacific Islander
  • Prior prediabetes: retest yearly
  • Type 1 diabetes presents acutely (usually childhood/teens), not screened the same way
Bottom line: If you have any 2+ risk factors and have never been tested, ask for an A1C at your next visit. It's a single tube of blood, no fasting required. Catching prediabetes early is the biggest single lever you have for preventing type 2 diabetes.
06

Reversing

Reversing Prediabetes

Concept

What actually works

  • Lose 5-7% body weight: cuts diabetes risk 58% over 3 years (Diabetes Prevention Program data)
  • 150 min moderate exercise/week: muscle uses glucose without insulin
  • Cut added sugars and refined carbs: less glucose load, less insulin spike
  • Strength training 2×/week: more muscle = better glucose uptake
  • Sleep 7+ hours: insulin resistance worsens with poor sleep
  • Reduce processed food: ultra-processed foods drive insulin resistance
  • Metformin: if lifestyle alone isn't enough, this is the first-line drug
  • Recheck A1C 3-6 months after starting changes
Bottom line: Prediabetes is reversible for most people. The Diabetes Prevention Program (a 16-session lifestyle coaching program) is covered by Medicare and most insurance, ask your doctor for a referral. Don't wait until you're on insulin to make changes.
