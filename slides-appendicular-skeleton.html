Function

What the Appendicular Skeleton Does

Concept

Movement + manipulation

  • 126 bones in adults
  • Locomotion + lifting + manipulation
  • Pectoral girdle: clavicle + scapula (2 bones × 2 sides)
  • Upper limb: 30 bones per side
  • Pelvic girdle: 2 hip bones (ilium + ischium + pubis fused)
  • Lower limb: 30 bones per side
Takeaway: pectoral girdle is loose and mobile (clavicle is the only bony link to axial). Pelvic girdle is fused and rigid for weight transfer. Mobility vs stability tradeoff.

Upper limb

Arm, Forearm, Hand

Draw

Bones, proximal to distal

  • Humerus arm
  • Radius (lateral) + Ulna (medial)
  • 8 carpals (wrist) in 2 rows of 4
  • 5 metacarpals (palm)
  • 14 phalanges (3 per finger, 2 in thumb)
Draw: an anterior view of the right upper limb from shoulder to fingertips. Label humerus, radius, ulna, mark proximal vs distal carpals, and number the metacarpals 1 to 5 from thumb.
03

Pectoral girdle

Clavicle & Scapula

Concept

The mobile mount

  • Clavicle: S-curved, only bony connection axial to upper limb
  • Most fractured bone in the body
  • Scapula: flat, triangular, floats on thoracic muscles
  • Key landmarks: acromion, coracoid process, glenoid cavity, spine, supra/infraspinous fossae
  • Glenohumeral joint = shallow, very mobile, frequently dislocated
Takeaway: sacrificing stability for range of motion is the design choice of the shoulder. The price is dislocation risk.

Pelvic girdle

Hip Bone & Pelvis

Draw

Three fused bones

  • Ilium superior, flaring
  • Ischium posterior-inferior
  • Pubis anterior-inferior
  • Three fuse at the acetabulum
  • Obturator foramen in the middle
  • Male vs female pelvis differences for childbirth
Draw: an anterior view of the right hip bone. Outline the three component bones (ilium top, ischium back, pubis front) and mark where they meet at the acetabulum.
05

Lower limb

Thigh, Leg, Foot

Draw

Bones, proximal to distal

  • Femur thigh
  • Patella sesamoid in quad tendon
  • Tibia (medial, weight) + Fibula (lateral, stability)
  • 7 tarsals (talus + calcaneus + 5 others)
  • 5 metatarsals
  • 14 phalanges
Draw: an anterior view of the right lower limb. Label femur, patella, tibia (medial), fibula (lateral). Sketch the tarsals as a cluster with talus on top and calcaneus behind. Number metatarsals 1 to 5.
06

Clinical

Fractures & Clinical Hits

Concept

Where things break

  • Clavicle fracture most common
  • Colles fracture distal radius, fall on outstretched hand
  • Scaphoid fracture tricky to detect, risk of avascular necrosis
  • Hip fracture usually femoral neck, common in osteoporosis
  • Boxer fracture 5th metacarpal head, punching injury
  • Pott fracture bimalleolar ankle fracture
Takeaway: name reveals mechanism. Colles = wrist fall, boxer = punch, Pott = ankle eversion. The location of the fracture predicts the activity that caused it.
