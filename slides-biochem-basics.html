Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates

Concept

Sugars and starches

  • Empirical formula (CH₂O)ₙ
  • Monosaccharides: glucose, fructose, galactose (6-carbon hexoses)
  • Disaccharides: sucrose (gluc+fruc), lactose (gluc+gal), maltose (gluc+gluc)
  • Joined by glycosidic bonds via dehydration synthesis; broken by hydrolysis
  • Polysaccharides: starch (plant storage), glycogen (animal storage, branched), cellulose (plant structure, unbranched), chitin (arthropod exoskeleton)
  • Glycogen stored mostly in liver + skeletal muscle
  • Primary function: quick fuel (4 kcal/g), basis of glycosylation tags, components of nucleotides
  • Cell-surface glycoproteins drive cell-cell recognition (ABO blood groups)
Takeaway: glucose is the universal currency of carbohydrate metabolism. Everything else eventually becomes glucose or feeds into glycolysis. That is why blood glucose is so tightly regulated.
02

Lipids

Lipids

Concept

Mostly hydrocarbons, mostly hydrophobic

  • Fatty acids: long hydrocarbon chains with a carboxyl head; saturated (no double bonds) vs unsaturated (one or more)
  • Triglycerides: glycerol + 3 fatty acids; main fat storage form (9 kcal/g)
  • Phospholipids: glycerol + 2 fatty acids + phosphate head; amphipathic, build all membranes
  • Steroids: 4 fused carbon rings; cholesterol is the precursor for sex hormones, cortisol, aldosterone, vitamin D, bile salts
  • Eicosanoids: 20-carbon signaling lipids (prostaglandins, leukotrienes, thromboxanes); target of NSAIDs
  • Lipoproteins: chylomicrons, VLDL, LDL, HDL — package hydrophobic lipids for transport in blood
  • Insulation, cushioning, fat-soluble vitamin storage (A, D, E, K)
Takeaway: lipids do three jobs: fuel storage (triglycerides), membranes (phospholipids), signaling (steroids and eicosanoids). Most fat-related drugs target one of those three roles.

Proteins

Proteins, Four Levels of Structure

Draw

From amino acid to folded function

  • Amino acid: central α-carbon with an amino group, carboxyl group, hydrogen, and an R-group that defines the 20 standard residues
  • R-groups determine charge, polarity, size, and reactivity — they drive folding
  • Peptide bond: covalent link between carboxyl of one residue and amino of the next; formed by dehydration
  • Primary: linear sequence of amino acids
  • Secondary: local folds, mostly α-helix and β-pleated sheet, stabilized by hydrogen bonds in the backbone
  • Tertiary: full 3D fold of one polypeptide, stabilized by R-group interactions (H bonds, ionic, hydrophobic, disulfide)
  • Quaternary: assembly of two or more folded polypeptides (hemoglobin is α₂β₂)
  • Denaturation (heat, pH, salt) unfolds tertiary structure and kills function
  • Roles: enzymes, structural (collagen, keratin), transport (hemoglobin, channels), signaling (hormones, receptors), defense (antibodies), motility (actin, myosin)

Nucleic acids

Nucleic Acids, In Brief

Concept

DNA, RNA, and the energy cousins

  • Nucleotide: pentose sugar + phosphate + nitrogenous base
  • DNA: deoxyribose sugar, double-stranded, bases A T G C (deeper coverage in DNA Structure)
  • RNA: ribose sugar (extra OH on C2), usually single-stranded, U replaces T
  • Three main RNA forms: mRNA (template for protein), tRNA (carries amino acids), rRNA (builds ribosomes)
  • ATP: adenine + ribose + 3 phosphates; the cell's energy currency
  • GTP: energy for protein synthesis + signaling (G-proteins)
  • NAD⁺ / NADH + FAD / FADH₂: electron carriers from the food we eat to the ETC
  • cAMP / cGMP: second messengers downstream of many receptors
Takeaway: nucleotides do three jobs: store genetic information (DNA), carry it out (RNA), and run energy or signaling errands (ATP, NAD, cAMP). Same monomer family, very different roles.

Enzymes

Enzymes

Concept

Biological catalysts

  • Catalyst: speeds up a reaction without being consumed
  • Lower the activation energy, do not change the equilibrium
  • Active site: pocket where substrate binds
  • Induced fit: the active site molds around the substrate (lock + key is the older simpler model)
  • Most enzymes end in -ase and reflect substrate or reaction (lactase, kinase, dehydrogenase)
  • Cofactors: inorganic helpers (Mg²⁺, Zn²⁺); coenzymes: organic (often vitamin-derived: NAD from niacin, FAD from riboflavin)
  • Optimal pH + temperature: pepsin loves pH 2, most body enzymes peak near 37 °C, pH 7.4
  • Inhibition: competitive (binds active site), noncompetitive (binds elsewhere), irreversible (covalent)
  • Allosteric regulation: a separate site flips the enzyme on or off; this is how negative feedback works at the molecular level
Takeaway: life would not run at body temperature without enzymes. The activation-energy hump for most metabolic reactions is too high without a catalyst, which is why even mild fever (40 °C) starts denaturing enzymes and breaking physiology.

ATP

ATP, the Energy Currency

Draw

Adenosine triphosphate

  • Structure: adenine (base) + ribose (sugar) + three phosphate groups
  • High-energy phosphoanhydride bonds: the last two phosphates carry the bulk of usable energy
  • ATP → ADP + Pᵢ releases ~7.3 kcal/mol under standard conditions, more in the cell
  • Coupling: ATP hydrolysis is paired with an unfavorable reaction (active transport, muscle contraction, protein synthesis) to make it go
  • Regeneration: cellular respiration phosphorylates ADP back to ATP
  • Three ways cells regenerate ATP: substrate-level (glycolysis, Krebs), oxidative phosphorylation (ETC + ATP synthase), creatine phosphate (muscle, very fast burst)
  • Daily turnover: a resting adult cycles through roughly their own body weight in ATP each day
  • Other nucleotide energy molecules: GTP (proteins, G-proteins), UTP (carbs), CTP (lipids)
