01

Depth

Burn Depth

Draw

How far into the skin does the burn reach

  • 1° (superficial / epidermal): epidermis only; red, dry, painful, no blisters; typical sunburn; heals in 3 to 7 days
  • 2° superficial partial-thickness: into papillary dermis; red, very painful, weeping, blisters; heals in 7 to 21 days, minimal scarring
  • 2° deep partial-thickness: into reticular dermis; mottled red/white, less pain (some nerve endings destroyed), slower cap refill; may need grafting; 3 to 8 weeks to heal
  • 3° (full thickness): through the entire dermis into subcutaneous fat; white, leathery, or charred; painless (nerves destroyed); does not re-epithelialize without grafting
  • 4°: through skin into muscle, tendon, or bone; surgical, may need amputation
  • Why partial-thickness hurts more than full-thickness: enough nerves survive to fire; in 3° they are all gone
  • Healing source: epidermal regeneration in 1° + superficial 2°, dermal appendages (hair follicles, glands) in deep 2°; 3° + 4° need a graft

02

BSA

Body Surface Area, Rule of Nines + Lund-Browder

Draw

How much surface is burned

  • Rule of Nines (adult): head 9%, each arm 9%, anterior trunk 18%, posterior trunk 18%, each leg 18%, perineum 1%
  • Fast at the scene; only count 2° and 3° burns, not 1° (sunburn)
  • Rule of Nines (pediatric, infant): head 18%, each leg 14% (proportionally bigger head, smaller legs)
  • Lund-Browder chart: gives more accurate, age-adjusted BSA; preferred in burn centers and for children
  • Palmar method (rule of palm): the patient's own palm + closed fingers ~1% TBSA; useful for irregular or patchy burns
  • %TBSA drives resuscitation, severity classification, and referral decisions

03

Parkland

Parkland Formula

Draw

Fluid resuscitation for major burns

  • Indication: adults with ≥20% TBSA partial- or full-thickness burns (some centers use 15%); pediatric thresholds lower
  • Formula: 4 mL × patient weight (kg) × %TBSA = total volume in first 24 h
  • Fluid: lactated Ringer's (LR)
  • Timing: ½ in first 8 hours, ½ over next 16 hours
  • Time clock starts at the moment of injury, not arrival
  • Titrate to urine output: 0.5 mL/kg/h in adults, 1 mL/kg/h in children; formula is a starting estimate not a fixed recipe
  • Worked example: 70 kg adult with 40% TBSA
  • 4 × 70 × 40 = 11,200 mL total over 24 h
  • First 8 h: 5,600 mL → ~700 mL/h
  • Next 16 h: 5,600 mL → ~350 mL/h
  • Modified Brooke (2 × kg × %TBSA) is the lower-volume alternative — many burn centers now use less fluid than original Parkland to avoid over-resuscitation ('fluid creep')

04

Special cases

Inhalation, Electrical, Chemical Burns

Concept

Cases that don't follow the rules

  • Inhalation injury: suspected with facial burns, singed nasal hair, soot in sputum, hoarseness, stridor, closed-space exposure
  • Doubles mortality at any given %TBSA; threshold for intubation should be low because airway edema can occlude quickly
  • Carbon monoxide: classic from house fires, binds hemoglobin ~200x stronger than O₂, pulse ox can read normal — use co-oximetry; treat with 100% O₂
  • Cyanide: from burning plastics, blocks ETC complex IV; treat with hydroxocobalamin
  • Electrical burns: small entry + exit wounds hide massive internal injury; risk of rhabdomyolysis, cardiac arrhythmia, compartment syndrome
  • Resuscitate to higher urine output (1 to 2 mL/kg/h) until myoglobin clears
  • Chemical burns: copious water irrigation for 20+ min for most; do not neutralize acid with base (exothermic, more injury)
  • Tar burns: cool first, then dissolve with mineral oil or petroleum jelly
  • Circumferential burns: leathery eschar can act as a tourniquet; need escharotomy
Takeaway: the BSA + Parkland approach works for thermal burns. Electrical, chemical, and inhalation injuries each break the formula in their own way. Mortality goes up the most when you miss an inhalation injury, so the airway is always question one.
05

Disposition

Burn Center Referral Criteria

Concept

Who gets transferred

  • Partial-thickness burns ≥10% TBSA
  • Any full-thickness burn
  • Burns to face, hands, feet, genitalia, perineum, or major joints (cosmetic + functional consequences)
  • Inhalation injury (any extent)
  • Electrical burns, including lightning
  • Chemical burns
  • Circumferential burns of limb or chest
  • Pediatric burns at a hospital without pediatric burn capability
  • Major trauma alongside the burn (stabilize the trauma first, then transfer)
  • Significant pre-existing conditions (diabetes, immunocompromise, etc.) that complicate management
  • Suspected non-accidental injury (immersion lines, stocking-glove pattern, multiple ages of injury) — mandatory reporting + protective service involvement
Takeaway: the American Burn Association lists 10+ specific referral criteria. The simplest mental check is: any burn that affects function (face, hands, joints, airway), any deep burn, any chemical or electrical mechanism, and any case where home dressing changes are not going to work. When in doubt, call the burn center.
