01

Energy use

BMR, RMR, and Total Energy Expenditure

Concept

How fast you burn at rest and active

  • Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR): minimum energy at complete rest, awake, post-absorptive, thermoneutral; ~60-75% of TEE
  • Resting Metabolic Rate (RMR): slightly higher than BMR, easier to measure, used clinically
  • Diet-Induced Thermogenesis (DIT): energy used digesting + absorbing food, ~10% of TEE
  • Activity thermogenesis: exercise + NEAT (non-exercise activity); most variable, 15-30%+ of TEE
  • Adult BMR estimate: ~1 kcal/kg/h (so a 70 kg adult ≈ 1,680 kcal/day at rest)
  • Mifflin-St Jeor equation for adult BMR: men = 10(kg) + 6.25(cm) − 5(age) + 5; women = same − 161
  • What raises BMR: muscle mass, fever (~13% per °C), thyroid hormone, growth phases, pregnancy, cold exposure
  • What lowers BMR: aging (~2%/decade), sarcopenia, hypothyroidism, severe caloric restriction
Takeaway: BMR is set mostly by lean body mass. Muscle costs energy 24/7, fat is metabolically quieter, which is why two people of the same weight can have very different daily calorie needs.
02

Building

Anabolism: Protein, Glycogen, Fat

Draw

Where calories go when you have enough

  • Anabolism: synthesis of complex molecules, requires energy
  • Protein: amino acids → polypeptides; built constantly, ~250 g/day turned over in an adult
  • Glycogen: glucose polymer stored mostly in liver (~100 g) and skeletal muscle (~400 g); short-term carb buffer
  • Fat (triglycerides): virtually unlimited storage, ~7,700 kcal/kg
  • Hormonal drivers of anabolism:
  • Insulin: dominant fed-state hormone — pushes glucose into cells, glycogen + fat synthesis, amino acid uptake
  • Growth hormone (GH): from anterior pituitary; stimulates IGF-1 from liver, drives long-bone growth (before plate closure) and tissue building
  • IGF-1 (somatomedin C): mediates most of GH's growth effects
  • Sex hormones (testosterone, estrogen): drive pubertal anabolism + bone density
  • Thyroid hormone: permissive — needed for normal growth and metabolism but is not itself an anabolic driver
Draw: a fed liver cell receiving glucose + amino acids. Show insulin docking to its receptor, glucose entering via GLUT2 then being polymerized into glycogen, amino acids being assembled on ribosomes into protein, and excess glucose being shunted into lipogenesis. Label each pathway.
03

Burning

Catabolism: Glycolysis, Krebs, ETC

Draw

Where fuel goes when cells need ATP

  • Catabolism: breakdown of complex molecules, releases energy + reducing equivalents (NADH, FADH₂)
  • Glycolysis: cytosol, anaerobic; glucose → 2 pyruvate; net +2 ATP, +2 NADH
  • Pyruvate → acetyl-CoA (mitochondrial matrix): +1 NADH, releases CO₂
  • Krebs (citric acid) cycle: 2 turns per glucose; per turn: +3 NADH, +1 FADH₂, +1 GTP, 2 CO₂
  • Electron transport chain + oxidative phosphorylation (inner mitochondrial membrane): NADH and FADH₂ drop electrons through complexes I–IV, pumping H+ to create a gradient; ATP synthase uses that gradient to make ATP
  • Total ATP from 1 glucose: ~30-32 ATP (modern count)
  • Without O₂: pyruvate → lactate; regenerates NAD+ for glycolysis; only 2 ATP/glucose
  • Fat catabolism: β-oxidation feeds acetyl-CoA into Krebs; yields more ATP per gram (9 vs 4 kcal/g)
  • Hormonal drivers of catabolism: glucagon (fasting), epinephrine (acute stress), cortisol (chronic stress), thyroid hormone (sets metabolic tone)

Switching states

Fed, Postabsorptive, and Fasting States

Concept

The hourly hormonal pendulum

  • Fed (absorptive) state, 0-4 h after meal: insulin high, glucagon low; glucose absorbed, used directly, or stored; fat synthesis active
  • Postabsorptive state, 4-12 h: insulin falls, glucagon rises; liver glycogen breaks down to maintain blood glucose; brain still uses glucose
  • Early fasting, 12-24 h: liver glycogen gone (~24 h); gluconeogenesis from amino acids + glycerol + lactate becomes the main glucose source
  • Prolonged fasting, >24-72 h: fat becomes dominant fuel; ketogenesis ramps up; brain shifts to using ketone bodies + glucose
  • Starvation, >1 week: protein sparing kicks in; basal energy drops to conserve lean mass; ketones supply ~75% of brain energy
  • Driver hormones: insulin = fed; glucagon = fasting; cortisol + GH = prolonged fasting; epinephrine = acute stress regardless of state
  • RQ (respiratory quotient): 1.0 pure carb, ~0.7 pure fat, ~0.8 mixed; lab clue to substrate use
Takeaway: the body uses different fuels at different times, and the switch is hormonal. Diabetes (uncontrolled insulin) and starvation look surprisingly similar at the cell level — both shift toward fat + ketones because glucose is unavailable to cells, either by lack of insulin or by lack of food.
05

Hormonal hierarchy

Master Hormones of Growth and Metabolism

Concept

Who controls what

  • Growth hormone (GH): anterior pituitary; drives growth via IGF-1 in childhood; in adults — anabolic + lipolytic, opposes insulin
  • Excess: gigantism (before plate closure), acromegaly (after closure)
  • Deficit: pituitary dwarfism in kids, decreased lean mass in adults
  • Thyroid hormone (T3, T4): sets the metabolic tempo of nearly every cell; permissive for GH action; needed for normal growth and CNS development
  • Hyperthyroid: heat intolerance, weight loss despite appetite, tachycardia, tremor
  • Hypothyroid: cold intolerance, weight gain, bradycardia, fatigue, depression
  • Insulin: fed-state anabolism — glucose uptake, glycogen + fat + protein synthesis; the only hormone that lowers blood glucose
  • Glucagon: fasting catabolism — glycogenolysis, gluconeogenesis, ketogenesis
  • Cortisol: chronic stress + diurnal rhythm; raises blood glucose by gluconeogenesis and insulin resistance; immunosuppressive long-term
  • Epinephrine + norepinephrine: acute stress; fast glycogenolysis, lipolysis, raise HR + BP
  • Leptin + ghrelin: appetite signals — leptin from adipose says full, ghrelin from stomach says hungry
Takeaway: a few hormones do most of the heavy lifting. Insulin and glucagon run hour-by-hour. GH and thyroid set the longer arc. Cortisol and catecholamines respond to threat. Understanding metabolic disease usually comes down to which of these is over- or underactive.
