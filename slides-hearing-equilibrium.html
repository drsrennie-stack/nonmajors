Anatomy

Three Regions of the Ear

Concept

Outer, middle, inner

  • Outer ear: auricle + external auditory canal + tympanic membrane
  • Middle ear: tympanic cavity with 3 ossicles (malleus, incus, stapes)
  • Eustachian (auditory) tube: middle ear ↔ pharynx, pressure equalization
  • Inner ear: bony + membranous labyrinth
  • Cochlea (hearing), vestibule + semicircular canals (equilibrium)
  • Innervation: CN VIII vestibulocochlear
Takeaway: ossicles amplify sound ~22x as they transfer from tympanic membrane (big, low-impedance air) to oval window (small, high-impedance fluid). Without them, most sound would reflect off the fluid.

Cochlea

Cochlear Anatomy

Draw

Three fluid-filled ducts

  • Scala vestibuli (top, perilymph)
  • Cochlear duct (middle, endolymph) — has organ of Corti
  • Scala tympani (bottom, perilymph)
  • Basilar membrane separates cochlear duct from scala tympani
  • Tectorial membrane above hair cells
  • Hair cells: inner (sensory) + outer (amplification)
  • Helicotrema: scala vestibuli meets scala tympani at apex
Draw: a cross-section through one turn of the cochlea. Show three ducts stacked. Label scala vestibuli (top), cochlear duct (middle, with organ of Corti on basilar membrane), scala tympani (bottom). Add tectorial membrane over hair cells.
03

Transduction

How Sound Becomes Signal

Concept

Hair cell mechanotransduction

  • Stapes pushes on oval window, creates pressure wave in scala vestibuli
  • Wave travels through perilymph, deflects basilar membrane
  • Frequency tonotopy: high pitch = base, low pitch = apex
  • Basilar deflection bends stereocilia against tectorial membrane
  • Tip links open K⁺ channels (yes, K⁺ in here because endolymph)
  • Hair cell depolarizes, releases glutamate onto CN VIII fiber
Takeaway: this is the only place in the body where K⁺ entering the cell depolarizes it. That works because endolymph has very high K⁺ + positive endocochlear potential, so K⁺ rushes IN when channels open.

Pathway

Auditory Pathway

Draw

Cochlea to cortex

  • Cochlear nerve (CN VIII) → cochlear nuclei (medulla)
  • Most fibers cross at superior olive (allows sound localization)
  • Lateral lemniscus → inferior colliculus (midbrain)
  • Medial geniculate nucleus (MGN) of thalamus
  • Primary auditory cortex on superior temporal gyrus
  • Bilateral representation means unilateral cortex lesion does NOT cause deafness
Draw: a side view of the auditory pathway. Cochlea → CN VIII → cochlear nuclei → superior olive → lateral lemniscus → inferior colliculus → MGN → auditory cortex. Use one continuous line with each station labeled.

Equilibrium

Vestibular System

Concept

Static + dynamic

  • Utricle + saccule (in vestibule): linear acceleration + head position
  • Contain maculae with otoliths (calcium carbonate crystals on gel)
  • 3 semicircular canals (anterior, posterior, lateral): angular acceleration
  • Each has a crista ampullaris with cupula
  • Endolymph lag deflects cupula when head rotates
  • All vestibular signals → CN VIII → vestibular nuclei → cerebellum + eye muscles + cortex
Takeaway: gravity pulls otoliths, which lag behind head movement and bend hair cells. Same mechanism for cupula in semicircular canals, but with endolymph fluid lag instead of crystals.
06

Clinical

Ear Disorders

Concept

Common hits

  • Otitis media middle ear infection, common in kids (short Eustachian tube)
  • Conductive hearing loss outer/middle ear problem (wax, fluid, otosclerosis)
  • Sensorineural hearing loss inner ear or CN VIII (presbycusis, noise damage)
  • Tinnitus ringing without external source
  • Vertigo sense of motion when still
  • BPPV displaced otoliths
  • Méniere disease endolymph fluid imbalance: vertigo + hearing loss + tinnitus
Takeaway: Rinne + Weber tuning fork tests differentiate conductive from sensorineural loss. Rinne: AC > BC normally. Weber: midline normally. The combination localizes the lesion.
