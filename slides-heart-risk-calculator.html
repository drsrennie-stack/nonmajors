What it does

What 10-Year Risk Actually Means

Draw

Population probability, not certainty

  • Calculator estimates: chance of heart attack or stroke in the next 10 years
  • Based on data from large population studies (Framingham, ARIC, others)
  • Output: a percent (e.g., "12% 10-year risk")
  • Doesn't predict YOUR future, predicts the average for people who look like you
  • Two people with same risk score can have very different outcomes
  • Useful for decision-making about meds, not fortune-telling
  • Recalculate every 4-6 years (sooner if numbers change)
Draw: two figures: a single person with a question mark above (you), and 100 stick figures (people like you). Color 12 of the 100 red (heart event in 10 years), 88 green. Caption: "12% risk means 12 of 100 similar people will have an event, but we can't tell WHICH 12."

Inputs

What Feeds the Calculator

Draw

The eight numbers

  • Age (40-79 for ASCVD): biggest single driver
  • Sex: men higher baseline risk
  • Race: Black vs other (calculator adjusts; imperfect)
  • Total cholesterol (mg/dL)
  • HDL cholesterol (mg/dL)
  • Systolic blood pressure (mmHg)
  • On BP medication? (yes/no)
  • Diabetes? (yes/no)
  • Smoker? (current yes/no)
Draw: a worksheet-style form with 8 lines, one per input, with your own numbers filled in. Then a box at the bottom showing the resulting risk percent.

Categories

Risk Categories & Actions

Draw

What your number unlocks

  • Low (< 5%): lifestyle focus, no statin unless other concerns
  • Borderline (5-7.5%): consider statin if risk-enhancers (family history, high LDL ≥ 160, chronic kidney disease)
  • Intermediate (7.5-20%): statin recommended; aim 30-49% LDL reduction
  • High (≥ 20%): statin recommended; aim ≥ 50% LDL reduction
  • Lower threshold if: family history of premature CAD, LDL ≥ 190, inflammatory disease
  • Coronary calcium scan (CAC) can refine borderline/intermediate decisions
  • Aspirin is no longer routine: bleeding risk often outweighs benefit unless history of MI/stroke
Draw: a horizontal bar split into 4 colored zones (green low, yellow borderline, orange intermediate, red high) with the percent thresholds marked. Place your own 10-year risk percent on the bar with an arrow. Below, write what's recommended at your level.

Limits

What the Calculator Misses

Concept

Important factors not in the formula

  • Family history of early heart disease (parent/sibling MI before 55/65)
  • LDL ≥ 160 or apolipoprotein B elevation
  • Coronary artery calcium score: most powerful refinement available
  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Inflammatory conditions: rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, HIV
  • Pre-eclampsia, premature menopause (women)
  • South Asian ancestry: higher risk than calculator suggests
  • Lipoprotein(a): genetic risk factor not in standard calculator
  • Sleep apnea, sedentary lifestyle, poor diet, stress: all matter, all missed
Bottom line: The calculator is a starting point, not the final word. If your number is borderline or intermediate, ask your doctor about a coronary artery calcium scan (~$100, often not covered by insurance but worth it). A score of 0 strongly suggests you can skip the statin; a score > 100 strongly argues for one.
05

Lifestyle

How Much You Can Move The Number

Concept

Real-world risk reduction

  • Quit smoking: risk drops ~50% within 1 year
  • Lose 5-10% body weight: better BP, lipids, glucose
  • Mediterranean diet: ~30% risk reduction (PREDIMED trial)
  • 150 min moderate exercise/week: ~30% risk reduction
  • Sleep 7-9 hours: < 6 or > 9 raises cardiovascular risk 12-30%
  • Stress management: meditation, social connection, therapy
  • Treat hypertension: each 10 mmHg systolic drop = ~20% lower stroke risk
  • Statin (if indicated): ~25-35% relative risk reduction
Bottom line: You can usually move your risk number 30-50% with lifestyle alone if you stack the changes. The biggest individual lever is quitting smoking. The biggest combined lever is the Mediterranean diet plus regular exercise. Recheck the calculator after 6-12 months of changes.
