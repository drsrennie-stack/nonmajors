01

Definition

Muscle Tissue: What It Does

Concept

Two defining properties

  • Excitable: responds to a stimulus
  • Contractile: shortens, generates pull
  • Three structural variants: skeletal, cardiac, smooth
  • ~40% of body mass is skeletal muscle
  • Generates heat (thermal homeostasis)
  • Develops from mesoderm, from myoblasts
Takeaway: every muscle tissue does the same thing (shorten to pull) using the same machinery (actin + myosin). What changes is who controls it and how the cells are organized.
02

Classification

Three Muscle Types

Reference

Skeletal, cardiac, smooth

  • Skeletal voluntary
  • Long cylindrical fibers, striated, many peripheral nuclei. Attached to bone.
  • Cardiac involuntary
  • Short, branched, striated, single central nucleus. Intercalated discs. Heart only.
  • Smooth involuntary
  • Short spindle-shaped, no striation, single nucleus. Walls of organs and vessels.
Key idea: nucleus count and position is the fastest histology tell. Many peripheral nuclei = skeletal. One central nucleus + striations = cardiac. One central nucleus + no striations = smooth.

Switch tissues

Nervous Tissue: What It Does

Concept

Two cell classes

  • Neurons conduct
  • Excitable. Send action potentials. Release neurotransmitters at synapses.
  • Neuroglia (glia) support
  • Non-conducting. Insulate, defend, regulate environment, form blood-brain barrier.
  • Both derive from ectoderm (except microglia)
  • Synapse = gap where signal crosses by chemical messenger
Takeaway: only neurons signal. The glia are the infrastructure that makes signaling possible: myelin, nutrient supply, ionic balance, immune defense.
05

Neuron anatomy

Parts of a Neuron

Draw

Three regions, one direction

  • Dendrites receive
  • Cell body (soma) integrate
  • Contains nucleus, organelles
  • Axon transmit
  • Single long process, may be myelinated
  • Ends at synapse
  • Multipolar / bipolar / unipolar based on process count
Draw: a multipolar neuron. Many short branching dendrites at the top, round soma with nucleus in middle, one long axon going down with myelin segments and a synaptic terminal at the end. Arrow shows signal direction.

Support cells + clinical

Glia & Clinical Relevance

Draw

Four glia, three clinical hits

  • Astrocyte CNS
  • Blood-brain barrier, ion balance
  • Oligodendrocyte CNS
  • Myelin in brain and spinal cord
  • Schwann cell PNS
  • Myelin in peripheral nerves
  • Microglia immune
  • Clinical: MS (CNS demyelination), muscular dystrophy, myasthenia gravis
