01

Trigger

Neuromuscular Junction (NMJ)

Concept

From nerve to muscle

  • Motor neuron reaches muscle, axon terminal enlarges into a synaptic knob
  • ACh (acetylcholine) released into the synaptic cleft
  • ACh binds nicotinic ACh receptors on motor end plate
  • Na⁺ rushes in, sarcolemma depolarizes
  • End plate potential triggers action potential along sarcolemma
  • AChE in cleft breaks down ACh to stop the signal
Takeaway: one motor neuron + all the fibers it innervates = one motor unit. Fine control (eye muscles) = few fibers per neuron. Power (quadriceps) = many fibers per neuron.
02

E-C coupling

Excitation-Contraction Coupling

Draw

Electrical to chemical

  • Action potential races along sarcolemma
  • AP dives into T-tubules
  • Voltage sensor (DHP receptor) opens ryanodine receptor on SR
  • Ca²⁺ floods sarcoplasm from SR
  • Ca²⁺ binds troponin C
  • Tropomyosin shifts off actin binding sites
  • Cross-bridges can now form
Draw: a triad in cross-section: T-tubule flanked by two terminal cisternae. Arrows show AP traveling down the T-tubule, triggering Ca²⁺ release into the sarcoplasm. Label DHP receptor and ryanodine receptor.
03

Theory

The Sliding Filament Theory

Concept

Filaments slide, sarcomeres shorten

  • Filaments themselves do not shrink
  • Thick + thin filaments slide past each other
  • Z-discs pull toward M-line
  • I band and H zone shrink
  • A band stays the same length
  • Whole sarcomere shortens, whole fiber shortens, whole muscle shortens
Takeaway: this is the load-bearing concept. The myosin heads walk along actin like a ratchet. Many heads pulling on many filaments adds up to whole-muscle force.
04

Mechanism

Cross-Bridge Cycle

Draw

Four steps, repeat

  • 1. Cross-bridge formation: myosin head binds actin
  • 2. Power stroke: head pivots, pulls thin filament toward M-line; ADP + Pi released
  • 3. Cross-bridge detachment: new ATP binds myosin head, head releases
  • 4. Cocking: ATP hydrolyzed (ATP → ADP + Pi), head re-cocks to high-energy position
  • Cycle repeats as long as Ca²⁺ and ATP are present
Draw: a 4-panel cartoon of the cross-bridge cycle. Panel 1 (bind), 2 (power stroke arrow), 3 (release), 4 (cock back). Use myosin head + actin filament in each panel. Label ATP / ADP+Pi at each step.
05

Relaxation

Stopping the Contraction

Draw

Reverse the chain

  • Motor neuron stops firing
  • AChE chews up remaining ACh
  • SR pumps Ca²⁺ back in (SERCA, costs ATP)
  • Troponin releases Ca²⁺
  • Tropomyosin re-covers actin sites
  • Cross-bridges cannot form
  • Rigor mortis: no ATP = no detachment, muscles stay locked
Draw: three side-by-side panels. Left = active (Ca²⁺ high, cross-bridges cycling). Middle = SERCA pumping Ca²⁺ back into SR. Right = relaxed (Ca²⁺ low, tropomyosin blocking sites). Arrows show Ca²⁺ direction.
06

Clinical

When the System Breaks

Concept

Real-world failures

  • Myasthenia gravis autoantibodies against ACh receptors → weak contraction
  • Botulinum toxin blocks ACh release → flaccid paralysis (Botox)
  • Curare blocks ACh receptors → paralysis (arrow poison)
  • Organophosphates inhibit AChE → too much ACh, spastic paralysis
  • Malignant hyperthermia RYR1 mutation causes runaway Ca²⁺ release with certain anesthetics
  • Rigor mortis appears in 2-6 hrs, resolves as proteins degrade
Takeaway: the NMJ is the most pharmacologically targeted synapse in the body. Surgery uses it (paralytics), cosmetics use it (Botox), poisons exploit it. Know where each drug acts.
