Structure

How Smooth Muscle is Built

Concept

Different from skeletal at every level

  • Small spindle-shaped cells, single central nucleus
  • No sarcomeres, so no striations — actin + myosin arranged in oblique networks anchored at dense bodies (analog of Z-discs)
  • Cell shortens by ~80% (vs ~30% for skeletal) — useful for sphincters and hollow organs
  • No T-tubules; caveolae + relatively scarce sarcoplasmic reticulum handle Ca²⁺ entry instead
  • Ca²⁺ comes from both extracellular space + SR (skeletal relies almost entirely on SR)
  • Gap junctions in single-unit smooth muscle: electrical coupling lets a wave of contraction sweep across many cells
  • Long, slow contractions with low ATP cost
  • No troponin — Ca²⁺ binds calmodulin instead
  • Innervated by autonomic nervous system, often varicosities releasing NT diffusely (not point-to-point like NMJ)
Takeaway: smooth muscle is built for slow, sustained, energy-cheap contraction over wide tissue areas. That makes it perfect for blood vessels and gut, where pressure or tone needs to be maintained for hours, not seconds.
02

Activation

Calmodulin, MLCK, and the Latch State

Draw

Different from troponin

  • Step 1: stimulus opens Ca²⁺ channels (voltage- or ligand-gated); Ca²⁺ enters cytosol
  • Step 2: Ca²⁺ binds calmodulin (CaM)
  • Step 3: Ca²⁺-CaM activates myosin light chain kinase (MLCK)
  • Step 4: MLCK phosphorylates the myosin light chain → myosin head can now bind actin + cycle
  • Step 5: cross-bridge cycling begins; smooth muscle contracts
  • Relaxation: Ca²⁺ pumped back out; myosin light chain phosphatase dephosphorylates myosin; cross-bridges stop
  • Latch state: dephosphorylated cross-bridges sometimes stay attached at low cycling rate → sustained tension at very low ATP cost
  • Latch is why an artery can hold tone for hours without fatiguing
  • Pharmacology pearls: Ca²⁺ channel blockers (amlodipine) relax vascular smooth muscle; β-agonists raise cAMP → activate MLCK phosphatase → bronchodilation in airways
Draw: the smooth-muscle activation pathway as a flowchart. Start: stimulus → Ca²⁺ enters cell. Ca²⁺ binds CaM. Ca²⁺-CaM activates MLCK. MLCK phosphorylates myosin. Myosin binds actin + cycles. Add a side path: myosin light chain phosphatase removes phosphate → relaxation. Label the latch state as a sustained but low-cycling attached cross-bridge.

Two types

Single-Unit vs Multi-Unit Smooth Muscle

Draw

Two organizations, two behaviors

  • Single-unit (visceral) smooth muscle: cells electrically coupled by gap junctions; AP spreads as a wave; contracts as one functional syncytium
  • Found in: most of GI tract, uterus, ureters, small blood vessels
  • Pacemaker cells (interstitial cells of Cajal in gut) generate spontaneous slow waves
  • Stretch-sensitive: distention triggers contraction (basis of peristalsis)
  • Multi-unit smooth muscle: cells largely independent; each gets its own innervation; no spreading wave
  • Found in: iris of the eye, ciliary muscle, large blood vessels (arrector pili of skin)
  • Finer control: each cell or small group can be activated separately
  • Both types respond to: autonomic nerve activity, circulating hormones, local chemistry (O₂, CO₂, pH, metabolites), mechanical stretch
  • Both lack troponin, use CaM-MLCK pathway

Right: 5-6 cells each with its own nerve terminal contacting it, no gap junctions, cells contracting independently. Label two examples for each type.
04

Control

What Drives Smooth Muscle

Concept

Autonomic, hormonal, local, mechanical

  • Sympathetic + parasympathetic: usually opposite effects, but the same NT can excite or inhibit depending on receptor subtype
  • Example, vascular smooth muscle: NE on α1 → vasoconstriction; NE on β2 → vasodilation (skeletal muscle vessels in fight-or-flight)
  • Example, GI smooth muscle: parasympathetic increases motility; sympathetic suppresses
  • Hormones: oxytocin (uterus, labor), histamine (bronchoconstriction in asthma), angiotensin II (potent vasoconstrictor), prostaglandins, vasopressin (ADH)
  • Local factors: in arterioles, local hypoxia + CO₂ + lactate + adenosine cause vasodilation (this matches blood flow to metabolic demand)
  • Endothelial signals: NO + prostacyclin from healthy endothelium dilate; endothelin constricts
  • Stretch: myogenic response — vascular smooth muscle contracts when stretched, helps autoregulate organ blood flow
  • Clinical relevance: most cardiovascular drugs (Ca²⁺ blockers, β-blockers, ACE inhibitors, ARBs, nitrates) work by modifying smooth muscle tone in arterioles + venules
Takeaway: smooth muscle integrates input from at least four channels — autonomic nerves, circulating hormones, local metabolic cues, and mechanical stretch — and produces a graded response. That integration is what makes it so much more drug-target-rich than skeletal muscle.
