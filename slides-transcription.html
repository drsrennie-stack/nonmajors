01

Initiation

Promoter and RNA Polymerase

Concept

Finding the start

  • Promoter: DNA sequence just upstream of a gene that tells the cell where to start
  • TATA box: classic core promoter element ~25 bp upstream of the start site
  • Transcription factors assemble at the promoter and recruit RNA polymerase
  • RNA polymerase II: makes mRNA in eukaryotes
  • RNA pol I makes rRNA; RNA pol III makes tRNA + small RNAs
  • No primer needed (unlike DNA replication)
  • Template strand: the strand RNA polymerase reads, 3' to 5'
  • Coding (sense) strand: the other strand, same sequence as the mRNA (with U for T)
  • Bacterial difference: single RNA polymerase, sigma factor + Pribnow box (-10), no nucleus so transcription + translation are coupled
Takeaway: the cell does not waste energy transcribing everything. Tissue-specific transcription factors are what make a liver cell different from a neuron, even though they share the same genome. Drugs like glucocorticoids work by changing which transcription factors are active.
02

Elongation

RNA Synthesis

Draw

Reading and writing

  • RNA polymerase moves 3' to 5' along the template strand
  • It builds the new RNA 5' to 3', adding one ribonucleotide at a time
  • Base pairing: A in template → U in RNA, T → A, G → C, C → G
  • Transcription bubble: ~10 to 14 bp of DNA are open at any moment
  • Speed: ~20 to 50 nucleotides per second in eukaryotes
  • Multiple RNA polymerases can transcribe the same gene at once (visible as a 'Christmas tree' on EM)
  • Proofreading: minimal compared to DNA replication; errors are tolerated because RNA is short-lived
  • Antibiotics: rifampin blocks bacterial RNA polymerase (treats TB)
  • Toxins: α-amanitin (death-cap mushroom) blocks RNA Pol II in humans, lethal
Draw: a transcription bubble. Show the DNA double helix opening at the RNA polymerase, with the template strand labeled (3' to 5' reading direction), an RNA strand growing 5' to 3' off the template, and three labeled base pairs (one A-U, one T-A, one G-C in the new RNA-DNA hybrid).
03

Termination

Termination of Transcription

Concept

Where to stop

  • Eukaryotic mRNA termination: the polymerase reads past a polyadenylation signal (AAUAAA in the RNA)
  • A complex cleaves the new RNA ~10 to 30 bases downstream of the signal
  • Polymerase eventually falls off ('torpedo' or 'allosteric' models)
  • Bacterial termination, rho-independent: a hairpin loop in the new RNA causes polymerase to release
  • Bacterial termination, rho-dependent: rho protein chases the polymerase down the RNA and pulls it off
  • After release, the pre-mRNA is still inside the nucleus and is not yet a mature mRNA
  • It cannot leave the nucleus until processing is done
Takeaway: the cell does not let an unfinished mRNA leave the nucleus. The 5' cap, splicing, and poly-A tail all have to be in place first, which is what the next slide covers.
04

Processing

mRNA Processing

Draw

Cap, splice, tail

  • 5' cap: a methylated guanine added to the 5' end almost immediately during transcription
  • Cap functions: ribosome recognition, protection from 5' exonucleases, nuclear export tag
  • Splicing: introns (non-coding) cut out, exons (coding) joined
  • Done by the spliceosome: large RNA-protein complex containing snRNPs (small nuclear ribonucleoproteins)
  • Splice sites: GU at the 5' end of an intron, AG at the 3' end (GU-AG rule)
  • Alternative splicing: a single gene can make several proteins by combining exons differently — this is why humans have ~20,000 genes but ~100,000 protein variants
  • 3' poly-A tail: ~100 to 250 adenines added by poly-A polymerase
  • Tail functions: stability + ribosome binding + translation efficiency
  • Tail shortens over time and that is one trigger for mRNA degradation
  • Mature mRNA is then exported through nuclear pores into the cytoplasm
  • Clinical: many genetic diseases come from splicing errors (β-thalassemia, some forms of muscular dystrophy)
