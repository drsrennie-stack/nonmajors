Mechanism

How Vaccines Actually Work

Draw

Training the immune system

  • Vaccine introduces a harmless piece of a pathogen (protein, weakened virus, or mRNA blueprint)
  • Antigen-presenting cells show this to T cells in lymph nodes
  • B cells make antibodies against it
  • Memory cells form: live for years to decades
  • On real exposure, memory cells respond fast + strong, often stopping infection before symptoms
  • Side effects (sore arm, fatigue, low fever) = your immune system responding, NOT the disease
  • Vaccine cannot "give you" the disease for inactivated/subunit/mRNA types
Draw: a flowchart: vaccine injected → antigen-presenting cell → lymph node → B cells make antibodies + T cells activated → memory cells form → 6 months later, real virus arrives → memory cells respond fast → infection stopped early.

Schedule

What Every Adult Should Have

Draw

The baseline schedule

  • Tdap: once as adult, then Td booster every 10 years (tetanus + diphtheria + pertussis)
  • Influenza (flu): annual, every fall
  • COVID-19: current CDC-recommended doses (changes; check)
  • Shingles (Shingrix): 2 doses at age 50+
  • Pneumococcal: at 65+ or younger with risk (PCV20 or PCV15+PPSV23)
  • HPV: 2-3 doses; routine up to age 26; some adults 27-45 with new partners
  • RSV: 60+ (shared decision-making)
  • Pregnancy: Tdap during each pregnancy, flu, RSV per CDC

Risk math

Side Effects vs Disease Risk

Draw

Make the comparison explicit

  • Common vaccine side effects: sore arm, fatigue, low fever, headache (24-48 hours)
  • Rare serious reactions: anaphylaxis ~1 in a million; specific risks documented for each vaccine
  • Disease risks for comparison: measles encephalitis 1 in 1000; flu hospitalization 1-2% in adults; shingles ~1 in 3 lifetime, 10-20% develop chronic nerve pain
  • Pertussis: can be fatal in infants; you get vaccinated to protect them
  • HPV: causes most cervical, anal, throat cancers; vaccine cuts these > 90%
  • Don't compare "vaccine side effects" to "nothing" — compare to "the disease the vaccine prevents"
  • Check VAERS for reports but understand it's unfiltered; v-safe and CDC have processed data

Special situations

When to Get Extras

Concept

Beyond the baseline schedule

  • International travel: hepatitis A, typhoid, yellow fever, Japanese encephalitis, rabies (per destination)
  • Healthcare workers: hepatitis B, MMR proof, annual flu, COVID
  • Pregnancy: Tdap each pregnancy (27-36 weeks), flu (any trimester), COVID, RSV
  • Immunocompromised: pneumococcal, more frequent flu, NO live vaccines (no MMR, varicella, yellow fever)
  • Asplenia/sickle cell: pneumococcal + meningococcal
  • Men who have sex with men: HPV, hepatitis A + B, meningococcal, monkeypox if available
  • Chronic conditions (diabetes, heart disease, COPD, kidney disease): annual flu, pneumococcal
  • Behind on schedule? Catch-up; never need to restart a series
Bottom line: Bring your vaccine record to every primary care visit. If you don't have one, your pharmacy or state immunization registry can usually reconstruct it. Most adult vaccines are covered by insurance under preventive care, no copay. CDC's adult schedule is the source of truth; update annually.
