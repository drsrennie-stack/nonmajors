01

Stroke

Stroke: F.A.S.T.

Draw

Every minute kills neurons

  • F = Face drooping: one side of face droops or feels numb. Ask them to smile.
  • A = Arm weakness: one arm is weak or numb. Ask them to raise both arms.
  • S = Speech difficulty: slurred, can't speak, hard to understand. Ask them to repeat a sentence.
  • T = Time to call 911: note the exact time symptoms started
  • Bonus signs: sudden severe headache, vision loss, dizziness, loss of balance
  • Call 911, do NOT drive yourself (EMS can pre-alert the hospital)
  • Clot-busting drug (tPA) works only within 3-4.5 hours; clot retrieval within 24 hours for some cases
  • Every minute ≈ 2 million neurons lost
Draw: a face on the left with one side drooping (asymmetric smile). An arm on the right with one side weak/drooping. Speech bubble below with garbled letters. Clock above showing 'TIME TO CALL 911'. Label each F.A.S.T. element.
02

Heart attack

Heart Attack Warning Signs

Draw

Not always the Hollywood version

  • Classic: chest pain or pressure, may radiate to left arm, jaw, back
  • Pressure, squeezing, fullness, NOT always sharp pain
  • Shortness of breath with or without chest pain
  • Cold sweat, nausea, lightheadedness
  • Women often have atypical symptoms: fatigue, jaw or back pain, indigestion-like discomfort, often without chest pain
  • Symptoms lasting more than a few minutes or coming and going
  • Call 911 immediately. Chew 1 adult aspirin (325 mg) if no allergy and not bleeding
  • Do NOT drive yourself. EMS starts treatment en route and notifies the cath lab
Draw: a body diagram with arrows pointing to all possible heart attack pain locations: chest (center), left arm, jaw, back, upper abdomen. Mark each with a label. Add a separate annotation showing women's atypical symptoms (fatigue, nausea, shortness of breath without chest pain).
03

Anaphylaxis

Anaphylaxis

Draw

Allergic reaction shutting down systems

  • Severe allergic reaction within minutes to hours of exposure (food, sting, medication)
  • Skin: hives, swelling (especially face, lips, tongue)
  • Airway: wheezing, tightness, hoarse voice
  • Breathing: shortness of breath
  • Circulation: dizziness, fainting, rapid pulse
  • GI: nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, cramping
  • Treatment: epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen) into outer thigh, hold 10 seconds
  • Call 911 even if EpiPen worked; reaction can rebound
  • Antihistamines (Benadryl) are NOT a substitute for epinephrine
Draw: a person with anaphylaxis symptoms labeled: hives + swelling on skin, swollen face/lips, wheezing arrows at airway, fast heart icon, BP drop. Next to them, draw an EpiPen with arrow to outer thigh and step numbers (uncap, swing-firm into thigh, hold 10 sec).
04

Other 911

Other Real Emergencies

Concept

Call 911, do not drive

  • Severe bleeding that won't stop with 10 minutes of pressure
  • Severe head injury with loss of consciousness, confusion, vomiting
  • Seizure lasting > 5 minutes, or first-ever seizure
  • Unresponsive or not breathing: call 911, start CPR immediately
  • Sudden severe headache ("worst of life"): may be hemorrhage
  • Choking with inability to speak or breathe: Heimlich
  • Major trauma: fall from height, car crash with injury, gunshot, stabbing
  • Suicidal with plan or attempt: call 988 or 911
  • Overdose: call 911; if opioid suspected, use naloxone (Narcan) nasal spray
Bottom line: When in doubt, call. 911 dispatchers can triage and tell you whether to drive or wait. The cost of an ambulance ride is real, but so is a stroke without tPA. Have your address and phone number written near every phone in your home, and keep emergency contacts in your phone's emergency info section.
05

Triage

911 vs Urgent Care vs Primary Care

Concept

Match the symptom to the level

  • 911 / ER: stroke signs, heart attack signs, anaphylaxis, severe bleeding, head injury, suicidal crisis, can't breathe, can't speak, sudden severe pain
  • Urgent care: suspected UTI, sprain, minor cut needing stitches, sore throat with fever, ear infection, rash, mild asthma flare, mild dehydration
  • Primary care: persistent cough, ongoing fatigue, medication refills, new mole evaluation, blood pressure check, annual exam
  • Pharmacist: OTC questions, side effects, drug interactions, vaccines
  • Telehealth: medication questions, rash photos, mild illness, mental health follow-up
  • Cost ranking: 911/ER > urgent care > primary care > telehealth/pharmacist
  • Insurance navigation: call the number on your card; nurse lines often free and 24/7
Bottom line: Use the right level: ER for emergencies, urgent care for can't-wait but not life-threatening, primary care for ongoing. Most insurance plans have 24/7 nurse hotlines free to members, that's often a great first call when you're not sure. Know the urgent care closest to you BEFORE you need it.
