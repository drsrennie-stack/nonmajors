01

Energy in

Caloric Value of Fuels

Concept

What enters the body

  • Carbohydrate: 4 kcal/g (16.7 kJ/g); glucose is the rapid-access form
  • Protein: 4 kcal/g; primarily for synthesis, used as fuel only when carb + fat are short
  • Fat: 9 kcal/g (37 kJ/g); densest fuel, slow to mobilize
  • Alcohol (ethanol): 7 kcal/g; metabolized in liver, no storage form
  • Daily recommended intake (rough US adult, sedentary): women ~1,800-2,000 kcal, men ~2,200-2,500 kcal
  • Atwater general factors: above values are usable energy after digestive losses
  • Bomb calorimeter: gives total combustion energy; physiologic value is lower because not everything is fully absorbed/oxidized
  • RQ (respiratory quotient): ratio CO₂ produced / O₂ consumed; 1.0 = pure carb, ~0.7 = pure fat, ~0.8 = protein, ~0.85 = mixed
  • Indirect calorimetry: measures O₂ uptake + CO₂ output → estimates energy use + fuel mix
Takeaway: the body cares about kcal and macronutrient ratio. Fat packs the most energy per gram and is the storage form, carbs are the fast fuel, protein is dual-use but reserved for synthesis. Alcohol is empty calories that the liver has to process first.
02

Energy out

Total Energy Expenditure (TEE)

Draw

Where calories go

  • BMR: ~60-75% of TEE; mostly brain, liver, heart, kidney, skeletal muscle
  • Brain alone: ~20% of BMR despite being ~2% of body weight
  • Diet-induced thermogenesis (DIT): ~10% of TEE; heat made digesting + absorbing food; highest for protein (~25% of its kcal), lower for carbs + fat
  • Activity thermogenesis: 15-30%+ of TEE; the most variable component
  • Subcomponents: exercise activity (EAT) + non-exercise activity thermogenesis (NEAT) like fidgeting, posture, daily movement
  • NEAT can vary by ~2,000 kcal/day between individuals at the same body weight
  • Mifflin-St Jeor equation: men BMR = 10(kg) + 6.25(cm) − 5(age) + 5; women = same − 161
  • TEE = BMR × activity factor: sedentary 1.2, light 1.375, moderate 1.55, very active 1.725
  • Adaptive thermogenesis: BMR drops with prolonged caloric restriction (~10-15% below predicted) — major reason weight regain is so common after dieting
Draw: a pie chart showing TEE divided into three slices: BMR (~65%), activity (~25%), DIT (~10%). Then a horizontal bar showing activity broken into EAT + NEAT. Beside it, write the Mifflin-St Jeor equation for one sample patient (e.g., 30 yo female, 65 kg, 165 cm) and walk through the BMR calculation. Add a note: 'Adaptive thermogenesis may lower BMR ~10-15% in restriction.'
03

State switching

Fed, Postabsorptive, Fasting, Starvation

Concept

The hourly metabolic pendulum

  • Fed (absorptive) state, 0-4 h: nutrients flooding from gut; insulin high, glucagon low
  • Glucose enters cells; liver + muscle build glycogen; adipose builds fat; protein synthesis active
  • Postabsorptive state, 4-12 h (overnight fast): blood glucose drops; insulin falls, glucagon rises
  • Liver breaks down glycogen → glucose for the blood; brain still uses glucose
  • Early fasting, 12-24 h: liver glycogen running out (~24 h); gluconeogenesis takes over
  • Amino acids (from muscle), glycerol (from fat), lactate (from anaerobic tissues) → glucose in liver
  • Prolonged fasting, >24-72 h: fat becomes dominant fuel; ketogenesis ramps up; ketone bodies (β-hydroxybutyrate, acetoacetate, acetone) supplement brain glucose
  • Starvation, >1 week: protein sparing kicks in — body conserves lean mass; BMR drops ~25%; ketones supply ~75% of brain energy
  • Refeeding syndrome: after prolonged starvation, abrupt carb intake triggers insulin surge → K, Mg, P shift into cells → dangerous hypophosphatemia, arrhythmia, heart failure; refeed slowly with electrolyte monitoring
  • Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA): looks like prolonged fasting on metabolism slide but caused by no insulin (type 1) — uncontrolled lipolysis + ketogenesis + hyperglycemia + dehydration
Takeaway: the body's switch from carbs to fat to ketones is automatic and hormonally orchestrated. Brain runs on glucose by default but can adapt to ketones over weeks. Recognizing where in this sequence a patient sits (overnight fast vs starved vs DKA) directs treatment.
04

Hormonal control

Master Hormones

Concept

Insulin, glucagon, cortisol, GH, leptin, ghrelin

  • Insulin: dominant fed-state hormone; pushes glucose, AA, fat into storage; only hormone that lowers blood glucose
  • Glucagon: rises in fasting; mobilizes liver glycogen, drives gluconeogenesis + ketogenesis
  • Cortisol: rises in chronic stress + prolonged fasting + diurnally; gluconeogenesis, insulin resistance, protein catabolism if chronic
  • Growth hormone: rises during fasting, sleep, exercise; spares glucose for brain by promoting fat use
  • Catecholamines (epi, NE): acute stress; glycogenolysis, lipolysis, raise BP + HR
  • Thyroid hormone: sets metabolic tone; modest changes shift BMR substantially
  • Leptin: from adipose; says 'I'm full' — suppresses appetite, raises energy use
  • Ghrelin: from empty stomach; says 'I'm hungry' — drives food-seeking
  • Leptin resistance: in obesity, leptin levels are high but signaling is blunted — body acts as if starving despite abundant fat stores
  • Insulin resistance: cells less responsive to insulin → pancreas compensates with higher output → eventually fails → type 2 diabetes
  • Clinical takeaway: most metabolic diseases are problems with this hormonal balance, not problems with the food itself
Takeaway: a small number of hormones regulate energy balance day-to-day and decade-to-decade. Diabetes, obesity, and metabolic syndrome are essentially long-running imbalances in this same pendulum. Understanding the timeline of state switching is what makes nutrition, fasting protocols, and pharmacology coherent rather than fad-driven.
