Physiology · Lab Skill 1

Calculate osmolarity of clinical IV fluids

Determine osmolarity (mOsm/L) for each common IV fluid. Identify whether each is isotonic, hypotonic, or hypertonic relative to plasma.

Osmolarity reference

Osmolarity (mOsm/L) = sum of all dissociated solute particles per liter. NaCl dissociates into 2 particles (Na⁺ and Cl⁻). Glucose does not dissociate (1 particle). Lactated Ringer's contains Na⁺, Cl⁻, K⁺, Ca²⁺, and lactate.

Plasma osmolarity: approximately 290 mOsm/L (range 275 to 295). This is your reference point for tonicity classification.

Tonicity classification: Isotonic ≈ 280 to 295 mOsm/L. Hypotonic < 280. Hypertonic > 295.

For each IV fluid, calculate osmolarity and classify tonicity. Show your work in the calculation column. The answer key uses standard clinical values; your calculations should approximate these.

Physiology · Lab Skill 2

Burn assessment: depth and Rule of Nines

Identify burn depth from clinical findings and calculate body surface area (BSA) using the Rule of Nines.

Burn depth classification

Superficial (1st degree): epidermis only. Red, painful, no blisters. Sunburn is a classic example. Heals in 3 to 7 days. NOT counted in BSA totals for resuscitation.

Partial thickness (2nd degree): epidermis and part of the dermis. Red, painful, blistered, may weep fluid. Counted in BSA totals.

Full thickness (3rd degree): epidermis, dermis, and hypodermis (sometimes deeper). White, leathery, or charred. Painless centrally because nerve endings are destroyed. Counted in BSA totals. Always counted.

Rule of Nines (adult)

Each region is assigned a percentage of total body surface area:

Head and neck: 9%  ·  Each arm: 9%  ·  Anterior trunk (chest + abdomen): 18%  ·  Posterior trunk (back): 18%  ·  Each leg: 18%  ·  Genitalia: 1%

Total = 100%. Pediatric values differ (head is larger proportionally), adult values are used in this dry lab.

Parkland Formula: fluid resuscitation

Calculate the 24-hour fluid replacement volume for a burn patient and explain the choice of Lactated Ringer's specifically.

The Parkland Formula

Total fluid (mL) for first 24 hours = 4 mL × patient weight (kg) × %BSA burned

First 8 hours: give half the total volume (timed from moment of injury, not from arrival to ER).

Next 16 hours: give the second half.

Fluid choice: Lactated Ringer's (LR). LR is preferred over Normal Saline for burn resuscitation. You will explain why in the synthesis question.

Physiology · Clinical Cases

Apply tonicity and burn resuscitation to four patients

Each case integrates cell membrane physiology, tissue damage assessment, and clinical fluid management.

Case 1

The dehydrated marathon runner

A 28-year-old runner collapses near the finish line. She is conscious but lethargic, tachycardic (HR 118), hypotensive (BP 88/56). Serum sodium is 132 mEq/L (slightly low). She has been drinking only plain water for the past 4 hours. She has no burns or trauma.

  1. What is happening to her cells based on her sodium level and fluid intake?
  2. Which IV fluid would you choose: D5W, NS, or 3% saline? Explain the reasoning based on tonicity.
Case 2

The kitchen fire victim

A 60 kg woman, age 45, presents 30 minutes after a kitchen grease fire. She has partial thickness burns over the entire anterior chest and abdomen, plus full thickness burns to her entire right arm. Her left arm and lower body are spared.

  1. Calculate her total %BSA burned (partial + full thickness only).
  2. Calculate her total Parkland fluid requirement for 24 hours.
  3. How much fluid should be given in the first 8 hours? In the next 16 hours?
Case 3

The traumatic brain injury patient

A 22-year-old motorcycle crash victim has a severe traumatic brain injury with cerebral edema. The neurosurgery team wants to reduce brain swelling pharmacologically before craniotomy. Their goal is to pull water out of brain cells and into the vasculature to reduce intracranial pressure.

  1. Which IV fluid would accomplish this goal: NS, ½NS, or 3% saline? Explain the tonicity logic.
  2. What would happen to the patient's brain cells if ½NS were given by mistake?
Case 4

The transfusion error

A nurse accidentally infuses 250 mL of D5W (rather than the intended NS) to flush a line that just received packed red blood cells. The patient develops a transfusion reaction with hemoglobinuria.

  1. What happened to the red blood cells in the line when they encountered D5W? Use the words tonicity, osmosis, and lysis in your answer.
  2. Why is NS the correct fluid to flush a transfusion line?

Synthesis question · Most weighted

In a burn patient, capillaries become leaky and large amounts of plasma fluid shift out of the vasculature into the interstitial space. This is why burn patients need so much fluid replacement.

In four to six sentences, explain why Lactated Ringer's is the resuscitation fluid of choice for burns instead of D5W or 3% saline. Your answer must integrate:

(1) tissue layers damaged in a full-thickness burn and why this causes fluid shift, (2) tonicity of LR vs D5W vs 3% saline relative to plasma, (3) what would happen to cells if D5W were used instead, (4) what would happen if 3% saline were used instead, (5) why LR's electrolyte composition matches what was lost.
