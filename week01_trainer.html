Anatomical Language: Position, Planes, Cavities, Directions

Anatomy is a language with rules. Before you can describe a structure or a finding, you have to know how to locate it on the body. Every clinical chart you ever read assumes you speak this language fluently.

▾
Retrieve Recall the language
  1. 1.
    Define anatomical position. Why does every directional term in anatomy assume the body is in this position?
    Anatomical position: standing erect, feet parallel and slightly apart, eyes facing forward, arms at the sides, palms facing forward (supinated), thumbs pointing away from the body. All directional terms (medial, lateral, anterior, posterior) reference this position so that descriptions are unambiguous regardless of how the patient is currently oriented.
  2. 2.
    List the three primary anatomical planes and the kind of section each one produces.
    Sagittal: divides body into left and right portions (midsagittal = equal halves). Frontal (coronal): divides body into anterior and posterior portions. Transverse (horizontal, axial): divides body into superior and inferior portions. Imaging studies almost always reference one of these three planes.
  3. 3.
    Name the two major body cavities and identify which one houses the heart, lungs, and digestive organs.
    Dorsal cavity (cranial + vertebral) houses the brain and spinal cord. Ventral cavity (thoracic + abdominopelvic) houses the heart, lungs, digestive organs, and reproductive organs. The diaphragm separates thoracic from abdominopelvic.
  4. 4.
    Define each pair: proximal / distal, superficial / deep, medial / lateral, anterior / posterior.
    Proximal / distal: closer to / farther from the trunk attachment (used for limbs). Superficial / deep: closer to / farther from the body surface. Medial / lateral: closer to / farther from the body midline. Anterior (ventral) / posterior (dorsal): toward the front / toward the back.

Levels of Organization & Homeostasis

Every system in this course is built from the same six structural levels and the same regulatory logic. Get this right in week one and the rest of the semester compounds in your favor.

▾
Retrieve The six levels
  1. 1.
    List the six levels of structural organization, smallest to largest. Give one example of each.
    Chemical (atoms, molecules, e.g., water, glucose) → Cellular (e.g., red blood cell, hepatocyte) → Tissue (e.g., simple squamous epithelium) → Organ (e.g., heart, kidney) → Organ system (e.g., cardiovascular) → Organism (the human body integrated as a whole).
  2. 2.
    Define homeostasis. Why is it described as dynamic, not static?
    Homeostasis is the maintenance of relatively stable internal conditions despite continuous changes inside and outside the body. It is dynamic because regulated variables (temperature, pH, glucose, blood pressure) constantly fluctuate around a set point. The body does not hold a fixed value, it corrects deviations through feedback loops in real time.
  3. 3.
    Identify the three components of any feedback loop, and give the standard sequence of steps.
    Receptor (sensor) detects a change in the regulated variable → Control center (often the brain or an endocrine gland) compares input to the set point and decides on a response → Effector (muscle or gland) carries out the response that returns the variable toward the set point.
  4. 4.
    Distinguish negative feedback from positive feedback. Which one maintains homeostasis, and why?
    Negative feedback reverses the change in the variable, restoring it toward the set point. This is the dominant mode of homeostasis (e.g., body temperature regulation, blood glucose). Positive feedback amplifies the change, driving the variable further from the starting point. It is used for events that need to complete rapidly and definitively (e.g., labor contractions, blood clotting, action potentials).

Chemical Foundations: Water, pH, Biomolecules

You do not need to memorize every electron orbital. You do need to know the chemistry that drives physiology: why water is special, what pH does to proteins, and how the four biomolecules show up in the body.

▾
Retrieve Inorganic and organic compounds
  1. 1.
    Name four properties of water that make it essential to physiology, and explain why each one matters in the body.
    Polarity (universal solvent, dissolves ions and many biomolecules); high specific heat (resists temperature changes, stabilizes core temperature); cohesion / surface tension (alveolar fluid behavior, blood column integrity); participates as a reactant in hydrolysis and dehydration synthesis (every digestive reaction, every protein synthesis step). Water also lubricates (synovial fluid, serous membranes) and cushions (CSF, amniotic fluid).
  2. 2.
    Define pH. What is the normal pH of arterial blood, and what is the difference between an acid and a base?
    pH is the negative log of the hydrogen ion concentration. Lower pH = more H⁺ = more acidic. Normal arterial blood pH is 7.35 to 7.45, slightly basic. An acid donates H⁺ (raises [H⁺], lowers pH). A base accepts H⁺ (lowers [H⁺], raises pH). A buffer (e.g., bicarbonate) resists pH change by absorbing or releasing H⁺ as conditions shift.
  3. 3.
    List the four classes of organic biomolecules. For each, give the building block (monomer) and one major physiological role.
    Carbohydrates: monomer = monosaccharide (glucose). Role: primary fuel, glycogen storage, glycoproteins on cell surfaces. Lipids: "monomer" varies (fatty acids + glycerol; cholesterol). Role: membrane bilayer (phospholipids), long-term energy (triglycerides), steroid hormones (cholesterol-derived). Proteins: monomer = amino acid. Role: enzymes, structural fibers (collagen, keratin), receptors, transporters, antibodies, contractile machinery. Nucleic acids: monomer = nucleotide. Role: information storage (DNA), protein synthesis (RNA), energy currency (ATP).
  4. 4.
    What does it mean for a protein to be denatured? Name two physiological conditions that can denature proteins.
    Denaturation is the loss of a protein's three-dimensional shape, which destroys its function (because protein function depends on shape). Causes include extreme temperature (high fever above 41°C, severe hypothermia), extreme pH (gastric acid, severe acidosis), and certain chemicals or heavy metals. Once denatured, most proteins cannot refold and the cell either repairs or degrades them.

Focus · OpenStax 3.1, 3.2

Cellular Architecture: Membrane, Transport, Organelles

The plasma membrane is the most heavily tested concept in this section because it is the gatekeeper of every physiological process. Master it now. Every chapter we cover from week 2 forward depends on it.

▾
Retrieve Membrane and organelle essentials
  1. 1.
    Describe the fluid mosaic model of the plasma membrane. What are the four molecular components and where is each one located?
    A phospholipid bilayer with hydrophilic phosphate heads facing the extracellular and intracellular fluids and hydrophobic fatty acid tails facing inward. Embedded in the bilayer: cholesterol (stabilizes fluidity), integral proteins (span the bilayer, function as channels, carriers, receptors), peripheral proteins (attached to one side, often anchor cytoskeleton or signal), and glycoproteins / glycolipids (carbohydrate chains on the extracellular side, function in cell recognition).
  2. 2.
    Compare passive and active transport. Which one requires ATP and why?
    Passive transport (simple diffusion, facilitated diffusion, osmosis) moves substances down their concentration gradient. No ATP required; the gradient itself provides the energy. Active transport moves substances against their concentration gradient. ATP is required because work must be done to push molecules from low to high concentration. The Na⁺/K⁺ pump is the classic example: 3 Na⁺ out, 2 K⁺ in, per ATP hydrolyzed.
  3. 3.
    Define tonicity. What happens to a red blood cell placed in a hypertonic solution versus a hypotonic solution?
    Tonicity is the relative solute concentration of a solution compared to the inside of the cell, and it predicts which way water will move by osmosis. Hypertonic (more solute outside): water leaves the cell, the cell shrinks (crenation in RBCs). Hypotonic (less solute outside): water enters the cell, the cell swells and may burst (hemolysis in RBCs). Isotonic: no net water movement, cell stays the same size. This principle is why IV fluids are matched to plasma osmolarity.
  4. 4.
    Match each organelle to its main job: mitochondrion, rough ER, smooth ER, Golgi apparatus, lysosome, ribosome, nucleus.
    Nucleus: houses DNA, controls gene expression. Ribosome: protein synthesis (free ribosomes make cytosolic proteins; bound ribosomes make secreted/membrane proteins). Rough ER: ribosome-studded, synthesizes and folds proteins for export. Smooth ER: lipid synthesis, detoxification, calcium storage (especially in muscle). Golgi apparatus: modifies, sorts, and packages proteins/lipids for secretion or delivery. Lysosome: digestive sac with hydrolytic enzymes; breaks down debris and worn organelles. Mitochondrion: ATP production via oxidative phosphorylation (the cell's power plant).

The Four Tissue Types

Every organ in the body is built from some combination of these four tissues. Lab Exam 1 will require you to identify them under the microscope and name their functions. Spend time here.

▾
Retrieve Tissue catalog
  1. 1.
    List the four primary tissue types and the general function of each one.
    Epithelial: covers, lines, secretes. Forms barriers and glands. Connective: binds, supports, protects, transports. Most diverse tissue type (includes blood, bone, fat). Muscle: contracts to produce movement. Three subtypes (skeletal, cardiac, smooth). Nervous: generates and conducts electrical signals for rapid communication. Includes neurons and glial cells.
  2. 2.
    Epithelial tissue is classified by two features. Name them and give the categories within each.
    Number of cell layers: simple (one layer), stratified (multiple layers), pseudostratified (one layer that looks multilayered). Cell shape: squamous (flat), cuboidal (cube-shaped), columnar (taller than wide). Combinations describe specific epithelia: simple squamous (alveoli, capillaries), stratified squamous (skin, esophagus), simple columnar (most of GI tract), pseudostratified ciliated columnar (respiratory tract).
  3. 3.
    What three things does connective tissue proper always contain? Why is the matrix the defining feature of connective tissue?
    Every connective tissue contains cells (often relatively few), protein fibers (collagen, elastic, reticular), and ground substance (the fluid, gel, or solid that fills the space between cells and fibers). Together fibers and ground substance make the extracellular matrix. The matrix, not the cells, gives each connective tissue its mechanical properties: bone is hard because its matrix is calcified, blood is fluid because its matrix is plasma, tendon is tough because its matrix is dense regular collagen.
  4. 4.
    Compare the three types of muscle tissue on these features: voluntary or involuntary, striated or non-striated, single or multiple nuclei, and one location example.
    Skeletal: voluntary, striated, multinucleated, attached to bones (e.g., biceps brachii). Cardiac: involuntary, striated, usually one (sometimes two) central nuclei, intercalated discs, found only in the heart. Smooth: involuntary, non-striated, single central nucleus, spindle-shaped, found in walls of hollow organs (GI tract, blood vessels, uterus, bladder).

Clinical Calculators: BMI & Body Surface Area

This week your lab introduces the measure, calculate, interpret pattern. Two calculations show up everywhere in clinical practice. Use the live tools below to learn the formulas, then run the apply problems by hand.

▾
Retrieve Why we calculate
  1. 1.
    Write the formula for BMI in metric units. What does BMI estimate?
    BMI = weight (kg) / height (m)². BMI estimates body fat indirectly using height and weight. It is a screening tool, not a diagnosis. It does not distinguish lean mass from fat mass, so muscular athletes and frail older adults can have misleading values.
  2. 2.
    Write the Mosteller formula for Body Surface Area. Why is BSA used instead of weight alone for many medications?
    BSA (m²) = √(height in cm × weight in kg / 3600). BSA correlates better than body weight with metabolic rate, blood volume, and cardiac output. Many chemotherapy and pediatric drugs are dosed per m² of BSA so that dose scales with physiological size, not just mass.
  3. 3.
    List the standard adult BMI categories.
    Underweight: less than 18.5. Normal: 18.5 to 24.9. Overweight: 25.0 to 29.9. Obesity class I: 30.0 to 34.9. Obesity class II: 35.0 to 39.9. Obesity class III: 40.0 or greater. (CDC and WHO adult criteria.)
