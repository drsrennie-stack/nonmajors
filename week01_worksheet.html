02
Build & Apply · Homeostasis
Feedback loops in action
A loop with no labeled receptor, control center, or effector loses points. Show the arrows that close the loop.
Build

Draw the negative feedback loop that controls body temperature when the body is too hot. Label the receptor, control center, effector, and the response. Show the arrows that close the loop and indicate whether each arrow represents stimulation or inhibition.

Rubric: All three components labeled. At least one effector named. Arrows form a closed loop. Set point indicated.

Apply

a. A patient enters the emergency department with a core temperature of 35.4°C. What feedback responses should you expect to see if their thermoregulation is intact? Address all four: sweating, shivering, skin color change, behavior.

Apply

b. Childbirth uses positive feedback (oxytocin and uterine contractions). Why is positive feedback the right design choice here, even though it is unstable?

04
Build & Apply · Cellular Architecture
The plasma membrane and cellular consequences
The membrane drawing is required from memory. Label everything.
Build

Draw a section of plasma membrane. Include and label all of the following: phospholipid bilayer, hydrophilic heads, hydrophobic tails, cholesterol, an integral protein channel, a peripheral protein, and a glycoprotein. Indicate which side is extracellular and which is intracellular.

Rubric: All seven components present and labeled. Bilayer orientation correct. Sides correctly identified.

Apply

a. A nurse accidentally infuses a patient with pure water IV instead of saline. Predict what happens to the patient's red blood cells. What term describes this event?

Apply

b. A pancreatic acinar cell secretes large amounts of digestive enzymes. Predict which organelle would be unusually abundant in this cell and explain why.

Apply

c. Mature red blood cells have no nucleus and no mitochondria. Predict two consequences of these absences for RBC physiology.

05
Build & Apply · Tissues
Tissue identity and design logic
Histology is taught by patterns. Defending why a tissue is built the way it is locks in the pattern.
Build

Draw a simple columnar epithelium with goblet cells. Label all of the following: apical surface, basal surface, basement membrane, nucleus, goblet cell, lumen.

Rubric: Cells columnar (taller than wide). Single layer. Goblet cell distinct. All six labels present.

Apply

a. The lining of the small intestine is simple columnar with microvilli. Justify each design choice (why simple and not stratified? why columnar and not squamous? why microvilli?).

Apply

b. The esophagus is lined with stratified squamous epithelium. The stomach is lined with simple columnar. Explain why the same digestive tract uses two different epithelia.

Apply

c. After a deep cut, scar tissue forms instead of original tissue. What kind of tissue is scar tissue, and why does the body default to it instead of regenerating the original?
