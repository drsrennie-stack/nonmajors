How this worksheet works

BIO 304 is an online course. Work through the Week 1 Cognitive Trainer first as your study tool. This worksheet is what you turn in. It captures only the drawings, predictions, calculations, and reflections.

How to complete: Click the Print button above (or use your browser's Print menu) and print the worksheet. Complete every box by hand in pen or pencil. Scan or photograph the completed pages as a single PDF. Upload that PDF to the Week 1 Worksheet assignment in Canvas.

Drawing tasks: The gridded boxes are for hand-drawn diagrams. Label every component listed in the prompt. An unlabeled drawing is a sketch and loses points.

If you cannot print: contact Dr. Rennie or DSPS for an alternative submission format.

Apply · Anatomical Language

Translate clinical descriptions into anatomical terminology

Use precise directional terms, planes, and cavity names. Do not just rewrite the sentence in better English. Use the language of anatomy.

Apply

Apply task. a. Translate this sentence using anatomical terminology: "The patient has a wound on the front of the lower leg, near the ankle."

Use directional terms and a regional name where possible.

Apply

Apply task. b. Translate this sentence using anatomical terminology: "The MRI was sliced into top-down images that separate the head into upper and lower portions."

Identify the imaging plane.

Apply

Apply task. c. Translate this sentence using anatomical terminology: "The tumor is located deep to the skin of the abdomen, on the right side, below the diaphragm."

Use directional terms and a quadrant or region name.

02

Build & Apply · Homeostasis

Feedback loops in action

A loop with no labeled receptor, control center, or effector loses points. Show the arrows that close the loop.

Build

Build task. Draw the negative feedback loop that controls body temperature when the body is too hot. Label the receptor, control center, effector, and the response. Show the arrows that close the loop.

Rubric: All three components labeled. At least one effector named. Arrows form a closed loop. Set point indicated.

If sketching by hand, leave this box for your drawing. If typing, describe your loop step by step.

Apply

Apply task. a. A patient enters the emergency department with a core temperature of 35.4°C. What feedback responses should you expect to see if their thermoregulation is intact? Address all four: sweating, shivering, skin color change, behavior.

Apply

Apply task. b. Childbirth uses positive feedback (oxytocin and uterine contractions). Why is positive feedback the right design choice here, even though it is unstable?

pH and the logarithmic scale

Show your math. The number alone is not the answer.

Apply

Apply task. A patient's blood pH falls from 7.40 to 7.20. By what factor has the [H⁺] increased? What is this acid-base disturbance called? Justify both parts of your answer with the chemistry.

04

Build & Apply · Cellular Architecture

The plasma membrane and cellular consequences

The membrane drawing is required from memory. Label everything.

Build

Build task. Draw a section of plasma membrane. Include and label all of the following: phospholipid bilayer, hydrophilic heads, hydrophobic tails, cholesterol, an integral protein channel, a peripheral protein, and a glycoprotein. Indicate which side is extracellular and which is intracellular.

Rubric: All seven components present and labeled. Bilayer orientation correct. Sides correctly identified.

Apply

Apply task. a. A nurse accidentally infuses a patient with pure water IV instead of saline. Predict what happens to the patient's red blood cells. What term describes this event?

Apply

Apply task. b. A pancreatic acinar cell secretes large amounts of digestive enzymes. Predict which organelle would be unusually abundant in this cell and explain why.

Apply

Apply task. c. Mature red blood cells have no nucleus and no mitochondria. Predict two consequences of these absences for RBC physiology.

05

Build & Apply · Tissues

Tissue identity and design logic

Histology is taught by patterns. Defending why a tissue is built the way it is locks in the pattern.

Build

Build task. Draw a simple columnar epithelium with goblet cells. Label all of the following: apical surface, basal surface, basement membrane, nucleus, goblet cell, lumen.

Rubric: Cells columnar (taller than wide). Single layer. Goblet cell distinct. All six labels present.

Apply

Apply task. a. The lining of the small intestine is simple columnar with microvilli. Justify each design choice (why simple and not stratified? why columnar and not squamous? why microvilli?).

Apply

Apply task. b. The esophagus is lined with stratified squamous epithelium. The stomach is lined with simple columnar. Explain why the same digestive tract uses two different epithelia.

Apply

Apply task. c. After a deep cut, scar tissue forms instead of original tissue. What kind of tissue is scar tissue, and why does the body default to it instead of regenerating the original?

06

Calculate · Lab Apply

BMI & BSA hand calculations

No calculators on lab quizzes. Show formulas, units, and a final classified result.

Calculate

Calculation task. a. Patient: 168 cm, 72 kg. Calculate BMI to one decimal place. Show the formula and units. Classify the result.

Show your work: formula, substituted values with units, calculation, final value, classification.

Calculate

Calculation task. b. Same patient. Calculate BSA using the Mosteller formula. Show your work to two decimal places.

Mosteller: BSA (m²) = √(height cm × weight kg / 3600).

Calculate

Calculation task. c. A chemotherapy drug is dosed at 80 mg/m². Using the BSA you calculated in part b, what dose should the patient receive? Round to the nearest milligram.
