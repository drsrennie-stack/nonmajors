Physiology · Lab Skill 1

Interpret DEXA scan results

Read T-score and Z-score values, classify bone density, and recognize when each is appropriate.

DEXA scan basics

DEXA (dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry) measures bone mineral density (BMD) at the spine and femoral neck. It is the gold standard for diagnosing osteoporosis.

T-score: compares the patient's BMD to a healthy young adult of the same sex. Used for postmenopausal women and men over 50.

Z-score: compares the patient's BMD to age-matched and sex-matched peers. Used for premenopausal women, men under 50, and children. Z-score below -2.0 prompts secondary cause workup.

WHO classification (T-score)

Normal: T-score ≥ -1.0

Osteopenia (low bone mass): T-score between -1.0 and -2.5

Osteoporosis: T-score ≤ -2.5

Severe (established) osteoporosis: T-score ≤ -2.5 PLUS one or more fragility fractures

Physiology · Lab Skill 2

FRAX 10-year fracture risk

Calculate 10-year risk of major osteoporotic fracture and hip fracture using clinical risk factors.

The FRAX tool

FRAX integrates 11 clinical risk factors with femoral neck BMD to predict 10-year fracture risk. The actual tool is online (sheffield.ac.uk/FRAX) and uses country-specific population data. For this lab, you will work with simplified case profiles to predict whether risk meets the National Osteoporosis Foundation (NOF) treatment threshold.

FRAX inputs: age · sex · weight · height (used to calculate BMI) · prior fragility fracture · parental hip fracture · current smoking · long-term glucocorticoid use · rheumatoid arthritis · secondary osteoporosis cause · alcohol intake (3+ units/day) · femoral neck T-score

NOF treatment thresholds

Pharmacologic treatment is recommended when:

10-year hip fracture risk ≥ 3%  OR  10-year major osteoporotic fracture risk ≥ 20%

Major osteoporotic fracture sites: hip, spine, wrist, proximal humerus.

Treatment is also indicated for any patient with: T-score ≤ -2.5 at hip or spine, OR a prior fragility fracture of hip or spine regardless of T-score.

Physiology · Lab Skill 3

Design an osteoporosis intervention plan

For one patient who meets treatment criteria, build a complete plan covering pharmacology, nutrition, exercise, and fall prevention.

Pharmacologic options

Bisphosphonates (alendronate, risedronate, zoledronic acid): first-line for most postmenopausal osteoporosis. Inhibit osteoclasts, reducing bone resorption. Oral or IV. Caution: not used in patients planning pregnancy (long bone half-life), severe esophageal disease, hypocalcemia, or significant CKD.

SERMs (raloxifene): selective estrogen receptor modulators. Mimic estrogen's bone-protective effect without breast/uterine stimulation. Used in postmenopausal women who cannot tolerate bisphosphonates. Caution: increased VTE risk.

Denosumab: RANK ligand inhibitor. Subcutaneous injection every 6 months. Inhibits osteoclast formation and survival. Used when bisphosphonates fail or are contraindicated. Caution: rebound bone loss if stopped without follow-on therapy.

Teriparatide / abaloparatide: PTH analogs. Anabolic agents that stimulate osteoblasts (rare among osteoporosis drugs). Used in severe osteoporosis. Daily injection, limited to 2 years.

Nutrition targets

Calcium: 1,200 mg/day for postmenopausal women and men over 70 (1,000 mg/day for younger adults). Diet first, supplement only as needed. Sources: dairy, fortified plant milks, leafy greens, sardines, tofu set with calcium.

Vitamin D: 800 to 1,000 IU/day for adults over 50, more if deficient (target serum 25-OH D ≥ 30 ng/mL). Required for calcium absorption.

Protein: 1.0 to 1.2 g/kg/day to support bone matrix and muscle mass.

Exercise prescription

Weight-bearing aerobic: 30 min × 4-5 days/week (walking, jogging, dancing, stair climbing). Bone responds to mechanical load.

Resistance training: 2-3 days/week, all major muscle groups. Muscle pull on bone is what drives bone remodeling.

Balance and posture: daily. Tai chi, yoga, balance drills. Reduces fall risk.

Avoid: high-impact activities for patients with severe osteoporosis or vertebral fractures (e.g., heavy spinal flexion).

Fall prevention

Most osteoporotic fractures happen during falls. Comprehensive fall prevention includes: home hazard removal (rugs, cords, poor lighting), vision check, medication review (sedating drugs, polypharmacy), footwear, vitamin D adequacy, and assistive devices when appropriate.

Synthesis question · Most weighted

Bone is constantly being remodeled by osteoblasts (which build) and osteoclasts (which break down). In healthy adults, these cells are in balance. In postmenopausal osteoporosis, the balance shifts.

In four to six sentences, explain why bisphosphonates are first-line for postmenopausal osteoporosis but problematic for younger women planning pregnancy. Your answer must integrate:

(1) what happens to estrogen at menopause and how that disrupts the osteoblast-osteoclast balance, (2) the specific cellular target of bisphosphonates, (3) why this restores balance in the postmenopausal patient, (4) the half-life property that makes bisphosphonates problematic in pregnancy planning, (5) what risk this poses to a developing fetus.
