Skin Architecture

The skin is the largest organ in the body. It uses all four tissues from Week 1, organized into layers that each do a specific job.

▾
Retrieve Layers, strata, cell types
  1. 1.
    Name the three major layers of skin from superficial to deep, and identify the dominant tissue type of each.
    Epidermis (stratified squamous epithelium, avascular, regenerative). Dermis (dense irregular connective tissue, vascularized, contains accessory structures). Hypodermis or subcutaneous layer (loose connective tissue and adipose, anchors skin and stores fat). The hypodermis is technically not part of the skin but is included clinically.
  2. 2.
    List the five strata of the epidermis from deep to superficial, and note where the stratum lucidum is found.
    Deep to superficial: stratum basale, spinosum, granulosum, lucidum, corneum. The stratum lucidum is found only in thick skin (palms and soles). Thin skin has only four strata. Mnemonic: "Bears Smell Garbage Like Cabbage."
  3. 3.
    Name the four cell types found in the epidermis and the function of each.
    Keratinocytes (most abundant, produce keratin, form the protective barrier). Melanocytes (produce melanin in stratum basale, transfer to keratinocytes for UV protection). Langerhans cells (dendritic immune cells, antigen presentation in stratum spinosum). Merkel cells (associated with sensory nerve endings in stratum basale, fine touch).
  4. 4.
    Distinguish thick skin from thin skin in terms of structure, location, and function.
    Thick skin: all 5 strata including stratum lucidum, very thick stratum corneum, no hair follicles. Found on palms, soles. Built for friction resistance. Thin skin: 4 strata (no lucidum), thinner stratum corneum, contains hair follicles. Covers most of the body. Built for sensation and flexibility.

Skin in Homeostasis: Functions, Glands, Burns

Skin is not just a wrapper. It thermoregulates, holds in water, makes vitamin D, defends against pathogens, and tells you when something is sharp or hot. When skin fails (a burn), all of these functions fail at once.

▾
Retrieve Functions and accessory structures
  1. 1.
    List six functions of skin and pair each with the structure or feature responsible.
    Protection: stratum corneum, melanin, sebum. Thermoregulation: sweat glands, dermal blood vessels, hair. Sensation: Merkel cells, free nerve endings, encapsulated receptors. Vitamin D synthesis: 7-dehydrocholesterol activated by UV in epidermis. Excretion: sweat releases small amounts of urea, salts, water. Immunity: Langerhans cells, antimicrobial sebum, intact barrier.
  2. 2.
    Compare eccrine and apocrine sweat glands in location, secretion, and activation.
    Eccrine: distributed everywhere, especially palms, soles, forehead. Secretes watery sweat directly to skin surface. Active for thermoregulation, triggered by heat. Apocrine: located in axilla, groin, areolae. Secretes thicker, lipid-rich fluid into hair follicles. Activated at puberty by hormones, responsive to emotional stress (not heat). Body odor results from bacterial breakdown of apocrine secretions.
  3. 3.
    How does the skin thermoregulate when the body is too hot? When too cold?
    Too hot: dermal arterioles dilate (vasodilation, flushed appearance, more heat radiates from surface). Eccrine sweat glands activate (evaporation removes heat). Too cold: dermal arterioles constrict (vasoconstriction, pale appearance, less heat lost). Arrector pili contract (goosebumps, raises hair to trap air, vestigial in humans). Skeletal muscle shivering generates metabolic heat.
  4. 4.
    Define first, second, and third degree burns in terms of which layers are affected and the typical clinical presentation.
    First degree: epidermis only. Red, painful, dry, no blisters. Heals in days (stratum basale intact). Sunburn is the typical example. Second degree: epidermis and part of dermis. Red, painful, blistered, weeping. Heals in 1 to 3 weeks if accessory structures survive. Third degree: full thickness through dermis, often into hypodermis. Charred or white, painless (nerves destroyed), dry, leathery. Cannot regenerate from skin alone, requires grafting.

Bone Tissue Architecture

Bone is living tissue. It has cells, blood supply, and a metabolically active matrix that responds to mechanical stress and hormonal signals. The osteon is the structural unit of compact bone and the central concept of this section.

▾
Retrieve Compact and spongy bone, cells, osteon
  1. 1.
    Compare compact bone and spongy bone in terms of structure, location within a bone, and function.
    Compact (cortical) bone: dense, organized into osteons, forms the outer shell of all bones. Provides strength against torsion and bending. Spongy (cancellous) bone: open lattice of trabeculae, no osteons, found in bone interiors and at epiphyses. Provides strength with reduced weight, houses red bone marrow, allows directional remodeling along stress lines.
  2. 2.
    Name the four bone cell types and the function of each. Note which one comes from a different lineage than the others.
    Osteogenic (osteoprogenitor) cells: stem cells in periosteum and endosteum, divide to produce osteoblasts. Osteoblasts: bone-forming cells, secrete osteoid matrix and trigger calcification. Osteocytes: mature osteoblasts trapped in matrix, maintain bone tissue and detect mechanical stress. Osteoclasts: large multinucleated cells from monocyte (white blood cell) lineage, resorb bone via acid and enzymes. The first three are mesenchymal; osteoclasts are hematopoietic.
  3. 3.
    List the components of an osteon (Haversian system) and describe the path of nutrients from blood to osteocyte.
    Components: central (Haversian) canal (contains blood vessel and nerve), concentric lamellae (rings of mineralized matrix), lacunae (small spaces housing osteocytes between lamellae), canaliculi (tiny channels connecting lacunae to each other and to the central canal). Path: blood enters the central canal → nutrients diffuse through canaliculi → reach osteocytes in lacunae. Perforating (Volkmann) canals connect adjacent osteons and link the central canals to the periosteum.
  4. 4.
    What are the periosteum and endosteum, and what is the role of each?
    Periosteum: double-layered membrane covering the outside of every bone except where joints articulate. Outer fibrous layer anchors tendons and ligaments. Inner cellular layer contains osteogenic cells and osteoblasts. Endosteum: thinner membrane lining the medullary cavity and the spaces of spongy bone. Contains osteogenic cells and osteoclasts. Both membranes are essential for bone growth, repair, and remodeling.

Focus · OpenStax 6.4, 6.5

Bone Formation, Remodeling, and Fractures

Bones rebuild themselves continuously. The same cells that make bone in development repair fractures in adulthood. Calcium homeostasis runs through this section because bone is the body's calcium reservoir.

▾
Retrieve Ossification, repair, calcium control
  1. 1.
    Compare intramembranous and endochondral ossification. Which bones form by each process?
    Intramembranous ossification: bone forms directly from mesenchymal connective tissue, no cartilage intermediate. Produces flat bones of the skull, the mandible, and most of the clavicle. Endochondral ossification: bone replaces a hyaline cartilage model. Produces all long bones and most of the rest of the skeleton. Continues at the epiphyseal plate (growth plate) until adolescence, when the plate ossifies and longitudinal growth ends.
  2. 2.
    Name the four stages of bone repair after a fracture, in order.
    (1) Hematoma formation: blood clot forms at fracture site, inflammation begins, dead bone tissue is cleared. (2) Fibrocartilaginous (soft) callus formation: fibroblasts and chondroblasts deposit collagen and cartilage to bridge the fracture. (3) Bony (hard) callus formation: osteoblasts replace the soft callus with woven bone over several weeks. (4) Bone remodeling: osteoclasts and osteoblasts reshape the callus into mature compact and spongy bone, often months to years.
  3. 3.
    Explain how parathyroid hormone (PTH) and calcitonin regulate blood calcium.
    PTH: released by the parathyroid glands when blood Ca²⁺ falls. Stimulates osteoclasts (bone resorption releases Ca²⁺), increases renal Ca²⁺ reabsorption, and activates vitamin D for intestinal Ca²⁺ absorption. Net effect: raises blood calcium. Calcitonin: released by thyroid C cells when blood Ca²⁺ is high. Inhibits osteoclasts and promotes osteoblast deposition of Ca²⁺ into bone. Net effect: lowers blood calcium. The two hormones form a classic antagonistic feedback pair around the calcium set point.
  4. 4.
    State Wolff's law.
    Wolff's law: bone remodels in response to the mechanical loads placed on it. Bone exposed to greater stress becomes denser and stronger; bone with reduced stress loses density. This explains why weight-bearing exercise builds bone, why dominant arms have denser bone than the non-dominant arm, and why astronauts and bedridden patients lose bone mass.

Focus · OpenStax 9.1

Joint Classification

Joints are classified two ways: by structure (what they are made of) and by function (how much they move). Most exam questions ask you to do both for the same joint.

▾
Retrieve Structural and functional categories
  1. 1.
    Name the three structural categories of joints and give one example of each.
    Fibrous: bones connected by dense connective tissue, no joint cavity. Example: skull sutures, gomphoses (teeth in sockets), syndesmoses (distal tib-fib). Cartilaginous: bones connected by cartilage, no cavity. Example: pubic symphysis, intervertebral discs, epiphyseal plates. Synovial: bones separated by a fluid-filled cavity enclosed in a capsule. Example: knee, shoulder, elbow, hip. Most joints in the body are synovial.
  2. 2.
    Name the three functional categories of joints based on the degree of movement allowed.
    Synarthrosis: immovable. Example: skull sutures, gomphoses. Amphiarthrosis: slightly movable. Example: pubic symphysis, intervertebral discs. Diarthrosis: freely movable. All synovial joints are diarthroses.
  3. 3.
    List the six structural components of a typical synovial joint.
    (1) Articular cartilage (hyaline, covers bone ends, low-friction surface). (2) Joint capsule (outer fibrous + inner synovial membrane). (3) Synovial fluid (lubricates, nourishes cartilage, removes debris). (4) Joint cavity (potential space inside capsule). (5) Reinforcing ligaments (bone-to-bone, restrict abnormal motion). (6) Sensory nerves and blood vessels (in the capsule and surrounding ligaments). Some synovial joints also have menisci, fat pads, or bursae.
  4. 4.
    Name the six subtypes of synovial joints, the type of motion each allows, and one example of each.
    Plane (gliding): flat surfaces slide. Intercarpal joints. Hinge: motion in one plane, like a door. Elbow, knee. Pivot: rotation around an axis. Atlantoaxial (C1 on C2), proximal radioulnar. Condyloid (ellipsoid): two planes, no rotation. Wrist (radiocarpal), metacarpophalangeal joints. Saddle: two planes plus limited rotation. First carpometacarpal (thumb base). Ball-and-socket: three planes, full range of motion. Shoulder, hip.

Muscle Tissue

Three muscle types do three different jobs. Skeletal moves bones. Cardiac pumps blood. Smooth lines hollow organs. The sarcomere is the contractile unit of striated muscle and the foundation for everything that follows in muscle physiology.

▾
Retrieve Three muscle types and the sarcomere
  1. 1.
    Compare the three muscle types on these features: control, striations, nuclei per cell, cell shape, and one location.
    Skeletal: voluntary, striated, multinucleated, long cylindrical fibers, attaches to bones. Cardiac: involuntary, striated, usually one (sometimes two) central nuclei, branched cells joined by intercalated discs, found only in heart. Smooth: involuntary, non-striated, single central nucleus, spindle-shaped cells, walls of hollow organs (GI tract, vessels, uterus, bladder).
  2. 2.
    Define a sarcomere and name its boundaries.
    A sarcomere is the basic contractile unit of striated muscle, defined as the region between two adjacent Z-discs (Z-lines). Each sarcomere contains overlapping thick (myosin) and thin (actin) filaments arranged in a precise pattern that produces the striated appearance.
  3. 3.
    Identify each band and zone of the sarcomere: A-band, I-band, H-zone, M-line.
    A-band: dark band, length of thick (myosin) filaments. Includes regions where thin and thick overlap. Does not change length during contraction. I-band: light band, contains only thin filaments. Spans across the Z-line between adjacent sarcomeres. Shortens during contraction. H-zone: central region of A-band containing only thick filaments. Shortens during contraction. M-line: middle of the sarcomere, anchors thick filaments.
  4. 4.
    State the sliding filament theory in one sentence and identify the role of calcium.
    Sliding filament theory: muscle contracts when myosin heads pull thin (actin) filaments toward the M-line, shortening each sarcomere. The thick and thin filaments themselves do not change length. Calcium released from the sarcoplasmic reticulum binds troponin on the thin filament, which moves tropomyosin off the actin binding sites, exposing them for myosin attachment. No calcium, no cross-bridge formation, no contraction.
