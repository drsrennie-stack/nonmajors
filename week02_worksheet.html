02

Build · Skin Cross-Section

Draw a labeled cross-section of skin

A drawing without labels is a sketch. Labels are required for credit.

Build

Build task. Draw a cross-section of thin skin showing all three layers. Label all of the following: epidermis (with at least three of the four strata named), dermis (with papillary and reticular regions), hypodermis, and at least three accessory structures from this list: hair follicle, sebaceous gland, eccrine sweat gland, sensory receptor, blood vessel, arrector pili muscle.

Rubric: Three layers in correct order. Three or more strata of epidermis named. Three or more accessory structures labeled in their correct layer.

03

Build & Apply · Bone Tissue

The osteon and bone cell behavior

The osteon is the structural unit of compact bone. Master it now and bone remodeling, fractures, and calcium homeostasis all become easier to reason through.

Build

Build task. Draw a single osteon in cross-section. Label all of the following: central (Haversian) canal, concentric lamellae, lacunae, canaliculi, osteocyte, and a perforating (Volkmann) canal connecting to an adjacent osteon.

Rubric: All six components present and labeled. Concentric ring structure visible. Canaliculi shown connecting lacunae.

Apply

Apply task. a. A postmenopausal patient is diagnosed with osteoporosis (osteoclast activity exceeds osteoblast activity). Predict the consequences for bone density, the osteon structure, and fracture risk.

Apply

Apply task. b. Cartilage is avascular and heals slowly. Bone is highly vascular and heals relatively well. Use the structure of the osteon to explain why bone has a built-in advantage in repair.

Apply · Ossification, Fractures, Calcium

Bone formation, repair, and calcium control

Fracture types are core content for Lab Exam 1. The calcium homeostasis cascade returns later in the semester with the parathyroid gland.

Apply

Apply task. a. A 7-year-old fractures the radius and is healed in three weeks. A 78-year-old fractures the same bone and the cast stays on for three months. Give two anatomical or physiological reasons for the difference.

Apply

Match task. b. Match each fracture type to the correct description by writing the matching letter in each row.

Fracture type Letter Description
Greenstick A. Bone breaks through skin, high infection risk
Comminuted B. Break runs perpendicular to long axis
Compound (open) C. Bone shatters into three or more pieces
Spiral D. Common in vertebrae of patients with osteoporosis
Transverse E. Incomplete break, common in children
Compression F. Caused by rotational force, twists around bone
Apply

Apply task. c. A patient's blood calcium falls to 7.5 mg/dL (low). Trace the hormonal cascade that should restore calcium to the normal range. Name the hormone, the gland, the target tissues, and the net effect.

05

Build & Apply · Joints

Synovial joint structure and joint classification

Most exam questions ask you to classify a joint by both structure and function, then name the synovial subtype. Practice the dual classification.

Build

Build task. Draw a generalized synovial joint with two articulating bones. Label all of the following: articular cartilage, joint capsule (outer fibrous and inner synovial layers), synovial fluid, joint cavity, and a reinforcing ligament.

Rubric: Two bones meeting clearly drawn. All five components labeled. Capsule layers distinguished.

Apply

Apply task. a. Classify each of the following joints by structure, function, and (if synovial) the synovial subtype: the elbow, the cranial sutures, the pubic symphysis, and the shoulder.

Apply

Apply task. b. A patient with rheumatoid arthritis has chronically inflamed synovial membranes. Predict three changes in joint function and explain each, using the components of a synovial joint.

06

Build & Apply · Muscle Tissue

The sarcomere and muscle type comparison

The sarcomere is the foundation of all striated muscle physiology. Get it right now and cardiac and skeletal physiology later in the semester run smoothly.

Build

Build task. Draw a sarcomere at rest showing the full overlap pattern of thick and thin filaments. Label all of the following: Z-line (both ends), thick filament (myosin), thin filament (actin), A-band, I-band, H-zone, and M-line.

Rubric: Z-lines define the sarcomere. Thick and thin filaments correctly placed. All four bands and lines labeled in their correct location.

Apply

Apply task. a. Compare skeletal, cardiac, and smooth muscle on the following five features: control (voluntary or involuntary), striations, number of nuclei per cell, cell shape, and one anatomical location. Use a paragraph or a small table.

Apply

Apply task. b. A neurotoxin blocks calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum in a skeletal muscle. Predict what happens to the contraction. Use the sliding filament theory and the role of troponin and tropomyosin in your explanation.

Apply

Apply task. c. During sarcomere contraction the I-band and H-zone shorten but the A-band stays the same length. Explain why, using the sliding filament model.
