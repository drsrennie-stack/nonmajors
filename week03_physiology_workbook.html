Physiology · Lab Skill 1

Interpret oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) curves

Read 2-hour glucose curves and classify glucose tolerance: normal, impaired (IGT), Type 2 diabetes, or gestational diabetes.

OGTT basics

The oral glucose tolerance test measures blood glucose at fasting (time 0) and 2 hours after the patient drinks 75 g of glucose. The shape of the curve and the 2-hour value tell you how well the pancreas can produce insulin and how well tissues can use it.

Normal: fasting < 100 mg/dL, 2-hour < 140 mg/dL. Beta cells respond fast, peripheral cells take up glucose efficiently.

Impaired Glucose Tolerance (IGT, prediabetes): fasting 100-125 mg/dL OR 2-hour 140-199 mg/dL. Beta cells can still respond but insulin sensitivity is decreased.

Type 2 Diabetes: fasting ≥ 126 mg/dL OR 2-hour ≥ 200 mg/dL. Beta cell dysfunction plus insulin resistance.

Gestational Diabetes (GDM, 1-step 75 g): any one of: fasting ≥ 92 mg/dL, 1-hour ≥ 180 mg/dL, 2-hour ≥ 153 mg/dL. Placental hormones cause insulin resistance.

HbA1c is also commonly used to diagnose diabetes (≥ 6.5%) and prediabetes (5.7-6.4%). It reflects average glucose over the prior 2-3 months.

Physiology · Lab Skill 2

Valsalva maneuver and the four phases of the autonomic response

Predict the heart rate and blood pressure changes during the four classic phases of the Valsalva response.

The Valsalva maneuver

The patient takes a deep breath and bears down (forced expiration against a closed glottis) for about 15 seconds. This raises intrathoracic pressure, which has predictable cascading effects on venous return, blood pressure, and heart rate. Real cardiologists use this at the bedside to test autonomic function and to terminate certain arrhythmias.

The four phases (in order)

Phase I (initial strain): intrathoracic pressure rises sharply. The aorta is mechanically squeezed, transiently raising blood pressure. Lasts about 1-2 seconds.

Phase II (sustained strain): high intrathoracic pressure compresses the vena cava, dropping venous return to the right heart. Stroke volume falls, blood pressure starts dropping. Baroreceptors detect the drop and trigger a sympathetic surge: heart rate rises, vasoconstriction begins. Lasts the rest of the strain (~10-15 seconds).

Phase III (release): patient releases the breath. Intrathoracic pressure drops. Briefly, before venous return rebounds, blood pressure dips further.

Phase IV (overshoot): venous return surges back into a peripherally vasoconstricted system. Blood pressure overshoots normal. Baroreceptors detect the overshoot and trigger a parasympathetic response: heart rate drops below baseline (overshoot bradycardia).

Physiology · Lab Skill 3

Cold pressor test

Predict the cardiovascular response to immersion of the hand in ice water and identify the receptor pathway.

The cold pressor test

The patient immerses one hand (up to the wrist) in ice water (0-4°C) for 60-90 seconds. Cold and pain receptors in the skin trigger a strong sympathetic response. This is a classic test of sympathetic reactivity and is also a research tool for studying pain and stress.

The expected response in a healthy adult: blood pressure rises 15-20 mmHg systolic within the first minute. Heart rate may rise mildly or stay flat (not the dominant feature). The response should resolve within 1-2 minutes after rewarming.

Physiology · Clinical Reasoning Cases

Apply OGTT and ANS testing to real patient scenarios

Each case integrates endocrine pancreas physiology with autonomic nervous system function.

Case 1

The newly diagnosed Type 2 diabetic

A 52-year-old woman with BMI 32 comes in for a routine physical. She reports increased thirst over the past 6 months and getting up twice a night to urinate. Her fasting glucose is 142 mg/dL. HbA1c is 7.8%. She has no prior diabetes history.

  1. Classify her diagnosis based on the criteria from Section 01.
  2. Estimate her average blood glucose using the HbA1c formula. Show your work.
  3. Explain her polyuria and polydipsia at the cellular level. Why does high blood glucose cause her to urinate frequently? (Reference renal glucose threshold and osmotic diuresis.)
  4. Beta cells and alpha cells of the islet have different roles in T2DM. Predict whether each is functioning normally, hyperactive, or hypoactive in this patient. Reference the Week 3 Anatomy Workbook islet labeling.
Case 2

The diabetic with autonomic neuropathy

A 64-year-old man with 18-year history of Type 2 diabetes (HbA1c 8.9% over the past year) presents with episodes of dizziness when standing. His cardiologist orders bedside ANS testing.

Findings: When the patient performs a Valsalva maneuver, his heart rate barely changes during Phase II (no sympathetic surge) and there is NO bradycardic overshoot in Phase IV. When he stands from supine, his BP drops 25 mmHg systolic but his HR only rises 4 bpm.

  1. What is happening to his autonomic nervous system? Be specific about which ANS branches are damaged.
  2. How does chronic hyperglycemia damage autonomic nerves? (Reference sorbitol pathway, glycation, vasa nervorum injury, or oxidative stress, any one is enough.)
  3. Why is his HR response to standing inadequate? Trace the failed baroreflex pathway from receptor to heart rate change.
  4. What sudden-death risk does diabetic autonomic neuropathy carry? (Hint: silent MI, abnormal HRV, prolonged QT.)
Case 3

The pregnant patient with gestational diabetes

A 30-year-old woman, 28 weeks pregnant, completes routine OGTT screening. Fasting 95 mg/dL, 1-hour 188 mg/dL, 2-hour 162 mg/dL.

  1. Does she meet criteria for gestational diabetes? Which value(s) qualify?
  2. Explain why pregnancy promotes insulin resistance. Reference placental hormones (specifically: human placental lactogen, cortisol, growth hormone, progesterone).
  3. What is the likely course after delivery? Will she still have diabetes? What is her lifetime risk for Type 2?
  4. What are the fetal risks of uncontrolled gestational diabetes? Name at least three.
