Neuron Structure & Glial Support

Neurons are the body's fast signaling cells. They receive, integrate, and transmit information through electrical and chemical signals. Glial cells outnumber neurons ten to one and provide essential support.

▾
RetrieveNeurons and glia
  1. 1.
    Name the three functional types of neurons and the role of each.
    Sensory (afferent): carry information from sensory receptors to the CNS. Motor (efferent): carry commands from the CNS to effectors (muscles and glands). Interneurons: located entirely within the CNS, integrate sensory input with motor output. By far the most numerous type.
  2. 2.
    List the parts of a typical neuron and the role of each.
    Dendrites: branched extensions that receive incoming signals. Soma (cell body): contains the nucleus and most organelles, integrates incoming signals. Axon hillock: trigger zone where the action potential is initiated if threshold is reached. Axon: conducts the action potential away from the cell body. Myelin sheath: insulates the axon, increases conduction speed. Axon terminals: release neurotransmitter at the synapse.
  3. 3.
    Name the six types of glial cells, their location (CNS or PNS), and the function of each.
    CNS: Astrocytes (form blood-brain barrier, provide structural and metabolic support). Microglia (immune defense, phagocytic). Oligodendrocytes (myelinate multiple axons in CNS). Ependymal cells (line ventricles, produce and circulate CSF). PNS: Schwann cells (each one myelinates one segment of one axon). Satellite cells (surround and support neuron cell bodies in ganglia).
  4. 4.
    State the resting membrane potential and explain what maintains it.
    Resting membrane potential is approximately -70 mV (cell interior negative compared to exterior). Maintained by: (1) the Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase pump (3 Na⁺ out, 2 K⁺ in per ATP), creating concentration gradients; (2) selective K⁺ leak channels that allow K⁺ to diffuse out, leaving the inside more negative; and (3) large negatively charged proteins trapped inside the cell.

Action Potentials and Synaptic Transmission

An action potential is an all-or-nothing electrical event that travels down an axon. At the end, it triggers a chemical handoff at the synapse. Two events, both essential for the nervous system to do anything.

▾
RetrieveAction potential mechanics
  1. 1.
    Describe the five phases of an action potential, including which channels open or close and which ions move at each phase.
    1. Resting state (-70 mV): voltage-gated channels closed. 2. Depolarization: stimulus reaches threshold (~-55 mV), voltage-gated Na⁺ channels open, Na⁺ rushes in, membrane reverses to ~+30 mV. 3. Repolarization: Na⁺ channels inactivate, voltage-gated K⁺ channels open, K⁺ rushes out, membrane returns toward negative. 4. Hyperpolarization: K⁺ channels stay open briefly, membrane drops below -70 mV. 5. Return to rest: K⁺ channels close, Na⁺/K⁺ pump restores ion gradients.
  2. 2.
    Define threshold, the all-or-nothing principle, and the absolute and relative refractory periods.
    Threshold: approximately -55 mV. The membrane potential at which voltage-gated Na⁺ channels open in sufficient numbers to trigger an AP. All-or-nothing: either the AP fires fully or it does not fire at all. There is no partial AP. Absolute refractory period: no AP can be triggered (Na⁺ channels are inactivated). Sets the maximum firing rate. Relative refractory period: a stronger-than-normal stimulus is required because the membrane is hyperpolarized below the resting state.
  3. 3.
    Define saltatory conduction and explain why myelinated axons conduct faster than unmyelinated axons.
    In myelinated axons, the action potential "jumps" from one node of Ranvier to the next, skipping the myelinated segments. This is saltatory conduction. It is faster than continuous conduction because depolarization only has to occur at the nodes, where ion channels are concentrated. Unmyelinated axons require AP regeneration along the entire length, which is slower.
  4. 4.
    List the steps of synaptic transmission from action potential arrival to postsynaptic effect.
    (1) AP arrives at axon terminal. (2) Voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open, Ca²⁺ enters terminal. (3) Ca²⁺ triggers vesicle fusion with the presynaptic membrane. (4) Neurotransmitter released into the synaptic cleft. (5) NT binds to postsynaptic receptors. (6) Receptor activation produces an EPSP (excitatory) or IPSP (inhibitory). (7) NT is removed by reuptake, enzymatic degradation, or diffusion away.

CNS Anatomy: Brain & Spinal Cord

Knowing the major brain regions is the foundation for neurological assessment. Damage to a specific region produces a specific deficit. The same is true for the spinal cord.

▾
RetrieveBrain regions and spinal cord
  1. 1.
    Name the major brain regions and the core function of each.
    Cerebrum: higher cognition, sensory perception, voluntary motor. Divided into four lobes (frontal, parietal, temporal, occipital). Cerebellum: motor coordination, balance, fine movement. Brainstem (midbrain, pons, medulla): vital functions including breathing, heart rate, swallow, eye reflexes. Diencephalon: thalamus (sensory relay) and hypothalamus (autonomic control, endocrine output, homeostasis).
  2. 2.
    Match each cerebral lobe to its primary functions.
    Frontal: voluntary motor (primary motor cortex), executive function, personality, Broca's area (speech production). Parietal: somatosensory cortex, spatial awareness, body schema. Temporal: hearing, memory, language comprehension (Wernicke's area). Occipital: vision (primary visual cortex).
  3. 3.
    Describe a cross-section of the spinal cord. Where is gray matter, where is white matter, and what enters and exits at the dorsal and ventral roots?
    Gray matter is in the center (H-shaped or butterfly-shaped) and contains cell bodies and synapses. White matter is on the outside and contains myelinated tracts. Dorsal (posterior) horns: receive sensory input. Ventral (anterior) horns: contain motor neuron cell bodies. Dorsal root: sensory neurons enter the cord (cell bodies in dorsal root ganglion, just outside the cord). Ventral root: motor neurons exit the cord. The dorsal and ventral roots merge into a mixed spinal nerve.
  4. 4.
    Name the three meninges from outer to inner, and trace the production and circulation of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).
    Meninges from outer to inner: dura mater (tough outer), arachnoid mater (web-like middle, with subarachnoid space below), pia mater (thin inner, hugs brain surface). CSF is made by the choroid plexus in the lateral, third, and fourth ventricles. It flows: lateral ventricles → third ventricle → cerebral aqueduct → fourth ventricle → out into the subarachnoid space → reabsorbed by arachnoid villi into venous blood.

Endocrine Signaling Basics

Hormones are chemical messengers that travel in blood. They are slower than nerves but reach every cell in the body. Speed and reach are tradeoffs in how the body sends signals.

▾
RetrieveHormone classes and signaling
  1. 1.
    Name the three classes of hormones, an example of each, and the key chemical feature.
    Amine: derived from an amino acid (tyrosine or tryptophan). Examples: epinephrine, norepinephrine, thyroid hormones, melatonin. Peptide / protein: chains of amino acids. Examples: insulin, ADH, growth hormone, oxytocin. The largest class. Water-soluble. Steroid: derived from cholesterol. Examples: cortisol, aldosterone, testosterone, estrogen. Lipid-soluble.
  2. 2.
    Explain why steroid hormones cross the cell membrane but peptide hormones do not, and how this affects their mechanism of action.
    Steroids are lipid-soluble and pass directly through the phospholipid bilayer. They bind intracellular receptors (cytoplasmic or nuclear), and the hormone-receptor complex acts on DNA to alter gene transcription. Slow onset, long duration. Peptides are water-soluble and cannot cross the bilayer. They bind surface receptors and trigger intracellular second messenger cascades (cAMP, IP3, etc.) that modify existing proteins. Fast onset, short duration.
  3. 3.
    Describe the hypothalamus-pituitary axis. How are anterior and posterior pituitary different in their relationship to the hypothalamus?
    The hypothalamus sits above the pituitary and controls it. Anterior pituitary: the hypothalamus releases regulating hormones (releasing or inhibiting) into the hypophyseal portal system, which carries them to the anterior pituitary. The anterior pituitary then releases tropic hormones (TSH, ACTH, FSH, LH, GH, prolactin) into general circulation. Posterior pituitary: hormones (ADH and oxytocin) are made by hypothalamic neurons whose axons terminate in the posterior pituitary. The posterior pituitary stores and releases these hormones; it does not synthesize them.
  4. 4.
    Explain how negative feedback maintains hormone set points, using the thyroid axis as an example.
    In the thyroid axis: hypothalamus releases TRH → anterior pituitary releases TSH → thyroid releases T3 and T4. When circulating T3/T4 rises, it inhibits both the hypothalamus (less TRH) and the pituitary (less TSH). With less stimulation, the thyroid releases less hormone, and T3/T4 returns to set point. Most endocrine axes use this same loop design.

Endocrine Pancreas: Insulin, Glucagon, Diabetes

Blood glucose has a tight set point because brain cells depend on it. Insulin and glucagon are antagonists that maintain that set point. Diabetes is what happens when this system fails. The COR for this course names this section as a key clinical anchor.

▾
RetrievePancreas, hormones, diabetes types
  1. 1.
    Name the three pancreatic islet cell types and the hormone each one secretes.
    Alpha cells: secrete glucagon (raises blood glucose). Beta cells: secrete insulin (lowers blood glucose). Most numerous islet cell. Delta cells: secrete somatostatin, which modulates both insulin and glucagon release and inhibits growth hormone. Islets of Langerhans are clusters scattered throughout the exocrine pancreas.
  2. 2.
    List the major actions of insulin and identify when it is released.
    Insulin is released by beta cells when blood glucose rises (after a meal). Actions: (1) increases glucose uptake by skeletal muscle and adipose tissue (via GLUT4 transporters); (2) promotes glycogen synthesis in the liver and muscle; (3) promotes triglyceride synthesis and storage in adipose; (4) promotes protein synthesis. Net effect: lowers blood glucose, builds energy stores. The brain and red blood cells take up glucose without needing insulin.
  3. 3.
    List the major actions of glucagon and identify when it is released.
    Glucagon is released by alpha cells when blood glucose falls (fasting state, between meals). Actions in the liver: (1) glycogenolysis (breakdown of glycogen to glucose); (2) gluconeogenesis (making new glucose from amino acids and lactate); (3) promotes lipolysis in adipose. Net effect: raises blood glucose.
  4. 4.
    Compare Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes in terms of cause, typical age of onset, and treatment.
    Type 1: autoimmune destruction of beta cells. The pancreas makes little or no insulin. Typical onset childhood or adolescence. Always insulin-dependent. High risk of diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) without insulin. Type 2: insulin resistance (target cells respond poorly to insulin), often combined with relative insulin deficiency over time. Typical onset adulthood, strong association with obesity and metabolic syndrome. Initially treated with lifestyle change and oral medications; many patients eventually need insulin.

The Autonomic Nervous System

The ANS controls visceral functions you do not think about. The autonomic-somatic split is COR-named content for this course. Two divisions, one purpose: maintain physiological homeostasis under varying conditions.

▾
RetrieveANS architecture and divisions
  1. 1.
    Compare the somatic nervous system and the autonomic nervous system. This is COR-named content.
    Somatic: voluntary control. Single neuron from CNS to skeletal muscle. ACh at the neuromuscular junction. Always excitatory at the muscle. Autonomic: involuntary control. Two-neuron chain (preganglionic from CNS to ganglion, postganglionic from ganglion to target). Targets smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, glands, and adipose. Can be excitatory or inhibitory depending on the receptor on the target.
  2. 2.
    Compare the anatomical origins of the sympathetic and parasympathetic divisions, including the length of pre- and postganglionic neurons.
    Sympathetic = thoracolumbar: preganglionic neurons originate in T1 to L2 spinal segments. Short preganglionic axons synapse in the sympathetic chain ganglia (alongside spinal cord). Long postganglionic axons travel to targets. Parasympathetic = craniosacral: preganglionic neurons originate in cranial nerves III, VII, IX, X (the vagus is huge, supplying most thoracic and abdominal organs) and sacral segments S2 to S4. Long preganglionic axons travel to ganglia near or within the target organ. Short postganglionic axons.
  3. 3.
    Identify the neurotransmitter at each ANS synapse: preganglionic, sympathetic postganglionic, and parasympathetic postganglionic.
    All preganglionic neurons (sympathetic and parasympathetic) release ACh, which binds nicotinic receptors on the postganglionic neuron. Sympathetic postganglionic releases norepinephrine (NE), which binds adrenergic receptors on the target. Parasympathetic postganglionic releases ACh, which binds muscarinic receptors on the target. Exception: sympathetic postganglionic neurons to sweat glands release ACh (muscarinic), not NE.
  4. 4.
    Compare the effects of sympathetic and parasympathetic stimulation on the heart, lungs, GI tract, pupils, and bladder.
    Heart: sympathetic increases HR and contractility; parasympathetic (vagus) decreases HR. Lungs: sympathetic dilates bronchioles; parasympathetic constricts. GI tract: sympathetic decreases motility and secretion; parasympathetic increases both (rest and digest). Pupils: sympathetic dilates (mydriasis); parasympathetic constricts (miosis). Bladder: sympathetic relaxes the detrusor (retains urine); parasympathetic contracts the detrusor (voiding).
