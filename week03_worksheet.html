How this worksheet works

BIO 304 is an online course. Work through the Week 3 Cognitive Trainer first as your study tool. This worksheet is what you turn in. It captures only the drawings, predictions, and reflections.

How to complete: Print the worksheet. Complete every box by hand in pen or pencil. Scan or photograph the completed pages as a single PDF. Upload that PDF to the Week 3 Worksheet assignment in Canvas.

Drawing tasks (three this week): action potential graph, blood glucose feedback loop, sympathetic vs parasympathetic comparison. Label every component listed in the prompt. An unlabeled drawing is a sketch and loses points.

Two sections cover COR-named content (endocrine pancreas in Section 5, autonomic-somatic split in Section 6). These will be tested directly on Lab Exam 2. Spend extra time on those.

If you cannot print: contact Dr. Rennie or DSPS for an alternative submission format.

02

Build & Apply · Action Potential

The action potential and synaptic transmission

Graphs are required to be labeled. A curve without phase labels and millivolt values is incomplete.

Build

Build task. Draw a graph of an action potential with time on the x-axis and membrane potential (mV) on the y-axis. Mark the threshold (-55 mV), resting potential (-70 mV), and peak (~+30 mV). Label all five phases: resting, depolarization, repolarization, hyperpolarization, return to rest.

Rubric: x-axis and y-axis labeled with units. Threshold and resting potential marked. All five phases labeled at the correct location on the curve.

Apply

Apply task. a. Tetrodotoxin from pufferfish blocks voltage-gated sodium channels. Predict the effect on action potentials, and explain at which phase the block matters most.

Apply

Apply task. b. A toxin blocks voltage-gated calcium channels at axon terminals. The action potential reaches the terminal but no signal crosses to the next neuron. Explain why, using the steps of synaptic transmission.

Apply

Apply task. c. Curare blocks acetylcholine receptors at the neuromuscular junction. Predict the effect on muscle contraction, and explain why the action potential in the motor neuron is unaffected.

Apply · Endocrine Signaling

Hormone classes and feedback

Use the chemical class (steroid, peptide, amine), the receptor location, and the time course in your reasoning.

Apply

Apply task. a. Cortisol (steroid) and insulin (peptide) reach a target cell at the same time. Which produces a measurable effect first, and why? Use the receptor location and mechanism in your answer.

Apply

05

Build & Apply · Endocrine Pancreas COR

Blood glucose regulation and diabetes

COR-named clinical anchor. Insulin, glucagon, and the pancreatic islets show up directly on Lab Exam 2.

Build

Build task. Draw the negative feedback loop that maintains blood glucose. Show both arms (high glucose response and low glucose response). Label the islet cell type for each arm, the hormone released, and the target tissues with their actions. Indicate clearly whether each arm is raising or lowering blood glucose.

Rubric: Both feedback arms shown. Alpha and beta cells correctly identified. Insulin and glucagon correctly assigned. At least one target tissue per arm with the correct action.

Apply

Apply task. a. A patient with Type 1 diabetes forgets to take insulin for two days. Predict their blood glucose level and the insulin/glucagon ratio. What metabolic emergency are they at risk for, and why does it happen specifically in Type 1 and not Type 2?

Apply

Apply task. b. After a high-carbohydrate meal, predict the action of insulin on three target tissues: skeletal muscle, liver, and adipose tissue. Be specific about what happens to glucose in each tissue.

Apply

Apply task. c. A patient has hypoglycemia (blood glucose 50 mg/dL) and is sweaty, tremulous, and confused. For each symptom, identify whether it is due to autonomic activation (sympathetic response to low glucose) or inadequate glucose supply to the brain.
