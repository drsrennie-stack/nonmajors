One neuron, many fibers

Motor unit basics

Motor unit
One motor neuron plus every muscle fiber it innervates.
Small motor unit
Few fibers per neuron, fine control. Extraocular muscles run about 3 to 5 fibers per neuron.
Large motor unit
Thousands of fibers per neuron, gross force. Gastrocnemius runs 1000+ fibers per neuron.
Size principle
Small motor units fire first; larger ones are added as more force is needed.
Recruitment
Activating more motor units to raise total force.
All-or-none (one fiber)
A single fiber contracts maximally or not at all when stimulated.

Built for endurance or for power

Fiber types

Type I, slow oxidative
Red, lots of mitochondria, fatigue-resistant. Built for endurance.
Type IIa, fast oxidative
Intermediate. Uses both aerobic and glycolytic pathways.
Type IIx/b, fast glycolytic
White, anaerobic, fast and powerful, fatigues quickly.

The size principle means slow Type I units are recruited first, which is why easy effort is fatigue-resistant and max effort is not.

Adding twitches together

Twitch and tetanus

Twitch
Response to one action potential. Latent, contraction, then relaxation phases.
Wave summation
A second twitch adds onto the first if it arrives before relaxation finishes.
Unfused tetanus
Rapid stimulation with partial relaxation between twitches.
Fused (complete) tetanus
Stimulation so rapid there is no relaxation. Smooth, sustained contraction.

Force depends on overlap and on movement

Length-tension and contraction types

Optimal length

Maximum cross-bridge overlap gives maximum force.

Too short

Thick filaments collide with the Z discs; force drops.

Too long

Too little overlap to form bridges; force drops.

Isotonic

Muscle changes length, load constant (lifting a weight).

Isometric

Force without a length change (holding a plank).

Concentric / Eccentric

Concentric shortens under load (curl up). Eccentric lengthens under load (lower the curl), which is what makes you sore.

Where the ATP comes from

Fuel for contraction

Direct ATP
The ATP already on hand. Lasts about 2 seconds.
Creatine phosphate
Fast ATP regeneration for about 10 to 15 seconds.
Anaerobic glycolysis
About 30 to 60 seconds. Produces lactate.
Aerobic respiration
Minutes to hours. Needs oxygen and substrate.

From nerve signal to calcium

Excitation-contraction coupling

  1. Action potential arrives at the axon terminal and ACh is released into the synapse.
  2. ACh binds nicotinic receptors on the motor end plate; Na+ enters and the sarcolemma reaches threshold.
  3. The action potential travels along the sarcolemma and down the T-tubules.
  4. DHP and ryanodine receptors open and the SR floods the cytoplasm with Ca2+.
  5. Ca2+ binds troponin, tropomyosin shifts, and the myosin-binding site on actin is exposed.

Four steps that repeat

The cross-bridge cycle

  1. Cross-bridge formation. The energized myosin head binds actin.
  2. Power stroke. The head pivots; the thin filament slides toward the M line; ADP and Pi are released.
  3. Detachment. A new ATP binds the myosin head and it releases actin.
  4. Re-cocking. ATP splits to ADP and Pi; the head re-energizes, ready to bind again.

The result: thin filaments slide toward the M line, Z discs are pulled in, the I band and H zone narrow, and the A band stays the same.

Turning it off, and when it cannot turn off

Relaxation and rigor mortis

Relaxation
The action potential stops, SERCA pumps Ca2+ back into the SR (this needs ATP), tropomyosin re-covers actin, and the sarcomere returns to rest.
Rigor mortis
After death there is no ATP, so myosin cannot release from actin. Cross-bridges lock and the muscle stays rigid.
Why it resolves
Proteolysis: enzymes eventually break down the proteins themselves.

Every step here is a drug target, which is why this cycle is worth knowing cold.
