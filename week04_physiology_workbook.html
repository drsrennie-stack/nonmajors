Physiology · Lab Skill 1

The five components of the bedside neurological exam

Identify and document each component. This is the standard order used in clinical practice.

The five components (in order)

1. Mental status: alert and oriented to person, place, time, situation (A&Ox4). Speech fluency, comprehension, repetition, naming.

2. Cranial nerves: selectively or all 12, depending on context. ER and bedside exams typically test II through XII.

3. Motor system: tone, bulk, strength (MRC 0-5), pronator drift.

4. Sensory system: light touch, pinprick (pain), proprioception, vibration. Sensory dermatomes if focal.

5. Reflexes and coordination: deep tendon reflexes (DTRs) graded 0-4+, Babinski sign, finger-to-nose, heel-to-shin, Romberg, gait.

Document the bedside test for each component below. Be specific about the action you ask the patient to do.

Component Two specific bedside tests for this component
1. Mental status
2. Cranial nerves
3. Motor system
4. Sensory system

Physiology · Lab Skill 2

Medical Research Council (MRC) strength scale

Grade muscle strength from 0 to 5 across patient scenarios.

MRC Strength Scale (0 to 5)

0: No muscle contraction visible at all.

1: Flicker or trace of contraction, no movement at the joint.

2: Active movement only with gravity eliminated (e.g., across a table, not against gravity).

3: Active movement against gravity but not against any added resistance.

4: Active movement against gravity AND against some resistance, but weaker than the other side or expected normal.

5: Normal strength against full resistance.

Many clinicians use 4-, 4, and 4+ to refine grade 4. Document strength bilaterally.

Grade each finding on the MRC scale (0-5). If the finding is in only one limb, also document the contralateral side as expected normal (5/5).

Patient finding My grade Brief reasoning
Patient asked to lift right arm against examiner pushing down. Patient holds firm. Lifts equal to left side.
Patient asked to lift right arm. Lifts off the bed but cannot resist any downward push.
Patient cannot lift right arm against gravity but can slide it across the bed sheet.
You see the right biceps twitch when patient is asked to flex elbow, but no actual movement.
Patient asked to flex right hip against resistance. Lifts leg but is noticeably weaker than left, can be overcome with steady push.
Patient asked to extend right knee. No movement, no contraction visible.

Pronator drift test: patient holds both arms straight out, palms up, eyes closed, for 20-30 seconds. What does it mean if the right arm slowly pronates and drifts down?

Physiology · Lab Skill 3

Deep tendon reflexes (DTRs) and Babinski sign

Grade reflex responses and recognize upper motor neuron findings.

DTR grading scale (0 to 4+)

0: Absent. 1+: Diminished (hypoactive). 2+: Normal. 3+: Brisker than average (hyperactive but no clonus). 4+: Hyperactive with clonus.

Common reflexes tested: biceps (C5-C6), triceps (C7), brachioradialis (C5-C6), patellar / knee jerk (L2-L4), Achilles / ankle jerk (S1-S2).

Babinski sign

Stroke the lateral sole of the foot from heel to little toe, then medially across the ball of the foot.

Normal in adults: toes flex (curl downward, "down-going").

Abnormal (positive Babinski) in adults: big toe extends (dorsiflexes, "up-going") and other toes fan out. This indicates an upper motor neuron lesion.

Babinski is normal in infants under approximately 12 months because their corticospinal tract is not yet fully myelinated.

Grade each DTR finding:

Patient finding Grade (0-4+) Upper motor neuron (UMN) or lower motor neuron (LMN) sign?
Patellar tap produces sharp knee extension, leg swings up vigorously, sustained beating at the ankle when foot is dorsiflexed.
Patellar tap produces brisk knee extension. No clonus.
No response at the patellar tendon despite multiple attempts.
Patellar tap produces a normal swing of the lower leg.

A 45-year-old patient has 3+ DTRs in the right leg, an up-going Babinski on the right, and weakness in the right leg. Are these findings UMN, LMN, or both? Where (anatomically) could the lesion be?

Physiology · Lab Skill 4

Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)

Score eye opening, verbal response, and motor response. Recognize TBI severity.

Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)

Scored on three components: Eye opening (1-4), Verbal response (1-5), Motor response (1-6). Total range: 3 to 15. Higher = better.

Eye opening (E):

4 = Spontaneous · 3 = To speech · 2 = To pain · 1 = None

Verbal response (V):

5 = Oriented · 4 = Confused conversation · 3 = Inappropriate words · 2 = Incomprehensible sounds · 1 = None

Motor response (M):

6 = Obeys commands · 5 = Localizes pain · 4 = Withdraws from pain · 3 = Abnormal flexion (decorticate) · 2 = Abnormal extension (decerebrate) · 1 = None

TBI severity by total GCS:

Mild: 13-15 · Moderate: 9-12 · Severe: 8 or below (intubation typically considered)

Score each patient's GCS by component (E + V + M = Total) and classify the TBI severity.

Patient presentation E V M Total Severity
Patient opens eyes when you walk in. Asks where they are. Follows command to lift left hand.
Patient eyes closed. Opens to loud voice. Mumbles incomprehensibly. Withdraws from sternal rub.
Patient eyes closed. No response to voice. Opens eyes to pinch. No verbal response. Brings hand toward the painful stimulus.
Patient eyes closed, no response to voice or pain. No verbal response. To sternal rub: arms flex at elbow, wrists flex inward (decorticate posturing).
Patient eyes closed, no response. No verbal response. To pain: arms extend rigidly with internal rotation (decerebrate posturing).

What is the difference between decorticate (flexion) and decerebrate (extension) posturing in terms of brain lesion location, and which is more concerning?

Physiology · Clinical Reasoning Cases

Apply the full neuro exam to four patients

Each case integrates cranial nerve assessment, motor and reflex testing, GCS, and stroke recognition.

Case 1

The right MCA stroke

A 68-year-old woman is brought in by family. She was last seen well 2 hours ago. On exam: left facial droop (forehead spared), 0/5 strength in left arm and 2/5 strength in left leg, slurred speech, neglect of left visual field, and gaze deviation to the right. GCS 14 (E4 V4 M6).

  1. Which cerebral artery is most likely affected? Justify based on the deficit pattern.
  2. Why is the forehead spared on the affected side? (Reference CN VII innervation pattern.)
  3. Calculate the time window for tPA. Is she still eligible?
  4. What three additional bedside tests would you do right now to refine the picture?
Case 2

The motorcycle crash

A 24-year-old man, helmeted, struck a vehicle on his motorcycle. EMS finds him unconscious. On scene: eyes closed, opens to painful stimulus, mumbles incomprehensible sounds, decerebrate posturing to sternal rub. Pupils 6 mm bilaterally, sluggishly reactive. BP 158/92, HR 56, irregular respirations.

  1. Calculate his GCS. Classify the TBI severity.
  2. The combination of hypertension, bradycardia, and irregular breathing has a name. What is it called and what does it indicate?
  3. Pupils are 6 mm and sluggish bilaterally. What CN is involved and what does this suggest about brainstem involvement?
  4. What is the most urgent next step in management?
Case 3

The basilar territory ischemia

A 71-year-old man with hypertension and atrial fibrillation comes in 30 minutes after sudden vertigo, double vision, and difficulty walking. Exam: cannot abduct the left eye (eye stays midline when asked to look left), unsteady gait, dysarthria, intact strength bilaterally, intact mental status.

  1. Which cranial nerve is involved on the left? What action does it normally control?
  2. The combination of cranial nerve deficits + cerebellar findings (gait, dysarthria) without major motor weakness points to what arterial territory?
  3. Why does atrial fibrillation predispose to this kind of stroke? (Reference clot origin and embolic path.)
Case 4

The peripheral facial palsy

A 40-year-old healthy woman wakes up with right-sided facial droop. She cannot raise her right eyebrow, cannot close her right eye fully, and her right corner of the mouth droops. Mental status is normal. Strength is 5/5 in all four limbs. No sensory deficits. No speech changes.

  1. Is this a stroke or a peripheral cranial nerve lesion (Bell's palsy)? Explain how the forehead involvement helps you decide.
  2. Which CN is affected, and what is the difference in CN innervation between the upper and lower face that makes this distinction possible?
  3. If this were a stroke, what facial findings would you expect instead?
