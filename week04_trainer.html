Sensory Perception

Sensation starts with a stimulus, gets transduced into electrical signals, and travels to the brain where it becomes perception. The brain interprets the signal, not the stimulus directly.

▾
RetrieveReceptors and pathways
  1. 1.
    Distinguish general senses from special senses, and give two examples of each.
    General senses are distributed throughout the body via receptors in skin, joints, muscles, and organs. Examples: touch, pressure, pain, temperature, vibration, proprioception. Special senses are localized to specific organs in the head, each with a dedicated cranial nerve. Examples: vision (CN II), hearing and equilibrium (CN VIII), taste (CN VII, IX), smell (CN I).
  2. 2.
    Name the five sensory receptor types classified by stimulus modality, and give one example of each.
    Mechanoreceptors: respond to deformation. Example: Pacinian corpuscles for vibration, hair cells in cochlea for sound. Thermoreceptors: respond to temperature. Nociceptors: respond to tissue damage (pain), triggered by chemical, mechanical, or thermal extremes. Photoreceptors: respond to light. Rods and cones in the retina. Chemoreceptors: respond to chemicals. Taste buds, olfactory receptors, blood gas sensors in the carotid bodies.
  3. 3.
    List the steps in a generic sensory pathway from stimulus to conscious perception.
    (1) Stimulus reaches the receptor. (2) Receptor transduces the stimulus into a graded receptor potential. (3) If threshold is reached, an action potential fires in the afferent (sensory) neuron. (4) Signal travels to the spinal cord and ascends in a sensory tract. (5) Synapses in the thalamus (relay station for almost all senses). (6) Relayed to the primary sensory cortex in the postcentral gyrus, where it becomes conscious perception. Exception: olfaction bypasses the thalamus and reaches the cortex directly.
  4. 4.
    Define sensory adaptation. Compare tonic and phasic receptors.
    Sensory adaptation: a decrease in receptor response to a sustained stimulus. Tonic (slow-adapting) receptors keep firing as long as the stimulus is present. Examples: pain, joint position, baroreceptors. Useful when continuous information matters. Phasic (fast-adapting) receptors fire briefly at stimulus onset and then quiet. Examples: touch, smell, pressure of clothing. Useful for detecting change rather than steady state.
ApplySensory pathway logic
a. Why does the smell of a room fade quickly when you walk in, but pain from a sprained ankle persists for days? Use phasic and tonic receptors in your answer.

Special Senses: Eye and Ear

The eye and ear dominate this week's lab. You will be expected to identify structures by name and explain their function. Both organs convert physical energy (light, sound, motion) into neural signals.

▾
RetrieveEye and ear anatomy
  1. 1.
    Trace the path of light through the eye from the cornea to the retina.
    Light passes through: cornea (refracts light) → aqueous humor (anterior chamber) → pupil (controlled by iris) → lens (further refracts, accommodates by changing shape) → vitreous humor (posterior chamber, gel) → strikes the retina, where photoreceptors (rods and cones) transduce light. Signal travels via bipolar and ganglion cells to the optic nerve (CN II).
  2. 2.
    Distinguish the roles of rods and cones in the retina.
    Rods: high sensitivity, work in low light, do not detect color. Concentrated in peripheral retina. Cones: require bright light, detect color (three subtypes for red, green, blue). Concentrated in the fovea (sharpest vision). The optic disc contains no photoreceptors and is the physiological blind spot where the optic nerve exits.
  3. 3.
    Trace the path of sound through the ear from the auricle to the cochlea.
    Sound waves enter the auricle (pinna) → travel down the external auditory canal → vibrate the tympanic membrane (eardrum). The vibration crosses three middle ear ossicles: malleus → incus → stapes. The stapes pushes on the oval window, sending pressure waves into the fluid-filled cochlea. Hair cells in the organ of Corti transduce vibration into neural signal carried by CN VIII.
  4. 4.
    Distinguish the cochlea from the semicircular canals in the inner ear.
    Cochlea: spiral-shaped, contains hair cells in the organ of Corti, transduces sound. Semicircular canals (and the vestibule): three perpendicular canals filled with fluid that move with head rotation, plus the utricle and saccule that detect linear acceleration and head position relative to gravity. Both are innervated by branches of CN VIII (vestibulocochlear nerve).

Central Processing: Sensory and Motor Cortex

The brain organizes sensory and motor information topographically. Body regions with the highest sensitivity or finest motor control take up the most cortical space, regardless of their actual size.

▾
RetrieveCortex location and the homunculus
  1. 1.
    Locate the primary somatosensory cortex and the primary motor cortex. Which lobes are they in, and which gyri?
    Primary somatosensory cortex: postcentral gyrus, parietal lobe (just behind the central sulcus). Receives general sensory information after thalamic relay. Primary motor cortex: precentral gyrus, frontal lobe (just in front of the central sulcus). Origin of voluntary motor commands. The two strips run parallel along the central sulcus.
  2. 2.
    Define the cortical homunculus and explain why it is distorted.
    The homunculus is a distorted body map representing how much cortical area is devoted to each body region. Areas with high sensitivity or fine motor control (hands, lips, face, tongue) take up disproportionately large cortical territory. Areas with coarser sensation or movement (trunk, legs) take up small territory. The distortion reflects functional importance, not anatomical size. Each hemisphere maps the contralateral body.
  3. 3.
    Define an association area, and give one example.
    An association area integrates information beyond what the primary sensory or motor cortex does alone. Example: the visual association cortex (in the occipital and temporal lobes near primary V1) integrates color, motion, and shape into recognition of objects and faces. Damage here can produce visual agnosia: the patient sees light and shapes but cannot recognize a familiar face or object.
  4. 4.
    Identify Broca's area and Wernicke's area, and the deficit produced by damage to each.
    Broca's area: frontal lobe (typically left hemisphere). Damage produces expressive aphasia: comprehension preserved but speech is halting, effortful, agrammatical. The patient knows what they want to say but cannot produce fluent speech. Wernicke's area: temporal lobe (typically left hemisphere). Damage produces receptive aphasia: speech is fluent but nonsensical, comprehension impaired. The patient does not realize they are not making sense.
ApplyLocalize the deficit

The Twelve Cranial Nerves

Twelve paired cranial nerves emerge from the brain. Some are pure sensory, some pure motor, some mixed. Knowing them is the basis of the cranial nerve exam in lab.

▾
RetrieveThe twelve cranial nerves
  1. 1.
    List the twelve cranial nerves in order from I to XII. Use a mnemonic if it helps.
    I Olfactory, II Optic, III Oculomotor, IV Trochlear, V Trigeminal, VI Abducens, VII Facial, VIII Vestibulocochlear, IX Glossopharyngeal, X Vagus, XI Accessory, XII Hypoglossal. Common mnemonic: "On Old Olympus' Towering Tops, A Finn And German Viewed Some Hops."
  2. 2.
    Classify each cranial nerve as Sensory, Motor, or Both. Use a mnemonic if helpful.
    "Some Say Marry Money But My Brother Says Big Brains Matter More." Sensory only: I, II, VIII. Motor only: III, IV, VI, XI, XII. Both: V, VII, IX, X.
  3. 3.
    Match the major function of CN II, V, VII, VIII, X, and XII.
    CN II Optic: vision. CN V Trigeminal: sensation from the face (three branches V1, V2, V3) and motor to muscles of mastication. CN VII Facial: motor to muscles of facial expression, taste from anterior 2/3 of tongue, parasympathetic to salivary and tear glands. CN VIII Vestibulocochlear: hearing and equilibrium. CN X Vagus: parasympathetic to most thoracic and abdominal organs, also motor to pharynx/larynx and sensory from viscera. CN XII Hypoglossal: motor to tongue muscles.
  4. 4.
    Describe one bedside test for each of the following cranial nerves: II, III, V, VII, VIII, XII.
    CN II: visual acuity (Snellen chart) and visual fields by confrontation. CN III: pupillary light reflex; check pupil size and reaction. Check extraocular movements. CN V: light touch on face (V1, V2, V3 territories); jaw clench against resistance. CN VII: ask patient to smile, raise eyebrows, puff cheeks, close eyes tightly. CN VIII: whispered voice test or finger rub at each ear. CN XII: stick out tongue and look for deviation; tongue deviates toward the side of a lower motor neuron lesion.

The Neurological Exam & FAST

Chapter 16 organizes the neurological exam into five components. The Cincinnati Prehospital Stroke Scale (FAST) is a rapid 60-second screen for stroke. Time matters because stroke treatment is time-critical.

▾
RetrieveNeuro exam structure and FAST
  1. 1.
    Name the five components of the neurological exam.
    (1) Mental status: alertness, orientation (person, place, time), attention, memory, language. (2) Cranial nerves: systematic test of CN I to XII. (3) Motor: strength (0 to 5 scale), tone, bulk, coordination. (4) Sensory: light touch, pain, temperature, vibration, proprioception, two-point discrimination. (5) Reflexes: deep tendon reflexes graded 0 to 4+, plus pathological reflexes like Babinski.
  2. 2.
    Describe what is being assessed in mental status testing.
    Level of consciousness (alert, lethargic, stuporous, comatose). Orientation to person, place, and time ("oriented x3"). Attention (e.g., counting backward by 7s, spelling "world" backward). Memory (immediate recall, short-term, remote). Language (fluency, comprehension, naming, repetition). Mental status often reveals the most about cortical function.
  3. 3.
    Define the motor strength scale 0 to 5.
    0: no muscle contraction. 1: visible flicker, no movement. 2: active movement with gravity eliminated. 3: active movement against gravity but not against resistance. 4: active movement against resistance but weaker than expected. 5: normal strength.
  4. 4.
    State what FAST stands for and the action required.
    Face: ask the patient to smile. Look for asymmetry or droop. Arms: ask the patient to raise both arms and hold for 10 seconds. Look for drift downward on one side. Speech: ask the patient to repeat a simple phrase. Listen for slurring or wrong words. Time: if any of the above is abnormal, call 911 immediately. Note the time symptoms started. Stroke treatment windows are measured in hours.

FAST: Cincinnati Prehospital Stroke Scale

One sided weakness, facial droop, and slurred speech together suggest stroke until proven otherwise. The faster a stroke patient reaches definitive care, the more brain tissue is salvageable. "Time is brain."
