How this worksheet works

BIO 304 is an online course. Work through the Week 4 Cognitive Trainer first as your study tool. This worksheet is what you turn in. It captures only the drawings, predictions, and reflections.

How to complete: Print the worksheet. Complete every box by hand in pen or pencil. Scan or photograph the completed pages as a single PDF. Upload that PDF to the Week 4 Worksheet assignment in Canvas.

Drawing tasks (two this week): eye cross-section and ear pathway. Label every component listed in the prompt. An unlabeled drawing is a sketch and loses points.

Section 5 anchors the FAST lab. Spend extra time there. The Cincinnati Prehospital Stroke Scale shows up directly in the lab quiz.

If you cannot print: contact Dr. Rennie or DSPS for an alternative submission format.

02

Build & Apply · Special Senses

Eye and ear anatomy

The eye and ear are the lab anchors this week. Drawings must be labeled to count.

Build

Build task. Draw a sagittal cross-section of the eye. Label all of the following: cornea, iris, pupil, lens, ciliary body, anterior chamber (aqueous humor), vitreous chamber (vitreous humor), retina, fovea, optic disc, optic nerve.

Rubric: All eleven components present and labeled in the correct location. Light path implied by structural arrangement.

Build

Build task. Draw the ear in three sections (outer, middle, inner) and trace the path of sound. Label all of the following: auricle (pinna), external auditory canal, tympanic membrane, malleus, incus, stapes, oval window, cochlea, semicircular canals, CN VIII (vestibulocochlear nerve).

Rubric: Three ear regions clearly distinguished. All nine structures labeled. Path of sound traced with arrows from auricle to cochlea.

Apply

Apply task. a. A patient has untreated glaucoma (chronically increased intraocular pressure). Predict which eye structure is damaged first and the resulting vision change. Why is this damage often irreversible?

Apply

Apply task. b. A patient develops vertigo (room-spinning sensation) but no hearing loss. Predict which inner ear structures are involved, and explain why hearing remains intact.

05

Apply · The Neurological Exam & FAST

Apply FAST to clinical scenarios

FAST is the lab anchor this week. Run it on each scenario below as you would on a real patient.

FAST: Cincinnati Prehospital Stroke Scale

Face droops? Arm drifts? Speech slurred or wrong? Time: call 911. Note when symptoms started.

Apply

Apply task. a. A 64-year-old patient develops sudden right-sided facial droop, slurred speech, and weakness in the right arm one hour ago. Apply FAST: which letters are positive? Identify the likely affected hemisphere and explain why facial droop and arm weakness are on the same side.

Apply

Apply task. b. Why does FAST work as a screening tool? Walk through what each letter (F, A, S) tests anatomically, and explain why "T" is the most important letter from a treatment standpoint.

Apply

Apply task. c. A patient with a cerebellar stroke fails the finger-to-nose test (their finger overshoots the target). Explain why finger-to-nose is a sensitive test for cerebellar function, and explain why FAST might miss a cerebellar stroke entirely.
