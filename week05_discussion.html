Your prompt

What to write about.

This week we covered the special senses and the endocrine system — how hormones released by glands travel in the blood and bind receptors on distant target cells, all held near a set point by feedback loops.

An endocrine disruptor is a chemical from outside the body that interferes with that signaling. It can mimic a hormone (switch a pathway on), block a receptor (switch it off), or change how a hormone is made, transported, or broken down.

For your initial post (250–350 words):

  1. Investigate two products. Go to the Environmental Working Group's Skin Deep database and look up two products you actually use at home (shampoo, lotion, sunscreen, deodorant, makeup, toothpaste — anything). For each, note the product name, its overall hazard score, and any ingredient flagged for endocrine disruption or developmental/reproductive concern. Common ones to watch for: phthalates (often hidden inside the word "fragrance"), parabens, oxybenzone, triclosan, and BPA.
  2. Explain the mechanism. Pick the more concerning of your two products. Name the specific ingredient and describe how it is proposed to disrupt hormones — does it act like estrogen, block a receptor, or interfere with the thyroid? Tie it to a real gland or hormone and what that hormone normally does in the body.
  3. Trace it through the physiology. If that hormone's signal were nudged up or down, what would the feedback loop do in response, and what downstream effects would you predict? Connect it back to the set-point and negative-feedback idea from Week 1.
  4. Think like a scientist, not just a shopper. EWG is an advocacy organization, and its scores rate hazard — not your personal risk. Comment on (a) dose and exposure (remember: "the dose makes the poison"), and (b) how strong you judge the evidence to be (human studies? animal or high-dose work? test-tube only?). Given all of that, would you actually change anything you use — why or why not?
