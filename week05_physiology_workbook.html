Physiology · Lab Skill 1

ECG basics: paper, intervals, and rate calculation

Understand ECG paper grid, normal interval values, and learn the two methods to calculate heart rate from a strip.

ECG paper basics

Small box: 1 mm × 1 mm = 0.04 seconds wide × 0.1 mV tall.

Large box (5 small): 5 mm × 5 mm = 0.20 seconds wide × 0.5 mV tall.

Standard speed: 25 mm/sec. Standard amplitude: 10 mm/mV.

Normal interval values

P wave duration: < 0.12 sec (3 small boxes). PR interval: 0.12-0.20 sec (3-5 small boxes). QRS duration: < 0.12 sec (3 small boxes). QT interval: < 0.44 sec (varies with heart rate, often corrected as QTc).

Two methods to calculate heart rate

Method 1 (regular rhythms): 300 method. Count the number of large boxes between two consecutive R waves. Divide 300 by that number. Memory line: 300, 150, 100, 75, 60, 50 (R-R intervals of 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 large boxes).

Method 2 (irregular rhythms): 6-second method. Count R waves in a 6-second strip (30 large boxes). Multiply by 10 for beats per minute.

Physiology · Lab Skill 2

The 5-step ECG interpretation method

Apply a systematic 5-step method to every ECG you read. This sequence prevents missed findings.

The 5 steps (in order)

1. Rate. Calculate using one of the two methods above. Normal: 60-100 bpm. Bradycardia: < 60. Tachycardia: > 100.

2. Rhythm. Regular or irregular? Check R-R intervals. Pattern can be regularly irregular (predictable) or irregularly irregular (no pattern).

3. P waves. Present? One before every QRS? Same shape every time? Upright in lead II?

4. PR interval. Within 0.12-0.20 sec? Constant? Lengthening progressively?

5. QRS. Width < 0.12 sec? Same shape each time? Anything before or after looks abnormal (ST elevation/depression, T wave inversion)?

Physiology · Lab Skill 3

Common cardiac rhythms: identification criteria

For each common rhythm, learn the diagnostic criteria. Use this section as your reference for the case strips that follow.

Normal sinus rhythm (NSR)

Rate 60-100. Regular. P before every QRS, QRS after every P. PR 0.12-0.20 sec. QRS < 0.12 sec.

Sinus tachycardia and sinus bradycardia

Sinus tach: all NSR criteria except rate > 100. Common causes: pain, fever, anxiety, hypovolemia, hypoxia, exercise.

Sinus brad: all NSR criteria except rate < 60. Common in athletes (normal). Pathologic causes: hypothyroidism, hypothermia, increased ICP, MI of the SA node, beta-blocker overdose.

Atrial fibrillation (A-fib)

Rhythm: irregularly irregular. No discernible P waves. Replaced by chaotic fibrillatory waves on the baseline. QRS narrow (originates above ventricles). Rate often 100-180 if untreated. Major risk: stroke from atrial clot formation.

Atrial flutter

Sawtooth flutter waves (F waves) replace P waves. Atrial rate ~250-350. Ventricular rate depends on AV conduction ratio (e.g., 2:1 block gives ventricular rate ~150). Rhythm often regular.

AV blocks

1st degree: PR interval > 0.20 sec but every P is followed by a QRS. Stable.

2nd degree Mobitz I (Wenckebach): PR progressively lengthens until a QRS is dropped, then resets. Often benign.

2nd degree Mobitz II: Constant PR, but occasional dropped QRS. More dangerous, often progresses to 3rd degree.

3rd degree (complete heart block): P waves and QRS complexes are independent. Atrial rate and ventricular rate are different. Ventricles fire from a junctional or ventricular escape rhythm. Emergency: requires pacemaker.

PVCs and ventricular tachycardia

Premature ventricular contraction (PVC): wide bizarre QRS, no preceding P, often with compensatory pause. Single PVCs are usually benign.

Ventricular tachycardia (V-tach): 3 or more PVCs in a row. Wide QRS, no P waves, rate > 100. Emergency: pulseless V-tach is treated like cardiac arrest.

Ventricular fibrillation (V-fib): chaotic, no organized QRS. Cardiac arrest. Defibrillate immediately.

Physiology · Lab Skill 4

ASCVD 10-year cardiovascular risk

Calculate 10-year risk of MI or stroke using the Pooled Cohort Equation inputs and decide on treatment.

The ASCVD risk score (Pooled Cohort Equation)

Predicts 10-year risk of a hard ASCVD event (heart attack or stroke) in adults 40-75 years without existing cardiovascular disease.

Inputs: age, sex, race, total cholesterol, HDL cholesterol, systolic BP, BP medication use (yes/no), diabetes (yes/no), smoking (yes/no).

Treatment thresholds (2018 AHA/ACC guidelines, simplified)

< 5%: Low risk. Lifestyle counseling.

5 to < 7.5%: Borderline. Selective statin if risk-enhancing factors present (family history early MI, chronic inflammatory conditions, etc.).

7.5 to < 20%: Intermediate. Moderate-intensity statin recommended after risk discussion.

≥ 20%: High risk. High-intensity statin recommended.

Patients with existing ASCVD, LDL ≥ 190, or diabetes (40-75y) get statin therapy regardless of calculated 10-year risk.

Physiology · Lab Skill 5

BP regulation and the RAAS pathway

Apply the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system to clinical scenarios.

The RAAS cascade (memorize the order)

Trigger: drop in renal perfusion (low BP, low Na+, sympathetic activation).

1. Juxtaglomerular cells (kidney) release renin.

2. Renin cleaves angiotensinogen (from liver) → angiotensin I.

3. Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE, mostly in lungs) converts angiotensin I → angiotensin II.

4. Angiotensin II causes systemic vasoconstriction (raising BP) AND stimulates the adrenal cortex to release aldosterone.

5. Aldosterone causes the kidneys to retain sodium and water (raising BP).

Case 1

The new-onset palpitations

A 72-year-old woman with hypertension and prior CHF comes in with palpitations and dizziness for 6 hours. Vitals: HR 142, BP 96/64. ECG: irregularly irregular rhythm, no discernible P waves, fibrillatory baseline, narrow QRS. She is alert and conversant.

  1. What is the ECG diagnosis?
  2. Why is her blood pressure low?
  3. What is her single largest long-term complication risk from this rhythm, and why? (Reference atrial physiology and clot formation.)
  4. Why might a new-onset arrhythmia like this cause CHF exacerbation in this patient?
Case 2

The hypertensive emergency

A 58-year-old man with poorly controlled HTN presents with severe headache, blurred vision, and chest pain. BP 218/124. Labs show acute kidney injury (creatinine rise from 1.0 to 2.1) and a small troponin elevation. ECG shows ST depression in the lateral leads.

  1. His severely elevated BP is causing damage in three organs. Identify the three organs and the specific damage in each.
  2. The team starts an IV antihypertensive. Which medication class would lower his BP within minutes by blocking RAAS at the angiotensin II receptor? (Hint: it is the IV form of one of the drugs in Section 05.)
  3. If they instead used spironolactone, why would it not bring his BP down quickly enough?
Case 3

The asymptomatic high-risk patient

A 62-year-old man with no symptoms comes in for annual physical. Father had MI at 52. Patient: BMI 28, BP 142/84, total cholesterol 248, HDL 35, LDL 168, HbA1c 5.9% (prediabetes), non-smoker. No known cardiovascular disease.

  1. Calculate (qualitatively) his ASCVD risk category.
  2. Identify which of his findings are modifiable risk factors and which are non-modifiable.
  3. If he were started on a statin, what would the goal LDL reduction be (typically a percentage decrease)?
  4. His prediabetes is a strong predictor of progression. Name two interventions that have been shown to reduce both ASCVD risk AND progression to T2DM.
Case 4

The complete heart block

An 80-year-old woman with new-onset confusion, dizziness, and a near-syncope episode. ECG: P wave rate 90, QRS rate 32, P waves and QRS complexes completely independent of each other. QRS is wide. Vitals: HR 32, BP 78/50.

  1. What is the ECG diagnosis?
  2. Reference your Week 5 Anatomy Workbook conduction system labeling. What is firing the ventricles at 32 bpm if the SA node signal cannot reach them?
  3. What is the definitive treatment?
  4. Why is her blood pressure low? Reference cardiac output = stroke volume × heart rate.
