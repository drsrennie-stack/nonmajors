Blood Composition & Hemostasis

Blood is a connective tissue: cells suspended in a plasma matrix. The lab will require identifying blood cell types under the microscope.

▾
RetrieveCells, plasma, hemostasis, ABO
  1. 1.
    Name the three components of blood by volume, and identify what makes up plasma.
    Whole blood separates into plasma (~55%) on top, a thin buffy coat (~1%, WBCs and platelets) in the middle, and erythrocytes (~45%, hematocrit) at the bottom. Plasma is ~92% water with dissolved proteins (albumin, globulins, fibrinogen), electrolytes, nutrients, gases, hormones, and waste products.
  2. 2.
    Name the three formed elements and the basic function of each. List the five WBC types from most to least abundant.
    Erythrocytes (RBCs): O₂ and CO₂ transport via hemoglobin, biconcave, no nucleus, lifespan ~120 days. Leukocytes (WBCs): immune defense. Platelets (thrombocytes): cell fragments, hemostasis. WBC types most to least: Neutrophils > Lymphocytes > Monocytes > Eosinophils > Basophils. Mnemonic: "Never Let Monkeys Eat Bananas."
  3. 3.
    List the four steps of hemostasis in order.
    (1) Vascular spasm: damaged vessel constricts immediately. (2) Platelet plug formation: platelets adhere to exposed collagen, become activated, recruit more platelets. (3) Coagulation cascade: intrinsic and extrinsic pathways converge to convert prothrombin to thrombin, which converts fibrinogen to fibrin (a mesh that traps the plug). (4) Clot retraction and fibrinolysis: clot tightens, then plasmin breaks it down once vessel heals.
  4. 4.
    Compare the four ABO blood types in terms of antigens, antibodies, and donor/recipient compatibility. Also note the role of Rh.
    Type A: A antigen on RBCs, anti-B antibodies in plasma. Type B: B antigen, anti-A antibodies. Type AB: both A and B antigens, no antibodies (universal recipient for RBCs). Type O: no antigens, both anti-A and anti-B antibodies (universal donor for RBCs). Rh: Rh+ has D antigen, Rh- does not. Rh- mothers carrying an Rh+ fetus can develop anti-Rh antibodies that attack a future Rh+ fetus (hemolytic disease of the newborn). Prevented with RhoGAM.

Heart Anatomy & Coronary Circulation

Four chambers, four valves, two pumps in series. The lab will test heart anatomy on a model.

▾
RetrieveChambers, valves, layers, coronary
  1. 1.
    Name the four chambers and the three layers of the heart wall.
    Chambers: right atrium, right ventricle, left atrium, left ventricle. The right side handles deoxygenated blood and pumps to the lungs. The left side handles oxygenated blood and pumps to the body. Wall layers from outside to inside: epicardium (visceral pericardium), myocardium (cardiac muscle, thickest in left ventricle), endocardium (inner lining continuous with vessel endothelium).
  2. 2.
    Trace the path of blood through the heart, naming all four valves in order.
    Deoxygenated blood enters via superior and inferior vena cavae → right atrium → tricuspid valve → right ventricle → pulmonary semilunar valve → pulmonary trunk and arteries → lungs. Oxygenated blood returns via pulmonary veins → left atrium → mitral (bicuspid) valve → left ventricle → aortic semilunar valve → aorta → systemic circulation.
  3. 3.
    Name the major coronary arteries and the regions of the heart they supply.
    Two main coronary arteries arise from the aortic root just above the aortic valve. Left coronary artery (LCA) branches into the left anterior descending (LAD) (supplies anterior left ventricle and interventricular septum) and the circumflex (supplies left atrium and lateral left ventricle). Right coronary artery (RCA) supplies right atrium, right ventricle, SA and AV nodes in most people, and the posterior left ventricle (via the posterior descending artery in right-dominant circulation).
  4. 4.
    Describe the pericardium and the role of pericardial fluid.
    The pericardium has an outer fibrous layer that anchors the heart, and an inner serous layer with two parts: parietal pericardium (lines the fibrous layer) and visceral pericardium / epicardium (covers the heart). Between the two serous layers is the pericardial cavity with a thin film of serous fluid that lubricates and reduces friction during contraction.

Cardiac Electrical Activity & the ECG

The heart has its own electrical system. Sequential depolarization produces the heartbeat and the ECG. The lab will require you to identify rate and rhythm from a tracing.

▾
RetrieveConduction system and ECG
  1. 1.
    Trace the conduction pathway through the heart from pacemaker to ventricular contraction.
    SA node (sinoatrial, in right atrium, pacemaker, ~60-100 bpm) → spreads across atria → AV node (atrioventricular, brief delay allows atrial contraction to complete) → bundle of His → right and left bundle branches (down the interventricular septum) → Purkinje fibers (rapid conduction throughout ventricles) → ventricular contraction.
  2. 2.
    Define the three special properties of cardiac muscle that distinguish it from skeletal muscle.
    Automaticity (autorhythmicity): SA node depolarizes spontaneously without nervous input. Intrinsic rate ~60-100 bpm; modulated by ANS. Functional syncytium: cardiac cells are connected by intercalated discs with gap junctions, so electrical signal spreads rapidly across the entire myocardium as a unit. Long absolute refractory period: prevents tetanic contraction (which would prevent filling).
  3. 3.
    Identify the three main waves of an ECG and what each represents.
    P wave: atrial depolarization (just before atrial contraction). QRS complex: ventricular depolarization (just before ventricular contraction). Atrial repolarization happens here too but is hidden by the QRS. T wave: ventricular repolarization. PR interval: time from atrial to ventricular depolarization (delay at AV node). ST segment: ventricles fully depolarized.
  4. 4.
    Describe how to estimate heart rate from an ECG using the "300 method" and what defines a regular rhythm.
    300 method: count the number of large boxes between two consecutive R waves and divide 300 by that number. (e.g., 4 large boxes = 75 bpm). Regular rhythm: R-to-R intervals are equal across the strip. Sinus rhythm: a P wave precedes every QRS, and every QRS is preceded by a P, with normal PR interval (0.12 to 0.20 seconds).

Blood Vessels: Structure, Flow, Resistance

Vessels have three layers, but each vessel type is built for a different job. Flow depends on the pressure gradient and the resistance.

▾
RetrieveVessel layers, types, flow equation
  1. 1.
    Name the three tunics (layers) of a typical blood vessel and the dominant tissue in each.
    Tunica intima: innermost. Endothelium (simple squamous epithelium) lining the lumen, plus a thin connective tissue basement membrane. Tunica media: middle. Smooth muscle and elastic fibers. The thickest layer in arteries; controls vasoconstriction and vasodilation. Tunica externa (adventitia): outermost. Connective tissue (collagen) that anchors the vessel.
  2. 2.
    Compare arteries, veins, and capillaries on key features: layer thickness, pressure, valves, and primary function.
    Arteries: thick tunica media, no valves, high pressure (systolic ~120 mmHg), carry blood away from heart. Designed to withstand and dampen pressure. Veins: thinner tunica media, valves to prevent backflow, low pressure (~5-10 mmHg in venules), carry blood to heart. Reservoir function (~60% of blood volume). Capillaries: single endothelial cell layer (tunica intima only), no valves, very low pressure, designed for exchange. Diameter just wider than one RBC.
  3. 3.
    State the equation for blood flow and identify the units of each variable.
    F = ΔP / R: flow equals pressure gradient divided by resistance. F is in mL/min, ΔP is the pressure difference between two points (mmHg), R is resistance. This is Ohm's law applied to blood. The most important driver of flow is the pressure gradient. Resistance reduces flow.
  4. 4.
    List the three factors that determine vascular resistance, and identify which has the largest effect.
    (1) Vessel radius (diameter): by far the largest effect. Resistance is proportional to 1/r⁴. Halving the radius increases resistance 16-fold. This is why vasoconstriction and vasodilation are such powerful regulators. (2) Vessel length: longer vessels = more resistance. Relatively fixed. (3) Blood viscosity: thicker blood = more resistance. Increased in dehydration and polycythemia.

Blood Pressure Regulation & ASCVD Risk

Blood pressure must stay within a narrow range. Short-term regulation comes from baroreceptors and the sympathetic nervous system. Long-term regulation comes from the kidneys via RAAS. The lab applies the ASCVD 10-year risk calculator.

▾
RetrieveBP equation, baroreceptor reflex, RAAS, ASCVD inputs
  1. 1.
    State the equation for blood pressure and define mean arterial pressure (MAP).
    BP = CO × SVR. Cardiac output (CO = HR × stroke volume) times systemic vascular resistance. Anything that increases either CO or SVR raises BP. MAP = diastolic + 1/3 (systolic - diastolic), normal ~70 to 105 mmHg. MAP must be ≥ 60 to perfuse vital organs.
  2. 2.
    Trace the baroreceptor reflex when blood pressure rises suddenly.
    High BP stretches baroreceptors in the carotid sinus and aortic arch → afferent signal via CN IX (glossopharyngeal) and CN X (vagus) → medullary cardiovascular centers integrate → decreased sympathetic, increased parasympathetic output → decreased heart rate, decreased contractility, vasodilation → BP returns toward set point. The opposite happens when BP falls.
  3. 3.
    Trace the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) cascade.
    Low BP, low Na⁺, or sympathetic stimulation triggers juxtaglomerular cells in the kidney to release renin. Renin converts angiotensinogen (from liver) to angiotensin I. ACE (angiotensin converting enzyme, in lung capillaries) converts angiotensin I to angiotensin II. Angiotensin II: (a) potent vasoconstrictor, raises SVR; (b) stimulates aldosterone release from adrenal cortex, which increases renal Na⁺ and water reabsorption; (c) stimulates ADH release. Net effect: raises BP via volume and resistance.
  4. 4.
    List the inputs to the ASCVD 10-year risk calculator and explain what it estimates.
    ASCVD (Atherosclerotic Cardiovascular Disease) 10-year risk uses the Pooled Cohort Equations. Inputs: age, sex, race, total cholesterol, HDL cholesterol, systolic blood pressure, on BP medication (yes/no), diabetes (yes/no), current smoker (yes/no). Output is the 10-year probability (%) of having a heart attack or stroke. Used clinically to guide statin therapy decisions: ≥7.5% generally meets threshold for recommendation.
ApplyBP regulation in scenarios
a. A patient stands up quickly from lying down. BP transiently falls. Trace the baroreceptor reflex and predict what would happen if the reflex failed (orthostatic hypotension and syncope).
b. A patient with congestive heart failure has fluid retention and edema. Explain via the RAAS cascade why this happens and why ACE inhibitors are useful.
c. A 55-year-old male, non-Hispanic white, total cholesterol 220, HDL 40, SBP 140, no BP meds, non-diabetic, non-smoker. Without calculating, identify the modifiable risk factors. Which lifestyle change would most affect his ASCVD risk?

Circulatory Pathways & Lymphatic Anatomy

Two parallel circulations: pulmonary (heart to lungs) and systemic (heart to rest of body). The lymphatic system runs alongside, draining tissue fluid and transporting immune cells.

▾
RetrieveCirculation and lymphatics
  1. 1.
    Compare pulmonary and systemic circulation: where each starts, where it ends, and what each carries.
    Pulmonary circulation: right ventricle → pulmonary trunk → pulmonary arteries → lung capillaries → pulmonary veins → left atrium. Carries deoxygenated blood to the lungs and returns oxygenated blood. Note: pulmonary arteries carry deoxygenated blood (the only arteries that do). Systemic circulation: left ventricle → aorta → systemic arteries → systemic capillaries → systemic veins → SVC/IVC → right atrium. Delivers oxygenated blood to tissues and returns deoxygenated blood.
  2. 2.
    Name the major arteries that branch from the aortic arch and the major veins that drain into the SVC and IVC.
    Aortic arch branches: brachiocephalic trunk (which splits into right common carotid and right subclavian), left common carotid, left subclavian. Major SVC tributaries: jugular veins (head/neck) and subclavian veins (arms). Major IVC tributaries: iliac veins (legs and pelvis), renal veins (kidneys), hepatic veins (liver, after hepatic portal system).
  3. 3.
    Trace the path of lymph from a tissue space back to the bloodstream.
    Tissue fluid leaks into lymphatic capillaries (blind-end vessels with overlapping endothelial flaps acting as one-way valves) → collecting lymphatic vessels (with valves) → lymphatic trunks → two main ducts: the thoracic duct (drains everything below the diaphragm and the left upper body into the left subclavian vein) and the right lymphatic duct (drains the right upper body into the right subclavian vein). Lymph nodes filter the fluid along the way.
  4. 4.
    Distinguish primary and secondary lymphoid organs and name the major organs in each category.
    Primary lymphoid organs (where lymphocytes are produced and mature): red bone marrow (B cells mature here, T cells originate here) and thymus (T cells mature here). Secondary lymphoid organs (where mature lymphocytes encounter antigens): lymph nodes (filter lymph), spleen (filters blood, breaks down old RBCs), tonsils, and mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue (MALT) including Peyer's patches in the gut.
ApplyVessel and lymphatic clinical reasoning
a. A patient has a finger infection and the lymph nodes in the axilla become swollen and tender. Explain the anatomical pathway and identify why these specific nodes are affected.
b. After breast cancer surgery, axillary lymph nodes are removed and the patient develops chronic arm swelling (lymphedema). Explain why this happens.
c. A patient has hepatic portal hypertension. Trace why this affects circulation in the GI tract and explain why fluid leaks into the abdominal cavity (ascites).

Innate & Adaptive Immunity

Innate immunity is fast and non-specific. Adaptive immunity is slow but specific and remembers. Both work together.

▾
RetrieveInnate, adaptive, antibodies, memory
  1. 1.
    List the components of innate immunity (barriers and cells) and the cardinal signs of inflammation.
    Barriers: intact skin, mucous membranes, secretions (lysozyme in tears/saliva, gastric acid, mucus). Cellular innate: phagocytes (neutrophils, macrophages), natural killer (NK) cells (kill virally infected and tumor cells), eosinophils, basophils. Inflammation cardinal signs: rubor (redness), calor (heat), tumor (swelling), dolor (pain), functio laesa (loss of function).
  2. 2.
    Distinguish helper T cells, cytotoxic T cells, and B cells in their roles in adaptive immunity.
    Helper T cells (CD4+): coordinator of the adaptive response. Activated by antigen-presenting cells (via MHC II), then secrete cytokines that activate B cells and cytotoxic T cells. The cell HIV destroys. Cytotoxic T cells (CD8+): directly kill infected or abnormal cells (via MHC I). B cells: when activated, differentiate into plasma cells that produce antibodies, or memory B cells that respond rapidly on re-exposure.
  3. 3.
    Name the five classes of antibodies (immunoglobulins) and the unique role of each.
    IgG: most abundant, primary antibody of secondary response. Crosses placenta to give passive immunity to fetus. IgM: first antibody produced in primary response. Pentameric structure. IgA: secreted into mucosal surfaces (saliva, tears, breast milk, gut). Mucosal immunity. IgE: binds mast cells and basophils, triggers allergic and parasitic responses. IgD: functions as B cell antigen receptor; minor role in serum.
  4. 4.
    Compare primary and secondary immune responses, and distinguish active and passive immunity.
    Primary response: first exposure. Slow (5-10 days to peak), IgM dominant, weaker. Generates memory. Secondary response: re-exposure. Fast (1-3 days), IgG dominant, much stronger. This is why vaccination works. Active immunity: body produces its own antibodies. Acquired by infection or vaccination. Long-lasting due to memory cells. Passive immunity: antibodies received from another source. Examples: maternal IgG across placenta, breast milk IgA, immunoglobulin injection. Immediate but temporary (no memory).
ApplyImmunity scenarios
a. HIV destroys CD4+ T cells. Predict the consequences for adaptive immunity, and explain why HIV patients develop opportunistic infections rather than typical infections.
b. A patient receives the flu vaccine. Trace what happens in the immune system and explain why protection lasts months but not lifelong (and why a booster might be needed).
c. A child gets chickenpox at age 6 and is severely ill for two weeks. As an adult, the same child is exposed to chickenpox again and has no symptoms. Explain in terms of primary versus secondary response.
