How this worksheet works

BIO 304 is an online course. Work through the Week 5 Cognitive Trainer first as your study tool. This worksheet is what you turn in. It captures only the drawings, predictions, and reflections.

How to complete: Print the worksheet. Complete every box by hand in pen or pencil. Scan or photograph the completed pages as a single PDF. Upload that PDF to the Week 5 Worksheet assignment in Canvas.

Drawing tasks (three this week): heart anatomy, ECG tracing, and a vessel comparison table. Label every component. An unlabeled drawing is a sketch and loses points.

Two sections directly anchor lab activities: Section 3 (ECG) and Section 5 (BP regulation and ASCVD). Spend extra time on those.

This is the heaviest week of the course. Plan four sittings, not one. Estimated 8 to 10 printed pages.

02

Build & Apply · Heart Anatomy

Heart structure and pathology

Drawings must be labeled to count.

Build

Build task. Draw a cross-section of the heart showing all four chambers. Label all of the following: right atrium, right ventricle, left atrium, left ventricle; tricuspid valve, pulmonary semilunar valve, mitral (bicuspid) valve, aortic semilunar valve; superior vena cava, inferior vena cava, pulmonary trunk, pulmonary veins, aorta. Use arrows to show the direction of blood flow.

Rubric: Four chambers labeled. Four valves labeled in correct positions. Five great vessels labeled. Arrows show blood path correctly (right side carries deoxygenated, left side carries oxygenated).

Apply

Apply task. a. A patient has mitral valve regurgitation (the valve leaks during ventricular systole). Predict the effect on left atrial pressure and on pulmonary circulation. What symptom would the patient most likely report?

Apply

Apply task. b. A patient has an LAD (left anterior descending) occlusion (heart attack). Identify which wall of the heart is most affected and predict whether contractility on that side will increase or decrease. Why might the SA node still function normally despite this occlusion?

Apply

Apply task. c. A patient has a large pericardial effusion (fluid in pericardial cavity). Predict the effect on cardiac filling and cardiac output. Name this emergency and explain why pericardiocentesis is treatment.

03

Build & Apply · Cardiac Electrical Activity ECG Lab

The ECG and clinical interpretation

Lab anchor. The ECG lab will require you to identify rate and rhythm from a tracing.

Build

Build task. Draw one cardiac cycle of an ECG tracing. Label the P wave, QRS complex, and T wave. Mark the PR interval and ST segment. Below the tracing, write what each wave represents (atrial depolarization, ventricular depolarization, ventricular repolarization).

Rubric: Three waves drawn in correct order with correct relative shape (small P, large narrow QRS, rounded T). All three waves labeled. PR interval and ST segment marked. Three event labels correct.

Apply

Apply task. a. A patient's SA node fails. Predict the heart rate and identify the backup pacemaker. What rate range would you expect, and why is it slower than the SA node's intrinsic rate?

Apply

Apply task. b. A patient has third-degree AV block (no signal reaches the ventricles from the atria). Predict what the ECG would show in terms of P waves, QRS complexes, and their relationship. Predict the patient's symptoms.

Apply

Apply task. c. A patient has atrial fibrillation. Predict the appearance of P waves and the rhythm regularity, and explain why this rhythm increases the risk of stroke.

05

Apply · BP Regulation ASCVD Lab

Blood pressure and cardiovascular risk

Lab anchor. Use the baroreceptor reflex and RAAS in your reasoning.

Apply

Apply task. a. A patient stands up quickly from lying down. BP transiently falls. Trace the baroreceptor reflex from baroreceptor activation to the BP response. Predict what would happen if the reflex failed (orthostatic hypotension and syncope) and identify one population at risk.

Apply

Apply task. b. A patient with congestive heart failure has fluid retention and pitting edema. Explain via the RAAS cascade why this happens, and explain why ACE inhibitors and aldosterone antagonists are mainstays of treatment. Identify which step each drug class blocks.

Apply

Apply task. c. A 55-year-old male, non-Hispanic white, total cholesterol 220, HDL 40, SBP 140, no BP medications, non-diabetic, non-smoker. Without calculating ASCVD risk, identify all modifiable risk factors. Rank in order which lifestyle change would most affect his 10-year risk and justify the ranking.

Circulatory and lymphatic clinical reasoning

Trace the anatomy. Identify which vessels and which nodes are involved in each scenario.

Apply

Apply task. a. A patient has a finger infection on their right hand and the lymph nodes in the right axilla become swollen and tender. Explain the anatomical pathway from the infected finger to the axillary nodes, and identify why these specific nodes (rather than left axillary or inguinal) are affected.

Apply

Apply task. b. After breast cancer surgery with axillary lymph node dissection, the patient develops chronic arm swelling (lymphedema). Explain why this happens anatomically and why the swelling does not resolve quickly.

Apply

Immune scenarios

Use specific cell types (CD4+, CD8+, B cell, plasma cell, memory cell) and antibody classes in your reasoning.

Apply

Apply task. a. HIV destroys CD4+ helper T cells. Predict the consequences for both T cell and B cell responses, and explain why HIV patients develop opportunistic infections (with organisms most people clear easily) rather than typical infections.

Apply

Apply task. b. A patient receives the flu vaccine. Trace what happens in the immune system step by step (antigen presentation through memory). Identify whether this is active or passive immunity, and explain why protection lasts months but not lifelong.

Apply

Explain in terms of primary versus secondary response, identify which antibody dominates each response, and identify which cells make memory possible.
