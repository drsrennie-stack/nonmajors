Physiology · Lab Skill 1

Spirometry: FEV1/FVC and the obstructive vs restrictive pattern

Interpret pulmonary function tests using the FEV1/FVC ratio and total lung volumes.

Key spirometry values

FVC (forced vital capacity): total volume exhaled forcefully after a maximal inhalation. Reflects total lung volume available.

FEV1 (forced expiratory volume in 1 second): volume exhaled in the first second of an FVC maneuver. Reflects airway speed/resistance.

FEV1/FVC ratio: the percentage of total volume that gets out in 1 second. Normal in healthy adults: ≥ 0.70 (or 70%).

TLC (total lung capacity): total air the lungs can hold. Used to differentiate obstructive from restrictive disease.

The four spirometry patterns

Normal: FEV1/FVC ≥ 0.70. FVC and FEV1 both within normal range. TLC normal.

Obstructive (asthma, COPD): FEV1/FVC < 0.70 (the airways are narrow, less air gets out in 1 second). FEV1 reduced. FVC may be normal or reduced. TLC often normal or increased (air trapping).

Restrictive (pulmonary fibrosis, chest wall disease, neuromuscular): FEV1/FVC ≥ 0.70 (or even higher than normal). FVC reduced. FEV1 reduced proportionally. TLC reduced. The lungs cannot expand to normal size.

Mixed: elements of both. FEV1/FVC < 0.70 AND TLC reduced.

Physiology · Lab Skill 2

ABG interpretation: primary disorder and compensation

Use a systematic 4-step method to identify the primary acid-base disorder and any compensation.

Normal ABG values

pH: 7.35-7.45  ·  PaCO₂: 35-45 mmHg  ·  HCO₃⁻: 22-26 mEq/L  ·  PaO₂: 80-100 mmHg (room air)

The 4-step ABG method

Step 1: Look at pH. < 7.35 = acidosis. > 7.45 = alkalosis. 7.35-7.45 with abnormal CO₂/HCO₃⁻ = fully compensated.

Step 2: Look at PaCO₂. If pH and CO₂ change in opposite directions (low pH + high CO₂, or high pH + low CO₂), the disorder is respiratory.

Step 3: Look at HCO₃⁻. If pH and HCO₃⁻ change in the same direction (low pH + low HCO₃⁻, or high pH + high HCO₃⁻), the disorder is metabolic.

Step 4: Compensation? If the other system has shifted toward correcting pH, compensation is present (partial if pH still abnormal, complete if pH normal).

Common causes of each disorder

Respiratory acidosis (high CO₂, low pH): hypoventilation. COPD exacerbation, opioid overdose, neuromuscular failure, severe asthma.

Respiratory alkalosis (low CO₂, high pH): hyperventilation. Anxiety/panic, pain, fever, pulmonary embolism, pregnancy, salicylate toxicity (early).

Metabolic acidosis (low HCO₃⁻, low pH): DKA, lactic acidosis, renal failure, severe diarrhea (loss of HCO₃⁻).

Metabolic alkalosis (high HCO₃⁻, high pH): vomiting, diuretic use, antacid overuse.

Physiology · Lab Skill 3

Urine dipstick interpretation

Read the standard 10-pad urine dipstick and recognize patterns of disease from the strip alone.

Normal urine dipstick (negative or trace)

Most pads should read negative or trace. The two that show normal positive values: specific gravity (1.005-1.030) and pH (4.5-8.0).

What each pad detects

Glucose: normally negative. Positive in DKA, hyperglycemia exceeding renal threshold (~180 mg/dL), pregnancy.

Ketones: normally negative. Positive in DKA, starvation, alcoholic ketoacidosis, ketogenic diet.

Specific gravity: measures urine concentration. Low (1.005) = dilute (overhydration, diabetes insipidus). High (>1.030) = concentrated (dehydration, SIADH).

Blood: normally negative. Positive in UTI, kidney stones, glomerulonephritis, rhabdomyolysis (myoglobin cross-reacts), trauma, menstrual contamination.

pH: normally 4.5-8.0. Acidic urine in metabolic acidosis. Alkaline urine in UTIs with urea-splitting bacteria (Proteus).

Protein: normally negative or trace. Positive in glomerular disease (nephrotic syndrome), preeclampsia, exercise.

Urobilinogen: trace normal. Increased in liver disease, hemolysis.

Nitrites: normally negative. Positive in UTI from gram-negative bacteria (E. coli, Proteus, Klebsiella) that convert nitrate to nitrite.

Leukocyte esterase: normally negative. Positive when neutrophils are present (UTI, infection).

Bilirubin: normally negative. Positive in liver disease, biliary obstruction.

Physiology · Clinical Reasoning Cases

Integrate spirometry, ABG, and urinalysis across four patients

Each case combines two or three lab skills to test integration across systems.

Case 1

The COPD exacerbation

A 70-year-old man with a 60 pack-year history comes to the ER with worsening dyspnea and productive cough. Spirometry: FEV1/FVC 0.46, FEV1 32% predicted, TLC 116% predicted. ABG (room air): pH 7.30, PaCO₂ 62, HCO₃⁻ 32, PaO₂ 56.

  1. Classify the spirometry pattern. Why is TLC elevated in this patient (reference air trapping)?
  2. Interpret the ABG using the 4-step method. What is the primary disorder and is there compensation?
  3. Why is HCO₃⁻ elevated despite this being primarily a respiratory problem?
  4. Treatment with bronchodilators and steroids is started, but the team avoids high-flow oxygen targeting normal SaO₂. Why? (Hint: hypoxic drive in chronic CO₂ retention.)
Case 2

The diabetic in DKA

A 19-year-old with type 1 diabetes presents confused with deep, rapid breathing (Kussmaul respirations). Glucose 480 mg/dL. ABG: pH 7.12, PaCO₂ 18, HCO₃⁻ 6, PaO₂ 102. Urine dipstick: glucose ++++, ketones +++, specific gravity 1.032.

  1. Interpret the ABG. What is the primary disorder and what is the role of the low PaCO₂?
  2. Why is glucose in the urine, and why are ketones present?
  3. Explain Kussmaul respirations as a compensatory mechanism. Reference both the brainstem chemoreceptors and the bicarbonate equation.
  4. Why is the specific gravity high? What does this say about the patient's volume status?
Case 3

The pulmonary embolism

A 28-year-old woman, 2 weeks postpartum, presents with sudden chest pain and shortness of breath. HR 118, SpO₂ 88% on room air. ABG: pH 7.51, PaCO₂ 26, HCO₃⁻ 22, PaO₂ 62.

  1. Interpret the ABG. What is the primary disorder?
  2. Why is PaO₂ low while pH is high? (These can seem contradictory.) Reference how PE affects ventilation vs perfusion.
  3. Why does PE cause hyperventilation in the first place? (Reference irritant receptors and hypoxia drive.)
  4. Two factors in this patient's history put her at high risk for venous thromboembolism. Identify both.
Case 4

The acute kidney injury

A 75-year-old woman is hospitalized for severe pneumonia and started on IV vancomycin and gentamicin. Five days in, urine output drops to 200 mL/day. Creatinine rises from 0.9 to 3.4. Urine dipstick: protein +, blood trace, specific gravity 1.012, all else negative. ABG: pH 7.30, PaCO₂ 32, HCO₃⁻ 16, PaO₂ 88.

  1. Interpret the ABG.
  2. What is the most likely cause of her acute kidney injury (AKI), and which specific nephron segment is affected? Reference the Week 6 Anatomy nephron labeling.
  3. Why is the urine specific gravity 1.012 despite her being oliguric? (Hint: damaged tubules cannot concentrate urine.)
  4. Why is metabolic acidosis a complication of AKI? (Reference the kidney's normal role in acid excretion and bicarbonate regeneration.)

Integrate respiratory and renal compensation across an acid-base disturbance

The most heavily weighted question in the workbook.

Synthesis question · Most weighted

A 62-year-old woman with chronic kidney disease (baseline creatinine 2.4) develops severe pneumonia and respiratory failure. ABG on arrival: pH 7.16, PaCO₂ 60, HCO₃⁻ 14, PaO₂ 50. Spirometry not feasible due to her acute respiratory failure.

In four to six sentences, integrate respiratory and renal physiology to explain her acid-base picture. Your answer must integrate:

(1) the primary respiratory disorder she has and why (reference pneumonia + alveolar gas exchange + the respiratory membrane), (2) the primary metabolic disorder she has and why (reference her CKD and normal kidney role in acid-base balance), (3) why this is called a "mixed disorder" rather than primary respiratory acidosis with metabolic compensation, (4) what would normally compensate for her respiratory acidosis if her kidneys were healthy, and why that compensation is failing, (5) why her PaO₂ matters more urgently than her acid-base status in determining the next intervention.
