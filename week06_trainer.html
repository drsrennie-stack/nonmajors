Week 6: how the body trades gases and disposes of waste

The lungs and kidneys work as a pair. The lungs handle CO₂ (a volatile acid) on a breath-by-breath timescale. The kidneys handle bicarbonate, fixed acids, water, and electrolytes on a slower timescale. Together they keep arterial pH between 7.35 and 7.45.

One section in this trainer covers COR-named content: Section 6 on the urine dipstick. The same section covers the regional extension on AirNow AQI and wildfire smoke, which is a real respiratory exposure for Northern California students.

Respiratory Anatomy & Ventilation

Two functional zones: a conducting zone that filters and humidifies air, and a respiratory zone where gas exchange happens. Breathing is a mechanical pump driven by pressure differences.

▾
RetrieveAirways, lungs, mechanics, volumes
  1. 1.
    Distinguish the conducting zone from the respiratory zone of the airway, and trace air from the nose to an alveolus.
    Conducting zone warms, humidifies, and filters air. No gas exchange. Respiratory zone is where gas exchange occurs. Path: nose/mouth → pharynx → larynx → trachea → primary bronchi → secondary and tertiary bronchi → bronchioles → terminal bronchioles (end of conducting zone) → respiratory bronchioles → alveolar ducts → alveoli (respiratory zone).
  2. 2.
    Describe the lungs and the alveolar wall, including the cell types in alveoli.
    Right lung has 3 lobes; left lung has 2 lobes (cardiac notch accommodates the heart). Each lung is wrapped in visceral pleura, the chest wall is lined with parietal pleura, and a thin film of pleural fluid sits between them. Alveolar wall has three cell types: Type I pneumocytes (thin squamous, gas exchange), Type II pneumocytes (cuboidal, secrete surfactant to reduce surface tension), alveolar macrophages (clear debris and pathogens). About 300 million alveoli per lung.
  3. 3.
    Explain the mechanics of inspiration and expiration. Identify which is active and which is passive.
    Inspiration is active. Diaphragm contracts (flattens) and external intercostals lift the ribs → thoracic volume increases → intrapulmonary pressure drops below atmospheric → air flows in down the gradient. Expiration at rest is passive. Inspiratory muscles relax, the lungs and chest wall recoil → thoracic volume decreases → intrapulmonary pressure rises above atmospheric → air flows out. Forced expiration adds active contraction of internal intercostals and abdominal muscles.
  4. 4.
    Define the four primary lung volumes (TV, IRV, ERV, RV) and the two main capacities (VC, TLC).
    Tidal volume (TV): normal breath volume, ~500 mL. Inspiratory reserve volume (IRV): extra air you can inhale after a normal breath in, ~3000 mL. Expiratory reserve volume (ERV): extra air you can exhale after a normal breath out, ~1100 mL. Residual volume (RV): air left in lungs after maximum exhalation, ~1200 mL. Cannot be measured by spirometry. Vital capacity (VC) = TV + IRV + ERV, ~4600 mL. Total lung capacity (TLC) = VC + RV, ~5800 mL.

Gas Exchange & Transport

Gas moves down partial pressure gradients. Hemoglobin makes O₂ delivery possible at the volume the body needs. CO₂ travels in three forms.

▾
RetrieveExternal and internal respiration, hemoglobin, CO₂, V/Q
  1. 1.
    Distinguish external and internal respiration in terms of location and direction of gas movement.
    External respiration: at the alveolar-capillary interface in the lungs. O₂ diffuses from alveoli (high PO₂) into pulmonary capillaries; CO₂ diffuses from capillaries into alveoli. Both move down their partial pressure gradients. Internal respiration: at the tissue-capillary interface throughout the body. O₂ diffuses from systemic capillaries into tissue cells; CO₂ diffuses from cells into capillaries.
  2. 2.
    Describe how hemoglobin transports O₂ and what the Bohr effect is.
    Hemoglobin has four heme groups, each carrying one O₂ molecule. One Hb carries up to 4 O₂. Binding is cooperative: the first O₂ makes binding the next ones easier, producing a sigmoidal saturation curve. Bohr effect: in tissues, low pH, high CO₂, high temperature, and high 2,3-BPG shift the curve right, decreasing Hb's O₂ affinity and releasing O₂ where the tissues need it most.
  3. 3.
    List the three forms in which CO₂ travels in blood, with approximate percentages.
    (1) Bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) in plasma: ~70%. CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻ (catalyzed by carbonic anhydrase in RBCs). The HCO₃⁻ leaves the RBC; this is the chloride shift. (2) Carbaminohemoglobin: ~23%. CO₂ binds the protein portion of Hb (not the heme). (3) Dissolved in plasma: ~7%. Direct dissolution.
  4. 4.
    Define ventilation-perfusion (V/Q) matching. Compare a low V/Q (shunt) with a high V/Q (dead space).
    Optimal V/Q ≈ 1: ventilation matches perfusion in each lung region, so all blood gets oxygenated. Low V/Q (shunt): perfusion without ventilation. Blood passes through unventilated alveoli (e.g., pneumonia, atelectasis) and stays deoxygenated. Causes hypoxemia. High V/Q (dead space): ventilation without perfusion. Air reaches alveoli but no blood is there to pick up O₂ (e.g., pulmonary embolism). Wasted ventilation.

c. A patient hyperventilates due to anxiety. Predict the effect on PaCO₂ and on blood pH. Identify the resulting acid-base disorder.

Urinary Anatomy & the Nephron

The kidneys filter ~180 L of plasma per day and return ~178 L to circulation. The nephron is the functional unit that does all of this.

▾
RetrieveGross anatomy and the nephron
  1. 1.
    Name the four organs of the urinary system and describe the gross internal anatomy of the kidney.
    Two kidneys filter blood. Two ureters transport urine to the bladder. The urinary bladder stores urine. The urethra conducts urine to the outside. Internal kidney layers: cortex (outer, contains glomeruli), medulla (inner, organized into pyramids; loops of Henle and collecting ducts live here), renal pelvis (collects urine from minor and major calyces and funnels it to the ureter).
  2. 2.
    List the parts of the nephron in the order filtrate flows through them.
    (1) Renal corpuscle = glomerulus + Bowman's capsule (filtration occurs here). (2) Proximal convoluted tubule (PCT): bulk reabsorption. (3) Loop of Henle: descending limb (water permeable), then ascending limb (solute permeable). (4) Distal convoluted tubule (DCT): regulated reabsorption and secretion. (5) Collecting duct: final water and Na⁺ adjustments under hormonal control.
  3. 3.
    Compare cortical and juxtamedullary nephrons.
    Cortical nephrons (~85%) sit mostly in the cortex with short loops of Henle that barely dip into the medulla. Handle routine filtration. Juxtamedullary nephrons (~15%) sit at the cortex-medulla border with long loops of Henle that descend deep into the medulla. They establish the medullary osmotic gradient that allows the kidney to concentrate urine.
  4. 4.
    Trace blood flow through the kidney from renal artery to renal vein, naming the key vessels around the nephron.
    Renal artery → segmental artery → interlobar artery → arcuate artery → cortical radiate artery → afferent arteriole → glomerulus (filtration) → efferent arteriole → peritubular capillaries (around PCT and DCT, reabsorption) or vasa recta (around loop of Henle in juxtamedullary nephrons) → cortical radiate vein → arcuate vein → interlobar vein → renal vein.

Urine Formation: Filtration, Reabsorption, Secretion

Three processes determine what ends up in urine. Filtration loads the tubule. Reabsorption reclaims what the body needs. Secretion adds what the body wants to dump.

▾
RetrieveThe three processes and the hormones that regulate them
  1. 1.
    Describe glomerular filtration. Identify the filtration membrane, what it excludes, and the normal GFR.
    Hydrostatic pressure in the glomerulus pushes plasma through a three-layer filtration membrane: (1) fenestrated capillary endothelium, (2) basement membrane, (3) podocyte filtration slits. Excludes blood cells and most plasma proteins. Net filtration pressure ≈ glomerular hydrostatic pressure − blood osmotic pressure − capsular hydrostatic pressure. Normal GFR ≈ 125 mL/min (~180 L/day). The body filters its plasma volume many times a day.
  2. 2.
    Describe what is reabsorbed in the PCT and the concept of transport maximum (Tm).
    The PCT reabsorbs ~65% of the filtrate. Returns essentially 100% of glucose and amino acids, ~65% of Na⁺ and water, most bicarbonate, much of K⁺ and Cl⁻. Transport relies on Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase on the basolateral side, creating gradients that pull other solutes via cotransport. Transport maximum (Tm): each transporter saturates at a maximum rate. When plasma glucose exceeds the renal threshold (~180 mg/dL), transporters saturate and glucose spills into urine.
  3. 3.
    Describe the countercurrent multiplier in the loop of Henle and why it matters.
    Descending limb: permeable to water but not solute. Water leaves into the increasingly concentrated medulla, so the filtrate becomes more concentrated. Ascending limb: permeable to solute (active Na⁺/K⁺/2Cl⁻ pumping) but impermeable to water. Solute leaves but water cannot follow, so the filtrate becomes dilute. The two limbs together build a medullary gradient from ~300 mOsm at the cortex-medulla border to ~1200 mOsm at the deepest medulla. This gradient allows the collecting duct to concentrate urine when ADH is present.
  4. 4.
    Name three major hormones that regulate urine output and their primary actions.
    ADH (antidiuretic hormone, vasopressin): released from posterior pituitary in response to high plasma osmolarity or low BP. Increases collecting duct water permeability via aquaporin insertion. Result: more water reabsorbed, concentrated urine. Aldosterone: released from adrenal cortex (RAAS). Increases Na⁺ reabsorption and K⁺ secretion in DCT and collecting duct. Result: more Na⁺ and water retained, raises BP. ANP (atrial natriuretic peptide): released from atrial cells when stretched (high blood volume). Increases Na⁺ excretion. Result: lowers blood volume and BP. Opposes RAAS.
ApplyUrine formation in disease and drugs
a. A patient with uncontrolled diabetes has a plasma glucose of 300 mg/dL. Predict whether their urine will contain glucose, and explain via the transport maximum.
b. Furosemide (Lasix) is a loop diuretic that blocks the Na⁺/K⁺/2Cl⁻ cotransporter in the ascending limb of the loop of Henle. Predict the effect on urine output, urine concentration, and blood pressure.
c. A patient with central diabetes insipidus produces no ADH. Predict their urine volume, urine concentration, and predict what happens to plasma osmolarity if they cannot drink enough water.

Focus · OpenStax 26.1, 26.4

Fluid, Electrolyte & Acid-Base Balance

Total body water is 60% of body weight, distributed across compartments. The lungs and kidneys regulate pH together, on different timescales.

▾
RetrieveCompartments, electrolytes, buffers, disorders
  1. 1.
    Describe the body fluid compartments and their approximate fractions of body weight.
    Total body water ≈ 60% of body weight (lower in females and the elderly because of higher fat fraction). Intracellular fluid (ICF): ~40% of body weight, the largest compartment. Extracellular fluid (ECF): ~20% of body weight. ECF subdivides into interstitial fluid (~15%) and plasma (~5%). Membranes between compartments allow water to follow osmotic gradients.
  2. 2.
    Identify the major cation in the ICF and the major cation in the ECF, and state the normal plasma values for Na⁺ and K⁺.
    Major ICF cation: K⁺ (~140 mEq/L inside cells, ~3.5 to 5.0 mEq/L in plasma). Major ECF cation: Na⁺ (~135 to 145 mEq/L in plasma, only ~10 mEq/L inside cells). The Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase maintains these gradients. Other key electrolytes: Cl⁻ and HCO₃⁻ are major ECF anions; phosphate and proteins are major ICF anions; Ca²⁺ is mostly ECF and bone.
  3. 3.
    State the normal arterial pH range and identify the three buffer systems that defend pH.
    Normal arterial pH: 7.35 to 7.45. Three buffer systems: (1) Bicarbonate buffer in plasma (extracellular). Fast. CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻. Lungs control PCO₂; kidneys control HCO₃⁻. (2) Phosphate buffer in urine and intracellular. (3) Protein buffers, especially hemoglobin in RBCs and proteins inside cells. Intracellular.
  4. 4.
    Compare the four primary acid-base disorders and identify how the body compensates.
    Respiratory acidosis: high PCO₂ (hypoventilation, COPD, drug overdose). pH low. Renal compensation: kidneys retain HCO₃⁻ (slow, days). Respiratory alkalosis: low PCO₂ (hyperventilation, anxiety, high altitude). pH high. Renal compensation: kidneys excrete HCO₃⁻. Metabolic acidosis: low HCO₃⁻ (DKA, lactic acidosis, diarrhea). pH low. Respiratory compensation: hyperventilation lowers PCO₂ (fast, minutes). Metabolic alkalosis: high HCO₃⁻ (vomiting, diuretics). pH high. Respiratory compensation: hypoventilation raises PCO₂. Lungs compensate fast; kidneys compensate slowly.
ApplyAcid-base scenarios
a. A patient has severe diarrhea for three days. Predict the acid-base disorder, the change in HCO₃⁻ and pH, and the expected respiratory compensation.
b. A patient with Type 1 diabetes presents in DKA with rapid deep breathing (Kussmaul respirations). Identify the acid-base disorder, explain why ketones cause it, and explain why the breathing pattern is the body's compensation.
c. A patient has an anxiety attack with rapid shallow breathing. They report tingling around the mouth and in the fingers. Identify the acid-base disorder and explain why the symptoms occur.

Lab anchor · Urine Dipstick (COR-named) & AirNow AQI

Bedside Tools: Dipstick & Air Quality

Two clinical tools for this week's lab. The urine dipstick is COR-named content. The AirNow AQI is a regional extension because Northern California air quality during wildfire season directly affects respiratory health.

▾
RetrieveDipstick parameters and AQI categories
  1. 1.
    List the parameters on a standard urine dipstick and what each indicates when abnormal.
    Specific gravity: urine concentration (1.005 to 1.030). High = concentrated/dehydrated. pH: 4.5 to 8.0. Glucose: normally negative; positive in diabetes mellitus once renal threshold (~180 mg/dL plasma) is exceeded. Ketones: normally negative; positive in DKA, starvation, low-carb diet. Protein: normally negative; positive in glomerular damage. Blood: normally negative; positive in UTI, kidney stones, glomerular disease. Leukocyte esterase: positive when WBCs present (UTI). Nitrites: positive when gram-negative bacteria reduce dietary nitrate (UTI). Bilirubin / urobilinogen: liver and biliary function.
  2. 2.
    Identify the typical dipstick patterns for diabetes mellitus, urinary tract infection, and glomerulonephritis.
    Uncontrolled diabetes mellitus: glucose positive, ketones positive (especially in DKA), often acidic pH. Specific gravity often elevated. UTI: leukocyte esterase positive, nitrites positive (when gram-negative organism), often blood trace, sometimes alkaline pH. Glomerulonephritis: protein positive (often heavy), blood positive (microscopic or gross hematuria). Hyaline or RBC casts on microscopy.
  3. 3.
    Identify the six AirNow Air Quality Index (AQI) categories with their numeric ranges and the populations affected at each level.
    0 to 50: Good (green). Air quality satisfactory; minimal risk. 51 to 100: Moderate (yellow). Unusually sensitive people may have symptoms. 101 to 150: Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups (orange). Children, elderly, those with heart or lung disease, and pregnant people should reduce outdoor activity. 151 to 200: Unhealthy (red). Everyone may experience effects; sensitive groups more serious. 201 to 300: Very Unhealthy (purple). Everyone should reduce outdoor activity; health alert. 301+: Hazardous (maroon). Emergency conditions; everyone should avoid outdoor activity.
  4. 4.
    Explain why PM2.5 from wildfire smoke is particularly harmful and which clinical conditions worsen during high-AQI days.
    PM2.5 = fine particulate matter, less than 2.5 micrometers in diameter. Small enough to bypass nasal filtering and reach the alveoli, where it triggers inflammation, crosses into systemic circulation, and contributes to cardiovascular events. Conditions that worsen during high-AQI days: asthma exacerbations, COPD flares, increased risk of heart attack and stroke, decreased exercise tolerance in healthy people. Effects are dose-dependent, so brief exposure at moderate levels is much less harmful than prolonged exposure at unhealthy levels.

Wrapping up Week 6

The respiratory and urinary systems are the body's two main waste managers. Together they regulate pH, blood volume, and electrolyte balance on different timescales. The dipstick reads urine; the AQI reads the air. Both come up in lab this week.
