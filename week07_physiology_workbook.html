Physiology · Lab Skill 1

Liver function panel (LFTs): hepatocellular vs cholestatic patterns

Distinguish hepatocellular injury from biliary obstruction using AST, ALT, ALP, GGT, and bilirubin patterns.

The five standard LFT components

AST (aspartate aminotransferase): normal < 40 U/L. Found in liver, heart, skeletal muscle, kidney. Less liver-specific than ALT.

ALT (alanine aminotransferase): normal < 40 U/L. Highly liver-specific. The most useful single marker of hepatocyte injury.

ALP (alkaline phosphatase): normal 44-147 U/L. Found in bile ducts, bone, placenta. Elevated in cholestasis (biliary obstruction) and bone disease.

GGT (gamma-glutamyl transferase): normal < 50 U/L. Confirms ALP elevation is from liver/biliary tree, not bone. Also elevated in alcohol-related liver injury.

Total bilirubin: normal 0.2-1.2 mg/dL. Elevated in hemolysis (indirect/unconjugated) or biliary obstruction (direct/conjugated).

Two main patterns of LFT abnormality

Hepatocellular injury: AST and ALT are dominantly elevated (often > 5x upper limit). ALP and bilirubin may be mildly elevated or normal. Causes: viral hepatitis, drug-induced liver injury (acetaminophen, alcohol, statins), ischemia.

Cholestatic / biliary obstruction: ALP and GGT are dominantly elevated. Bilirubin elevated (mostly direct). AST/ALT mildly elevated. Causes: gallstones, cholangiocarcinoma, primary biliary cholangitis, drug-induced cholestasis.

AST > ALT (ratio > 2:1) with both modestly elevated suggests alcoholic liver disease.

ALT > AST in a patient with elevated transaminases suggests viral hepatitis or NAFLD (non-alcoholic fatty liver disease).

Physiology · Lab Skill 2

Lipid panel interpretation and ASCVD risk recalculation

Read total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, and triglycerides. Apply to ASCVD risk and statin decisions.

The standard lipid panel (fasting or non-fasting)

Total cholesterol: goal < 200 mg/dL. Borderline 200-239. High ≥ 240.

LDL ("bad" cholesterol): goal varies by ASCVD risk. < 100 optimal for most. < 70 for established cardiovascular disease.

HDL ("good" cholesterol): protective. ≥ 60 mg/dL is favorable. < 40 in men or < 50 in women is a risk factor.

Triglycerides: < 150 normal. 150-499 elevated. ≥ 500 high risk for pancreatitis.

Friedewald estimate: LDL ≈ TC − HDL − (TG/5). Valid only when TG < 400 mg/dL.

Statin treatment decisions (recap from Week 5)

Automatic statin candidates (regardless of ASCVD score): existing ASCVD, LDL ≥ 190 mg/dL, diabetes (age 40-75) with LDL ≥ 70.

By 10-year ASCVD risk: ≥ 20% high-intensity statin. 7.5-20% moderate-intensity statin. 5-7.5% borderline (consider risk enhancers). < 5% lifestyle only.

Statin intensity: moderate-intensity expected to lower LDL by 30-49%. High-intensity expected to lower LDL by ≥ 50%.

Physiology · Lab Skill 3

Nutrition label audit

Read a Nutrition Facts label and evaluate it for cardiovascular and metabolic health.

Nutrition Facts label key thresholds

Serving size: always check first. The values that follow apply to one serving, not the whole package.

Sodium: < 140 mg per serving = low sodium. > 480 mg per serving = high sodium. Daily limit is < 2,300 mg (ideally < 1,500 mg in hypertension).

Saturated fat: goal < 6% of daily calories (about < 13 g/day on a 2000 kcal diet). Trans fat: zero is the only acceptable amount.

Added sugars: goal < 10% of daily calories (about < 50 g/day on a 2000 kcal diet, < 25-36 g recommended by AHA).

Fiber: ≥ 5g per serving = high fiber. Daily goal: 25-38 g.

% Daily Value (%DV): 5% or less is "low," 20% or more is "high."

Physiology · Clinical Reasoning Cases

Integrate LFTs, lipid panel, and nutrition across four patients

Each case combines two or more lab skills with anatomy from the Week 7 Anatomy Workbook.

Case 1

The acute viral hepatitis

A 25-year-old recently traveled internationally and presents with 1 week of fatigue, nausea, and dark urine. Skin appears slightly yellow. LFTs: AST 1820, ALT 2640, ALP 145, GGT 88, total bilirubin 4.2 (direct 3.1, indirect 1.1). Hepatitis A IgM antibody: positive.

  1. Classify the LFT pattern. Is this hepatocellular or cholestatic?
  2. Why is the bilirubin predominantly direct (conjugated)? Reference the Week 7 anatomy workbook bile pathway from hepatocyte to gut.
  3. Hepatitis A is fecal-oral transmission. Trace the path of the virus from contaminated food to liver damage. Reference relevant anatomy.
  4. Why does the urine appear dark? Which form of bilirubin is water-soluble enough to spill into urine?
Case 2

The gallstone with obstruction

A 48-year-old woman, BMI 32, presents with sudden severe right upper quadrant pain after a fatty meal. Skin is yellow. Stool is pale (clay-colored). Urine is dark. LFTs: AST 142, ALT 168, ALP 720, GGT 580, total bilirubin 8.6 (direct 7.2, indirect 1.4). Ultrasound: dilated common bile duct with a stone in the distal duct.

  1. Classify the LFT pattern. Is this hepatocellular or cholestatic?
  2. Why is the stool pale? Reference what gives stool its normal brown color.
  3. The stone is lodged in the distal common bile duct. Trace where bile is backing up to. Reference the biliary tree anatomy from Week 7.
  4. If the obstruction were proximal to where the cystic duct joins the common hepatic duct (above the gallbladder), would the gallbladder be enlarged or normal? Explain.
Case 3

The metabolic syndrome patient

A 54-year-old man comes in for routine physical. BMI 36, BP 148/92, waist circumference 44 inches. Labs: fasting glucose 118, HbA1c 6.0%, total cholesterol 252, HDL 32, TG 280. LFTs: AST 58, ALT 92, ALP 102, GGT 76, bilirubin 0.8.

  1. Calculate his LDL using Friedewald.
  2. Identify the criteria for metabolic syndrome that this patient meets (need at least 3 of 5).
  3. His LFTs show AST 58 / ALT 92 (ALT > AST). What is the most likely cause given his metabolic profile? (Hint: not viral hepatitis, not alcohol if no history, but very common.)
  4. What two interventions would address both his metabolic syndrome and his liver findings simultaneously?
Case 4

The statin candidate with worsening LFTs

A 62-year-old woman started on atorvastatin 40 mg 6 weeks ago for primary prevention (10-year ASCVD risk 16%). She reports muscle aches but no severe symptoms. Labs now: AST 142, ALT 158 (both were normal at baseline), CK 380 (slight elevation), no rise in bilirubin or ALP.

  1. Are her LFTs reflecting hepatocellular injury, cholestatic injury, or muscle injury? How do you tell?
  2. Should the statin be stopped immediately, or watched? Reference the typical threshold (3x upper limit of normal for AST/ALT) and the role of CK.
  3. Why is CK elevated? Reference the muscle organization anatomy from Week 2.
  4. If the statin is causing rhabdomyolysis at higher doses, what specific complication could affect the kidneys? Reference Week 6 nephron anatomy.

Integrate digestion, lipid metabolism, and nutrition in cardiovascular disease prevention

Synthesis question · Most weighted

A 50-year-old man with prediabetes (HbA1c 6.1%), BMI 32, BP 138/86, and a recent lipid panel showing TC 244, HDL 36, TG 220, calculated LDL 164 sits down with you. He eats fast food 4-5 times per week (typical lunch: cheeseburger, fries, sugared soda). His LFTs show ALT 78, AST 52 (mild elevation, ALT-dominant). He has no symptoms.

In four to six sentences, integrate digestive anatomy, lipid metabolism, and nutrition advice into a coherent intervention plan. Your answer must integrate:

(1) why his LFT pattern (ALT > AST mild elevation) is consistent with non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) given his metabolic profile, (2) the path lipids take from his lunch into the bloodstream (reference small intestine absorption, lacteals, chylomicrons, and the liver from Week 7 anatomy), (3) why his HDL is low and his TG is high in the context of insulin resistance, (4) two specific dietary changes (not "eat better") that target both the NAFLD and the lipid panel, and (5) whether you would start a statin now or after lifestyle intervention based on his ASCVD risk and the available labs.
