Week 7: how the body breaks down food and uses it

The GI tract is one continuous tube about nine meters long. Food enters at the mouth, gets mechanically and chemically broken down along the way, and leaves at the anus. The accessory organs (liver, gallbladder, pancreas, salivary glands) sit alongside and contribute enzymes, bile, and metabolic processing.

One section in this trainer covers COR-named content: Section 4 on the liver as metabolic hub. The lab anchors are TDEE calculation using Mifflin-St Jeor and a nutrition label audit, both of which Section 6 prepares you for.

Digestive Tract Anatomy

One continuous tube from mouth to anus, with a four-layer wall throughout. Accessory organs deliver enzymes and bile through ducts. The lab quiz will require identification of every major structure.

▾
RetrieveGI tract organs and wall layers
  1. 1.
    List the organs of the alimentary canal in order from mouth to anus.
    Mouth (oral cavity) → pharynx → esophagus → stomach → small intestine (duodenum, jejunum, ileum) → large intestine (cecum, ascending colon, transverse colon, descending colon, sigmoid colon, rectum) → anal canal and anus. Total length about 9 meters in the cadaver, shorter in life because of muscle tone.
  2. 2.
    Name the four layers of the GI tract wall from inside to outside, and identify what each layer contains.
    Mucosa: innermost, three sublayers (epithelium, lamina propria, muscularis mucosae). Absorption and secretion happen here. Submucosa: dense connective tissue with blood vessels, nerves (submucosal plexus), and glands. Muscularis externa: smooth muscle with two layers (inner circular, outer longitudinal) that produce peristalsis and segmentation. The myenteric plexus sits between them. Serosa: outermost, visceral peritoneum (or adventitia where the organ is retroperitoneal).
  3. 3.
    Name the four accessory digestive organs and what each contributes.
    Salivary glands (parotid, submandibular, sublingual): secrete saliva with amylase (carbohydrate digestion) and lysozyme. Liver: produces bile (emulsifies fats) and processes nutrients absorbed from the GI tract via the hepatic portal vein. Gallbladder: stores and concentrates bile, releases it into the duodenum after a fatty meal. Pancreas: exocrine cells secrete pancreatic juice (amylase, lipase, proteases, bicarbonate) into the duodenum; endocrine islets secrete insulin and glucagon.
  4. 4.
    Describe the peritoneum and distinguish intraperitoneal from retroperitoneal organs.
    The peritoneum is a serous membrane lining the abdominal cavity. Parietal peritoneum lines the body wall; visceral peritoneum covers organs. The space between contains a thin film of serous fluid for lubrication. Mesentery is a double layer of peritoneum that anchors organs to the body wall and carries vessels and nerves. Intraperitoneal organs (stomach, small intestine, transverse colon) hang from a mesentery. Retroperitoneal organs (kidneys, pancreas, ascending and descending colon, duodenum) sit behind the parietal peritoneum.
ApplyGI organ scenarios
a. A patient with cirrhosis (liver damage) develops impaired bile production. Predict the effect on fat digestion in the small intestine.
b. A patient has a cholecystectomy (gallbladder removal). Predict the effect on bile delivery and on tolerance for high-fat meals.
c. A patient with chronic pancreatitis has reduced pancreatic enzyme secretion. Predict three nutrient categories that will be malabsorbed and explain why.

Mechanical & Chemical Digestion

Mechanical digestion breaks food physically. Chemical digestion uses enzymes to break macromolecules into absorbable units. Each location handles a different macronutrient.

▾
RetrieveWhere digestion happens and which enzymes
  1. 1.
    Identify the major site of mechanical digestion at each location: mouth, stomach, and small intestine.
    Mouth: mastication (chewing) by teeth and tongue. Mixing with saliva forms a bolus. Stomach: churning by smooth muscle of three layers (longitudinal, circular, oblique). Combines with gastric juice to form chyme. Small intestine: segmentation, a back-and-forth chopping motion that mixes chyme with bile and pancreatic enzymes. Peristalsis moves material forward more slowly here.
  2. 2.
    Trace carbohydrate digestion from polysaccharides to absorbable monosaccharides, naming each enzyme and its location.
    (1) Salivary amylase (in mouth) begins breakdown of starch to disaccharides. Inactivated by stomach acid. (2) Pancreatic amylase (in duodenum) continues, producing disaccharides. (3) Brush border enzymes on small intestine microvilli finish the job: maltase (maltose to 2 glucose), sucrase (sucrose to glucose + fructose), lactase (lactose to glucose + galactose). Final products absorbed: glucose, galactose, fructose.
  3. 3.
    Trace protein digestion from intact protein to absorbable amino acids, naming each enzyme and its location.
    (1) Pepsin in the stomach (activated from pepsinogen by HCl) breaks proteins into peptides. (2) Pancreatic proteases in the duodenum (trypsin, chymotrypsin, carboxypeptidase) further fragment peptides. Secreted as inactive zymogens; trypsin activated by enterokinase, then trypsin activates the others. (3) Brush border peptidases finish breakdown to single amino acids and small peptides. Absorbed as amino acids primarily.
  4. 4.
    Trace lipid digestion, noting why fats need extra preparation before enzymatic breakdown.
    Lipids are not water-soluble, so enzymes (which are in aqueous fluid) cannot easily access them. (1) Bile from the liver and gallbladder emulsifies large fat droplets into many tiny droplets, increasing surface area. (2) Pancreatic lipase then breaks triglycerides into monoglycerides and free fatty acids. (3) These products combine with bile salts to form micelles, which deliver the lipids to the brush border for absorption. Inside the enterocyte, lipids are repackaged into chylomicrons that enter lacteals (lymph vessels), bypassing the hepatic portal circulation.

c. A patient has Zollinger-Ellison syndrome with severely high gastric acid. Predict the effect on pepsin activity, on the duodenal mucosa, and on the function of pancreatic enzymes that arrive in the duodenum.

Absorption in the Small Intestine

The small intestine is where almost all absorption happens. Three structural amplifiers boost surface area roughly 600-fold.

▾
RetrieveSurface area, transport mechanisms, vitamins
  1. 1.
    Name the three structural features that amplify small intestinal surface area, and the approximate fold increase from each.
    Circular folds (plicae circulares): permanent ridges in the inner wall, ~3-fold. Villi: finger-like projections of the mucosa, each ~1 mm tall, ~10-fold. Microvilli: brush border on each enterocyte, ~20-fold. Together about 600-fold increase, yielding ~200 m² of absorptive surface.
  2. 2.
    Compare the absorption mechanisms for glucose, fructose, and amino acids at the apical (luminal) membrane of the enterocyte.
    Glucose and galactose: secondary active transport via the SGLT1 cotransporter, coupled with Na⁺. Fructose: facilitated diffusion via GLUT5. Amino acids: mostly secondary active transport coupled with Na⁺ via family-specific transporters. All exit the basolateral side via passive carriers (GLUT2 for sugars) into capillaries that drain into the hepatic portal vein.
  3. 3.
    Trace fat absorption from the lumen to the lymphatic system.
    Monoglycerides and free fatty acids form micelles with bile salts → micelles ferry lipids to the brush border → lipids diffuse across the apical membrane into the enterocyte → reassembled into triglycerides → packaged with cholesterol and apoproteins into chylomicrons → exit the basolateral side into lacteals (lymphatic capillaries in the villi) → travel via the thoracic duct into the left subclavian vein. Fats bypass the liver on first pass.
  4. 4.
    Distinguish water-soluble and fat-soluble vitamin absorption, and identify why vitamin B₁₂ has a special requirement.
    Water-soluble vitamins (B vitamins, vitamin C): absorbed by simple or facilitated diffusion across the small intestine, into capillaries with the rest of the water-soluble nutrients. Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K): absorbed with dietary fat in chylomicrons via the lymphatic system. Require fat in the meal and adequate bile. Vitamin B₁₂: requires intrinsic factor, a glycoprotein secreted by parietal cells in the stomach. The B₁₂-IF complex is absorbed in the distal ileum. Deficiency causes pernicious anemia and neurologic symptoms.

c. A patient with cystic fibrosis has pancreatic exocrine insufficiency (low lipase). Predict their fat-soluble vitamin status and explain why this is a chronic problem.

The Liver as Metabolic Hub

Almost everything absorbed from the GI tract goes through the liver before reaching the rest of the body. The liver processes nutrients, makes plasma proteins and bile, detoxifies drugs and hormones, and manages blood glucose. Section 23.6 is COR-named for this course.

▾
RetrievePortal circulation and the liver's many jobs
  1. 1.
    Trace the hepatic portal circulation and explain the first-pass effect.
    Capillaries in the GI tract → hepatic portal vein → liver sinusoids (a second capillary bed) → hepatic veins → IVC → right heart. This means everything absorbed from the gut (except fats in chylomicrons) reaches the liver before circulating to the rest of the body. First-pass effect: the liver can metabolize, store, or detoxify substances before they reach systemic circulation. This is why oral drug doses are often higher than IV doses, and why the liver is the first to be exposed to oral toxins.
  2. 2.
    List the liver's major roles in carbohydrate, protein, and lipid metabolism.
    Carbohydrates: glycogenesis (storage when blood glucose is high), glycogenolysis (release when low), gluconeogenesis (making new glucose from amino acids and lactate during fasting). The liver is the central blood glucose regulator. Proteins: synthesizes most plasma proteins (albumin, clotting factors I-V-VII-IX-X, and others); deaminates amino acids; runs the urea cycle to detoxify ammonia. Lipids: synthesizes cholesterol and lipoproteins (VLDL, HDL); produces bile salts from cholesterol; performs ketogenesis during prolonged fasting.
  3. 3.
    Describe bile production and storage. Identify the components and the function of bile.
    The liver produces ~600 to 1000 mL of bile per day. Bile is stored and concentrated in the gallbladder between meals. After a fatty meal, CCK from the duodenum triggers gallbladder contraction, releasing bile into the duodenum. Components: bile salts (made from cholesterol, the active ingredient for fat emulsification), bilirubin (from RBC breakdown, the brown-yellow pigment), cholesterol, and electrolytes. Function: emulsifies fats; eliminates bilirubin and excess cholesterol.
  4. 4.
    Describe the liver's detoxification roles, including drugs, hormones, and bilirubin handling.
    Drugs and toxins: the cytochrome P450 system in hepatocytes oxidizes drugs (Phase I), then conjugates them (Phase II) to make them water-soluble for excretion in bile or urine. Hormones: the liver clears circulating insulin, thyroid hormone, sex hormones, and others. Bilirubin: when RBCs are broken down (in the spleen), heme is converted to unconjugated (indirect) bilirubin. The liver conjugates it (adds glucuronic acid) and excretes it in bile. Failure at any step causes jaundice: pre-hepatic (hemolysis), hepatic (liver disease), or post-hepatic (biliary obstruction).

Macronutrients & Micronutrients

Macronutrients (carbohydrates, fats, proteins) provide energy. Micronutrients (vitamins, minerals) support enzyme and structural functions in trace amounts. Both have dietary recommendations.

▾
RetrieveMacronutrients, AMDR, vitamins, minerals
  1. 1.
    State the energy value (kcal/g) of carbohydrates, proteins, fats, and alcohol.
    Carbohydrate: 4 kcal/g. Protein: 4 kcal/g. Fat: 9 kcal/g (more than twice as energy-dense as carb or protein). Alcohol: 7 kcal/g (provides energy but no other nutrients, so often called "empty calories"). Knowing these lets you reverse-engineer caloric content from a nutrition label.
  2. 2.
    State the Acceptable Macronutrient Distribution Ranges (AMDRs) and explain what they represent.
    Carbohydrates: 45 to 65% of total calories. Fat: 20 to 35% (with limits on saturated and trans fat). Protein: 10 to 35%. AMDRs are the percentage ranges associated with reduced risk of chronic disease while providing essential nutrients. They define a flexible target rather than a single optimal value.
  3. 3.
    Distinguish essential and non-essential amino acids and fatty acids, and explain why "essential" matters for diet planning.
    "Essential" means the body cannot synthesize it from other compounds, so it must come from food. Nine essential amino acids (e.g., lysine, methionine, leucine, valine). Animal proteins are "complete" because they contain all nine; plant proteins often miss one or more, so vegetarians need protein variety. Two essential fatty acids: linoleic acid (omega-6) and alpha-linolenic acid (omega-3). Found in plant oils, nuts, seeds, fatty fish.
  4. 4.
    Match three vitamin or mineral deficiencies to the disease they cause: vitamin C, vitamin D, iron.
    Vitamin C deficiency: scurvy (impaired collagen synthesis, bleeding gums, poor wound healing, joint pain). Vitamin D deficiency: rickets in children (soft, deformed bones), osteomalacia in adults (bone pain, weakness). Reduced calcium absorption is the underlying mechanism. Iron deficiency: microcytic hypochromic anemia (small, pale RBCs because hemoglobin synthesis is impaired). Symptoms: fatigue, pallor, shortness of breath.

b. A patient on a very low-fat diet (less than 10% of calories from fat) develops night vision problems and easy bruising over six months. Identify the deficiency category and the mechanism.
c. A patient drinks heavily and obtains 30% of their calories from alcohol. Predict the consequence for vitamin status and overall nutrition, and explain why "empty calories" is a fitting description.

TDEE Calculation & Reading Nutrition Labels

Two clinical tools for this week's lab. Mifflin-St Jeor estimates BMR, then activity multiplier scales to TDEE. Nutrition labels follow a standard format with Daily Values for comparison.

▾
RetrieveFormula, factors, label structure
  1. 1.
    State the Mifflin-St Jeor equation for basal metabolic rate (BMR) for males and females.
    Inputs: weight (W) in kilograms, height (H) in centimeters, age (A) in years.
    Male BMR = (10 × W) + (6.25 × H) - (5 × A) + 5
    Female BMR = (10 × W) + (6.25 × H) - (5 × A) - 161
    This is the number of kilocalories the body burns at rest in 24 hours just to maintain basic functions. Mifflin-St Jeor is the most widely used and accurate equation for healthy adults.
  2. 2.
    List the standard activity multipliers used to convert BMR to TDEE (total daily energy expenditure).
    1.2 Sedentary: little to no exercise, desk job. 1.375 Lightly active: light exercise 1 to 3 days per week. 1.55 Moderately active: moderate exercise 3 to 5 days per week. 1.725 Very active: hard exercise 6 to 7 days per week. 1.9 Extremely active: very hard daily exercise plus physical job or training twice per day. TDEE = BMR × activity factor.
  3. 3.
    List the major elements of a US nutrition label, in order from top to bottom.
    (1) Serving size and servings per container at the top. Read this first; portions can be deceiving. (2) Calories per serving (large font since 2016). (3) Macronutrients with %DV: total fat (saturated, trans), cholesterol, sodium, total carbohydrate (dietary fiber, total sugars including added sugars), protein. (4) Micronutrients with %DV: vitamin D, calcium, iron, potassium (these four are required since 2016). (5) Footnote: "% Daily Values are based on a 2000 calorie diet."
  4. 4.
    Identify the four most useful red flags to audit on a packaged food label.
    Added sugars: Daily Value cap is 50 g (10% of calories). One 20-oz soda has more than this. Saturated fat: DV cap 20 g. Sodium: DV cap 2300 mg. Many processed foods exceed 30% DV per serving. Fiber: DV target 28 g (more is better). General rule: %DV ≤ 5% is low; ≥ 20% is high. Aim for low %DV on the cap nutrients (added sugar, saturated fat, sodium) and high %DV on the target nutrients (fiber, vitamin D, calcium, iron, potassium).
Mifflin-St Jeor formulas:
Male BMR = (10 × kg) + (6.25 × cm) - (5 × years) + 5
Female BMR = (10 × kg) + (6.25 × cm) - (5 × years) - 161
TDEE = BMR × activity factor (1.2 to 1.9)

c. A patient eats two servings of the food in part b. Recalculate the calories, added sugars, and sodium consumed, and predict whether they have exceeded any of the daily caps in this single snack.
