How this workbook works

Print, handwrite, scan, submit. This is the Anatomy Workbook for Week 8, the final week of the course. Complete this first, then move to the Physiology Workbook for the EEG & lifespan integration dry lab.

This week integrates everything. Reproduction connects endocrine (Week 3), vascular (Week 5), and tissue physiology (Week 1). Pregnancy adds a developing organism with two circulations sharing one body. Aging touches every organ system you have studied. The lab exam will test reproductive anatomy and pregnancy/placental anatomy heavily, so identify every structure carefully.

Recall structures from memory. No word banks. Drawing tasks and synthesis questions are weighted heavily.

Anatomy · Drawing from Memory

Capstone integration: HPG axis and fetal-to-newborn circulation transition

Without referencing figures above, draw two integrated diagrams. Label every component.

Drawing 1: Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Gonadal (HPG) axis

Draw the full HPG axis for either male or female (your choice). Required components: hypothalamus releasing GnRH, anterior pituitary releasing FSH and LH, gonad (testis or ovary) producing sex hormones, target tissues, AND negative feedback arrows from sex hormones back to hypothalamus and pituitary. If you choose female, include positive feedback at mid-cycle (LH surge).

Drawing space: HPG axis with feedback loops

Drawing 2: Fetal-to-newborn circulation transition

Draw two side-by-side hearts: fetal circulation (with all three shunts open and umbilical vessels active) and newborn circulation (after first breath). Show what closes, what opens, and why. Required components: foramen ovale, ductus arteriosus, ductus venosus, umbilical vessels, pulmonary circuit (with arrows showing blood flow direction in each scenario). For the newborn, indicate why pulmonary vascular resistance drops and how that drives the foramen ovale to close.

Drawing space: fetal vs newborn circulation
Synthesis question

A baby is born with a persistent ductus arteriosus that fails to close in the first days of life. Using your fetal-to-newborn drawing, predict the cardiovascular consequences: where will blood flow inappropriately, how will this affect the lungs, and why might the baby develop signs of heart failure?
