Physiology · Lab Skill 1

EEG basics: the four major brainwave frequencies

Recognize the standard EEG waveforms by frequency, amplitude, and the state of consciousness each represents.

The four major EEG frequencies (memorize)

Beta (β): 13-30 Hz, low amplitude, fast. Awake, alert, eyes open, actively thinking. Beta dominates the awake adult EEG.

Alpha (α): 8-12 Hz, medium amplitude. Awake, relaxed, eyes closed. Best seen over occipital regions when eyes are closed.

Theta (θ): 4-7 Hz, medium-high amplitude. Drowsy state, light sleep (NREM stage 1). In awake adults, abnormal and may suggest pathology.

Delta (δ): 0.5-3 Hz, high amplitude, slow. Deep sleep (NREM stages 3-4) and pathologic states (encephalopathy, severe brain dysfunction).

Sleep stage architecture (NREM and REM)

Stage N1 (light sleep): low-amplitude theta. Brief transition from wake.

Stage N2: theta with sleep spindles and K-complexes. Most of the night.

Stage N3 (deep / slow-wave sleep): dominant delta waves. Restorative sleep.

REM sleep: low-amplitude mixed-frequency activity (resembles awake EEG), but with skeletal muscle paralysis and rapid eye movements. Dreams.

For each EEG description, identify the dominant frequency band and the most likely state of consciousness:

Low amplitude, fast waves at ~20 Hz, recorded in a college student taking an exam.
Medium amplitude waves at 10 Hz dominant over occipital leads, patient lying with eyes closed but awake.

Physiology · Lab Skill 2

Pathologic EEG patterns: seizure, coma, and brain death

Recognize the EEG signatures of seizures (focal and generalized), coma states, and the isoelectric pattern of brain death.

Seizure patterns

Focal (partial) seizure: abnormal rhythmic spikes or sharp waves localized to one brain region. The patient may have focal motor symptoms (e.g., right arm jerking) corresponding to the affected cortex.

Generalized tonic-clonic seizure: high-amplitude, high-frequency rhythmic discharges across all leads simultaneously. Patient is unconscious with whole-body convulsions.

Absence (petit mal) seizure: classic 3 Hz spike-and-wave pattern across all leads. Brief loss of awareness without convulsions. Most common in children.

Status epilepticus: continuous or recurrent seizure activity for > 5 minutes (or repeated seizures without recovery between). EEG shows persistent epileptiform discharges. Medical emergency.

Coma and brain death EEG

Coma (reversible): slow background (delta predominant) but EEG activity is present. Some reactivity to stimuli may be seen.

Burst suppression: alternating periods of high-amplitude activity ("bursts") separated by isoelectric periods ("suppression"). Seen in deep anesthesia, severe brain injury, hypothermia, or barbiturate coma.

Isoelectric (electrocerebral silence): a flat line, no electrical activity from the cortex. Required for brain death determination in the presence of supportive clinical findings.

Physiology · Lab Skill 3

Brain death determination and the apnea test

Recognize the clinical and ancillary criteria for brain death, and walk the apnea test step by step.

Clinical criteria for brain death (all required)

1. Coma with a known irreversible cause (no metabolic, toxic, or pharmacologic confounders).

2. Absence of brainstem reflexes: no pupillary light response, no corneal reflex, no oculocephalic (doll's eyes) response, no oculovestibular (cold caloric) response, no gag, no cough.

3. Apnea confirmed by formal apnea test.

The apnea test: step by step

1. Pre-test prep: patient is normothermic (≥ 36°C), normotensive (SBP ≥ 100), euvolemic, normal electrolytes, no sedatives or paralytics on board, normal pre-test PaCO₂ (~35-45 mmHg).

2. Pre-oxygenate with 100% O₂ for 10 minutes to fill lung reserves.

3. Disconnect the ventilator. Provide passive O₂ via tracheal cannula at 6 L/min.

4. Observe for spontaneous respiratory effort for 8-10 minutes.

5. Draw repeat ABG at the end.

Result interpretation:

Positive (consistent with brain death): no respiratory effort observed AND PaCO₂ rises to ≥ 60 mmHg (or rises ≥ 20 mmHg above baseline). The brainstem chemoreceptors normally trigger breathing at PaCO₂ ~ 40 mmHg; failure to breathe at 60 mmHg = brainstem is non-functional.

Test must be aborted if patient becomes hypotensive, hypoxic, or has an arrhythmia. The test is reattempted later with stabilization.

Case 1 · Pregnancy

The 36-week-pregnant patient with breathlessness

A 32-year-old woman, 36 weeks pregnant with her second child, reports increasing shortness of breath and ankle swelling. BP 134/86, HR 96, SpO₂ 96% room air. Lungs clear. Ankle edema 2+. ABG: pH 7.45, PaCO₂ 32, HCO₃⁻ 22, PaO₂ 102. CBC: Hb 10.4 g/dL (mildly low). Glucose 124. Urine dipstick: trace protein, no glucose.

  1. Her ABG shows mild respiratory alkalosis. Why is this normal in pregnancy? Reference progesterone's effect on the brainstem respiratory center.
  2. Her hemoglobin is mildly low. Is this anemia of pregnancy or pathologic? Explain plasma volume expansion vs RBC mass.
  3. Her BP is mildly elevated and she has 2+ edema with trace proteinuria. What pregnancy-specific condition should be ruled out? What organ systems does it affect?
  4. Her glucose is 124. Does she meet criteria for gestational diabetes (you may reference Week 3 OGTT criteria)? What further test would confirm?
Case 2 · Newborn

The premature infant with respiratory distress

A baby born at 30 weeks gestation, weighing 1.4 kg, develops grunting respirations, retractions, and SpO₂ 84% on room air at 30 minutes of life. Chest X-ray shows ground-glass opacities throughout both lungs.

  1. What anatomical structure is incompletely developed in this premature infant's lungs, and which cell normally produces what is missing? Reference the alveolar histology from Week 6.
  2. Why does the absence of this substance cause the alveoli to collapse? Reference surface tension and the ground-glass appearance on X-ray.
  3. The treatment is exogenous surfactant administration. How is it delivered, and what immediate physiologic improvement should you expect?
  4. Why did the team also evaluate this baby for a patent ductus arteriosus? Reference the Week 8 anatomy fetal-circulation transition.
Case 3 · Adolescent

The 14-year-old with delayed puberty

A 14-year-old girl is brought in because she has not yet started menstruating and shows minimal breast development. Height 4'10". She reports she "always feels cold," has been increasingly tired, and her hair has thinned. Labs: low FSH, low LH, low estradiol, elevated prolactin, low T4 with high TSH.

  1. Map her hormone results to the HPG axis you drew in the Anatomy Workbook. Where is the dysfunction (hypothalamus, pituitary, ovary, or feedback)?
  2. The combination of cold intolerance, fatigue, hair thinning, and elevated TSH points to which other endocrine disorder? Reference Week 3 endocrine anatomy.
  3. How can her primary thyroid disease cause low LH/FSH and delayed puberty? (Hint: TRH cross-stimulation of prolactin and impact on GnRH pulsatility.)
  4. If treated appropriately, what would you expect to see in her LH, FSH, and estradiol over the following months?
Case 4 · Older Adult

The 84-year-old with multi-system decline

An 84-year-old woman is brought in by family for evaluation. Over the past year: she has fallen 3 times, lost 10 lbs unintentionally, started forgetting names, has urinary incontinence, walks with a stooped posture, and gets short of breath climbing one flight of stairs. Vitals: BP 158/86 supine, 128/72 standing (orthostatic), HR 72. Labs: Cr 1.4 (eGFR 38), HbA1c 6.2%, TSH normal, B12 mildly low.

  1. For each of her findings, identify which organ system is showing age-related change and reference the specific anatomical change you noted in the Anatomy Workbook Section 09 table.
  2. Her orthostatic hypotension is concerning. Which two age-related changes (cardiovascular AND autonomic) make her vulnerable? Reference Weeks 3 and 5.
  3. Her eGFR is 38 (CKD stage 3b). Which medications need dose adjustment because of this? Why does this matter as you assess her current medication list?
  4. Looking at the whole picture: what is the single most modifiable factor you would address first to reduce her fall risk?
Case 5 · End of Life

The brain death determination

A 19-year-old man is admitted to the ICU after a motorcycle crash with severe traumatic brain injury. After 48 hours of supportive care, he meets clinical criteria for brain death evaluation: GCS 3, no pupillary, corneal, oculocephalic, oculovestibular, gag, or cough reflexes. Normothermic. Off all sedation for 24 hours. Pre-test labs and ABG are within normal limits. The team prepares to perform the apnea test.

  1. Walk the apnea test step by step (referencing Section 03). What pre-test conditions must be met before the test can begin?
  2. The test runs 9 minutes with no respiratory effort. Pre-test PaCO₂ was 40, post-test PaCO₂ is 66. What does this result mean physiologically? Reference the brainstem chemoreceptors' role in normal breathing.
  3. The team also performs an EEG, which shows electrocerebral silence (flat across all leads). The team confirms brain death. Explain to the family in plain terms what brain death means and why it differs from "coma."
  4. The patient's organs are donated. The heart will be transplanted. Reference Week 5 anatomy: what is happening to the heart's electrical conduction in a brain-dead donor that allows the heart to continue beating until cross-clamp?
