Reproductive Anatomy: Both Sexes

Two systems built around the same goal: producing gametes and providing for fertilization. The lab quiz will require identification of structures in both sexes.

▾
RetrieveMale and female anatomy and hormones
  1. 1.
    Trace the path of sperm from production to ejaculation, naming each duct and accessory gland.
    Sperm produced in seminiferous tubules of the testes → rete testis → epididymis (maturation and storage) → vas (ductus) deferens → ejaculatory duct (joined by seminal vesicle duct) → prostatic urethra → membranous urethra → spongy (penile) urethra → exit. Accessory glands: seminal vesicles (fructose, prostaglandins, ~60% of semen volume), prostate gland (alkaline fluid, ~30%), bulbourethral (Cowper's) glands (pre-ejaculate mucus).
  2. 2.
    Name the three layers of the uterus and trace the path of an oocyte from ovary to uterus.
    Uterine layers from outer to inner: perimetrium (serosa), myometrium (smooth muscle, contracts during labor), endometrium (mucosa, sheds during menstruation). Ovum path: oocyte released from ovarian follicle at ovulation → swept into fimbriae of the fallopian (uterine) tube → travels via cilia and peristalsis along the tube → fertilization typically occurs in the ampulla → zygote enters the uterus and implants in the endometrium.
  3. 3.
    Trace the male hormonal axis from hypothalamus to testicular hormone output.
    Hypothalamus releases GnRH → anterior pituitary releases LH and FSH. LH acts on Leydig cells (interstitial cells) → produce testosterone. FSH acts on Sertoli cells in the seminiferous tubules → support spermatogenesis. Negative feedback: testosterone inhibits hypothalamus and pituitary; inhibin (from Sertoli cells) selectively inhibits FSH.
  4. 4.
    Describe the female ovarian cycle (follicular, ovulation, luteal phases) and the corresponding uterine cycle.
    Ovarian cycle: Follicular phase (days 1-13): FSH stimulates follicle growth; the dominant follicle produces estrogen. Ovulation (~day 14): LH surge ruptures the follicle, releasing the oocyte. Luteal phase (days 14-28): the ruptured follicle becomes the corpus luteum, which produces progesterone and estrogen. If no fertilization, corpus luteum degenerates and hormones drop. Uterine cycle: Menstrual phase (days 1-5): endometrium sheds. Proliferative phase (days 6-14): estrogen rebuilds the endometrium. Secretory phase (days 15-28): progesterone makes endometrium glandular and receptive.

Fertilization, Pregnancy & Adjustments at Birth

Fertilization, implantation, and the dramatic physiological transitions of birth. Deep embryonic and fetal staging are optional in this course.

▾
RetrieveFertilization to birth
  1. 1.
    Describe fertilization, the first week after fertilization, and where implantation occurs.
    Fertilization typically happens in the ampulla of the fallopian tube within ~24 hours of ovulation. One sperm penetrates the zona pellucida and fuses with the oocyte to form a zygote (diploid). Over 6 to 7 days, the zygote undergoes cleavage (mitotic divisions without growth) → morula (16+ cell ball) → blastocyst (hollow ball with inner cell mass and trophoblast). The blastocyst implants in the endometrium of the uterus, typically in the upper posterior wall, around day 6 to 9 post-fertilization.
  2. 2.
    Name the major hormones of pregnancy and explain how the hormonal source shifts during the first trimester.
    Early pregnancy: the trophoblast secretes hCG (human chorionic gonadotropin), which mimics LH and keeps the corpus luteum alive past its normal lifespan. The corpus luteum produces estrogen and progesterone to maintain the endometrium. Around weeks 8 to 12, the placenta takes over hormone production, secreting estrogen, progesterone, and human placental lactogen (hPL). hCG drops after the first trimester. hCG is the basis of urine and blood pregnancy tests.
  3. 3.
    Describe the three stages of labor.
    Stage 1: Dilation. Cervix effaces and dilates from 0 to 10 cm. Begins with regular contractions; ends when cervix is fully dilated. Longest stage (6 to 12+ hours in first pregnancy). Stage 2: Expulsion. Mother actively pushes; baby moves through birth canal and is delivered. Typically 30 minutes to 2 hours. Stage 3: Placental. Placenta detaches and is expelled, usually within 30 minutes after birth. Uterine contractions help control postpartum bleeding.
  4. 4.
    Describe the four major adjustments at birth as the newborn transitions to extrauterine life.
    (1) First breath: umbilical cord clamping ends placental gas exchange; CO₂ rises. The newborn takes a strong negative-pressure breath, expanding the lungs for the first time. (2) Foramen ovale closes: increased left atrial pressure (now that the lungs have blood flow) pushes the flap closed; eventually fuses to become the fossa ovalis. (3) Ductus arteriosus closes: rising arterial O₂ and falling prostaglandins trigger constriction; eventually fibroses to become the ligamentum arteriosum. (4) Ductus venosus closes: blood now passes through the liver; remnant becomes the ligamentum venosum. The hepatic portal circulation activates.
ApplyPregnancy and birth scenarios

c. A 39-week-pregnant patient is in stage 1 labor for 14 hours but is only 5 cm dilated. Identify what stage 1 progress should look like, predict what would happen if labor stalls, and identify one drug that augments uterine contractions.

Lactation

Mammary glands produce milk under hormonal control. Two distinct hormones do two distinct jobs: synthesis and ejection.

▾
RetrieveHormones, composition, immunity
  1. 1.
    Identify the two key hormones of lactation and the distinct role of each.
    Prolactin (anterior pituitary) drives milk synthesis. Estrogen and progesterone during pregnancy prepare the breasts but inhibit prolactin's action; the drop in those hormones after delivery releases prolactin's effect. Suckling stimulates ongoing prolactin release. Oxytocin (posterior pituitary) drives milk ejection / let-down by causing contraction of myoepithelial cells around the alveoli. Released by neuroendocrine reflex when the infant suckles or sometimes when the mother hears the baby cry.
  2. 2.
    Compare colostrum and mature milk in composition and timing.
    Colostrum: first ~3 days after delivery. Yellowish, thick, low in volume but high in protein, especially secretory IgA. Provides passive immunity and seeds gut microbiome. Lower in fat and lactose than mature milk. Mature milk: appears around day 3 to 5. Higher fat and lactose for energy, lower protein concentration. Continues to contain IgA, lactoferrin, lysozyme, and live immune cells. The composition shifts within a single feed too (foremilk thinner, hindmilk fattier).
  3. 3.
    List the major immune components of breast milk and explain why breastfeeding is described as passive immunity.
    Breast milk contains secretory IgA (binds pathogens at mucosal surfaces in the infant gut), lactoferrin (binds iron, starving bacteria), lysozyme (degrades bacterial cell walls), and live maternal leukocytes (macrophages, lymphocytes). Because these are pre-formed antibodies and cells from the mother (not produced by the infant's own immune system), this is passive immunity. It is immediate but temporary: it only lasts as long as breastfeeding continues, and the infant must develop its own active immunity over time.
  4. 4.
    Explain why breastfeeding can suppress ovulation.
    Frequent suckling maintains high prolactin levels. Prolactin inhibits hypothalamic GnRH release, which suppresses LH and FSH from the anterior pituitary. Without an LH surge, ovulation does not occur. This is called lactational amenorrhea. It is most reliable in the first six months when feeding is exclusive, on demand, and frequent. Less reliable as feedings space out.
ApplyLactation scenarios
a. A new mother reports that her milk does not seem to "let down" when feeding. Identify the hormone responsible for milk ejection and predict three things that might inhibit the let-down reflex.
b. A premature infant is fed donor breast milk specifically because of the immune protection. Identify the major immune components and explain why this matters more in a preemie than in a full-term infant.
c. A breastfeeding patient mistakenly assumes she cannot get pregnant. Identify the conditions under which lactational amenorrhea is most reliable, and explain why combined oral contraceptives are sometimes avoided in breastfeeding.

Synthesis · Aging across systems

Aging Physiology Across Systems

Every system you have studied this term changes with age. The pattern is loss of reserve: function is adequate at rest but the buffer for stress shrinks. This section pulls prior weeks back through an aging lens.

▾
RetrieveAging changes by system
  1. 1.
    Describe major aging changes in skin, bone, and skeletal muscle (Weeks 1-2 systems).
    Skin: thinner epidermis, less elastin and collagen (wrinkles), decreased sebum and sweat (dryness, heat intolerance), slower wound healing, decreased melanocyte function (graying, more burn risk). Bone: osteoblast activity declines while osteoclast activity does not, so bone density falls. Postmenopausal estrogen drop accelerates loss in females (osteoporosis). Hip and vertebral fracture risk rises. Muscle: sarcopenia (loss of muscle mass and strength), partly due to motor neuron loss, decreased anabolic hormones, and reduced activity. Strength training partially preserves it.
  2. 2.
    Describe major aging changes in nervous, special senses, and endocrine systems (Weeks 3-4 systems).
    Nervous: brain volume slowly decreases; processing speed slows; some memory consolidation declines (though crystallized intelligence is preserved). Risk of dementia rises with age. Autonomic responses blunted (orthostatic hypotension more common). Special senses: presbyopia (lens stiffens, near vision worsens); presbycusis (high-frequency hearing loss); reduced taste and smell sensitivity; reduced proprioception (fall risk). Endocrine: menopause (ovarian estrogen drops abruptly around age 50); andropause (testosterone gradually declines); thyroid hormone metabolism slows; insulin resistance often increases.
  3. 3.
    Describe major aging changes in cardiovascular, respiratory, and renal systems (Weeks 5-6 systems).
    Cardiovascular: arterial stiffening (raises systolic BP, isolated systolic hypertension), maximum heart rate decreases (~220 minus age), atherosclerosis progresses, baroreceptor reflex blunts (orthostatic intolerance). Respiratory: chest wall stiffens; alveolar elastic recoil decreases; vital capacity falls; residual volume rises; cough reflex weakens (aspiration and pneumonia risk). Renal: nephrons are lost; GFR declines by ~1 mL/min per year after age 40; concentrating ability falls (dehydration risk); drug clearance decreases (medication dosing must adjust).
  4. 4.
    Describe major aging changes in digestive, immune, and reproductive systems (Weeks 7-8 systems).
    Digestive: reduced saliva, reduced stomach acid (lower B₁₂ absorption, more bacterial overgrowth), slower GI motility (constipation), reduced liver mass and CYP450 activity (slower drug metabolism). Immune: immunosenescence: thymic involution, reduced naive T cells, blunted antibody response to new antigens (why vaccines work less well in elderly), increased autoimmunity. Reproductive: female menopause around age 45 to 55 with abrupt estrogen drop and end of fertility; male testosterone declines slowly with age, fertility preserved into late life but reduced; both have reduced sexual response, vaginal atrophy, and erectile changes.

Lab anchor · APGAR & Naegele's Rule

Newborn Scoring & Pregnancy Dating

Two clinical tools used in this week's lab. APGAR scores newborn condition at one and five minutes. Naegele's Rule estimates due date from last menstrual period.

▾
RetrieveAPGAR components and Naegele's Rule
  1. 1.
    Identify the five components of APGAR and the score for each (0, 1, or 2).
    Appearance (color): 0 blue/pale, 1 pink body with blue extremities (acrocyanosis), 2 completely pink. Pulse: 0 absent, 1 less than 100, 2 greater than 100. Grimace (reflex irritability to nasal suction): 0 no response, 1 grimace, 2 cough/sneeze/cry. Activity (muscle tone): 0 limp, 1 some flexion, 2 active motion. Respiratory effort: 0 absent, 1 weak/irregular, 2 strong cry. Total 0 to 10.
  2. 2.
    State the timing of APGAR scoring and the score ranges for routine, fairly low, and critically low.
    Scored at 1 minute after birth (reflects intrapartum status) and 5 minutes after birth (reflects response to resuscitation). If the 5-minute score is below 7, scoring is repeated every 5 minutes until 20 minutes. 7 to 10: generally normal, routine care. 4 to 6: moderately depressed, may need stimulation, suction, or oxygen. 0 to 3: critically depressed, immediate resuscitation indicated. APGAR is a tool to flag and monitor, not a predictor of long-term outcome.
  3. 3.
    State Naegele's Rule for estimating the due date and explain its assumptions.
    Naegele's Rule: Estimated date of delivery (EDD) = first day of last menstrual period (LMP) + 7 days - 3 months + 1 year. Equivalent to LMP + 280 days. Assumptions: regular 28-day cycles with ovulation on day 14, fertilization shortly after, and pregnancy of about 40 weeks gestation from LMP. The rule overestimates pregnancy length for someone with a longer cycle and underestimates for a shorter cycle. Ultrasound dating in the first trimester is more accurate.
  4. 4.
    Describe how trimesters are defined and what milestone happens at each.
    First trimester: weeks 1 to 12. Most organogenesis completes by end of week 8 (especially weeks 3 to 8). Highest risk of teratogenic injury and miscarriage. Second trimester: weeks 13 to 27. Quickening (felt fetal movement) around 18 to 22 weeks. Viability threshold typically around 24 weeks. Third trimester: weeks 28 to 40. Major fetal growth, lung surfactant production, brain development. Term: 37 to 42 weeks. Preterm: before 37 weeks.
Naegele's Rule: EDD = LMP + 7 days − 3 months + 1 year (= LMP + 280 days)
APGAR: A (color), P (pulse), G (grimace), A (activity), R (respiration). Each scored 0/1/2. Total 0-10. Done at 1 min and 5 min.
ApplyScore and date
a. A newborn at 1 minute is pink with blue hands and feet, has a heart rate of 130, cries vigorously when suctioned, has active flexion, and is breathing strongly with a loud cry. Calculate the APGAR score by component and total. Interpret the score.
b. A newborn at 1 minute is bluish all over, heart rate 80, no response to suction, limp, with weak irregular gasps. Calculate the APGAR score and identify the immediate clinical action.
c. A patient's last menstrual period began on July 10, 2026. Apply Naegele's Rule to estimate her due date. Show your work.

USPSTF Age-Based Screening

The US Preventive Services Task Force recommends evidence-based screenings by age. This is where the eight weeks of A&P content show up at the bedside, and where prevention happens.

▾
RetrieveMajor USPSTF age-based screenings
  1. 1.
    Identify the USPSTF letter grades A, B, C, D, and I, and what each means for screening recommendations.
    A: high certainty of substantial net benefit. Recommended. B: high certainty of moderate, or moderate certainty of moderate to substantial, net benefit. Recommended. C: small net benefit. Selectively offer based on individual circumstances. D: no benefit or harms outweigh benefits. Recommend against. I: insufficient evidence. Cannot make a recommendation. Most clinical screening guidelines fall under A or B.
  2. 2.
    Identify the USPSTF age ranges and methods for cervical, breast, and colorectal cancer screening (Grade A or B recommendations).
    Cervical cancer: ages 21 to 29 cytology every 3 years; ages 30 to 65 hrHPV alone every 5 years, co-testing every 5 years, or cytology every 3 years; stop at 65 with adequate prior screening. Breast cancer: biennial mammography for women ages 40 to 74 (updated recommendation lowering start age to 40). Colorectal cancer: ages 45 to 75. Multiple options: colonoscopy every 10 years, FIT annually, stool DNA every 3 years, flexible sigmoidoscopy plus FIT, or CT colonography every 5 years.
  3. 3.
    Identify the USPSTF criteria for lung cancer, abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA), and osteoporosis screening.
    Lung cancer: annual low-dose CT (LDCT) for adults ages 50 to 80 with a 20+ pack-year smoking history, currently smoking or quit within 15 years. AAA: one-time abdominal ultrasound for men ages 65 to 75 who have ever smoked. Selective consideration for never-smoking men in this age range. Osteoporosis: DEXA bone density for women age 65 and older, and for postmenopausal women under 65 at increased fracture risk based on tools like FRAX.
  4. 4.
    Identify the USPSTF screening for hypertension, diabetes (type 2), and the use of statins for primary prevention. Connect each to a prior week of this course.
    Hypertension: screen all adults age 18+ with office BP measurement, with confirmatory readings outside the office before starting treatment. Connects to Week 5 (BP regulation, baroreceptor reflex, RAAS). Type 2 diabetes: screen adults ages 35 to 70 who are overweight or obese. Connects to Week 3 (insulin/glucagon, COR-named) and Week 7 (nutrition, TDEE). Statins for primary prevention: ages 40 to 75 with at least one cardiovascular risk factor and 10-year ASCVD risk of 10% or higher. Connects to Week 5 (ASCVD calculator).

The eight-week ribbon

USPSTF screening pulls every prior week into one clinical view. Cervical and breast cancer screening (Week 8 reproductive). Colorectal screening (Week 7 digestive). Lung cancer screening (Week 6 respiratory). Hypertension and statins (Week 5 cardiovascular). Diabetes screening (Week 3 endocrine, Week 7 nutrition). Osteoporosis screening (Week 2 skeletal, Week 8 menopause). The body you have studied for eight weeks is the body the screening guidelines were built to protect.

Synthesis · Death, Dying & Brain Death

End of the Lifespan

The lifespan ends. This section serves both pre-nursing and mortuary science students. Two definitions of death are recognized in US law. The body undergoes predictable postmortem changes that have forensic and embalming significance. Brain death has a specific clinical exam protocol.

▾
RetrieveDefinitions, postmortem changes, brain death exam
  1. 1.
    State the two legally recognized definitions of death under the Uniform Determination of Death Act (UDDA), and identify what evidence is required for each.
    The Uniform Determination of Death Act (1981), adopted in some form by all 50 states, recognizes two legal definitions of death. (1) Cardiopulmonary death: irreversible cessation of circulatory and respiratory functions. Confirmed at the bedside by absent pulse, absent spontaneous respiration, and unresponsiveness, with confirmation that resuscitation will not restore function. (2) Brain death: irreversible cessation of all functions of the entire brain, including the brainstem. Either definition is sufficient to declare legal death. Brain death matters most when the patient is on mechanical ventilation, because the heart can keep beating even after brain function is gone.
  2. 2.
    Name the five major postmortem changes in order, with approximate timing.
    Pallor mortis: paleness within 15 to 20 minutes after circulatory arrest as blood drains from skin capillaries. Algor mortis: body cooling, approximately 1.5°F per hour at room temperature, slower in obese or clothed bodies and faster in lean or unclothed ones. Continues until body temperature equilibrates with the environment. Livor mortis: purple-red discoloration in dependent areas as blood pools by gravity. Onset 1 to 2 hours, becomes fixed (will not blanch with pressure or shift with repositioning) by 8 to 12 hours. Rigor mortis: stiffening of muscles. Onset 2 to 6 hours, peaks around 12 hours, resolves over 24 to 48 hours. Decomposition: autolysis and bacterial putrefaction begin within 24 to 72 hours at room temperature, accelerated by heat and humidity.
  3. 3.
    Describe the prerequisites for a brain death exam and the three clinical findings required to declare brain death.
    Prerequisites (must be met before exam): (a) known cause of catastrophic and irreversible brain injury (e.g., severe TBI, anoxic injury, massive stroke), confirmed by imaging; (b) no confounders that could mimic brain death: body temperature ≥36°C (no hypothermia), no sedating drugs or paralytics on board, no severe metabolic derangement, no severe hypotension. Three required clinical findings: (1) Coma with no responsiveness to noxious stimuli. (2) Absent brainstem reflexes: fixed pupils, absent corneal reflex, absent oculocephalic (doll's eyes) and oculovestibular (cold caloric) responses, absent gag and cough reflexes. (3) Apnea documented by a formal apnea test.
  4. 4.
    Describe how the apnea test is performed and what ancillary tests are used when the clinical exam cannot be completed.
    Apnea test: the patient is preoxygenated, then disconnected from the ventilator while supplemental O₂ is delivered via a tracheal cannula. The clinician watches for any spontaneous respiratory effort while PaCO₂ rises (a powerful stimulus to any preserved brainstem respiratory center). The test is positive for brain death when there is no respiratory effort at all despite PaCO₂ ≥60 mmHg or 20+ mmHg above the patient's baseline. Ancillary tests are used when the clinical exam or apnea test cannot be completed (severe facial trauma, severe pulmonary disease, suspected drug effect): EEG showing electrocerebral silence, or cerebral blood flow studies (nuclear medicine, transcranial Doppler, or CT angiography) showing no blood flow above the foramen magnum. Two clinical exams separated by an observation interval are typically required (interval and details vary by state, hospital policy, and patient age).
